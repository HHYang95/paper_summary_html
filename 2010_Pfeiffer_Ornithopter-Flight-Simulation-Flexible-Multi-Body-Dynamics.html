Overview

This paper presents an efficient integrative simulation framework for ornithopter (flapping-wing air vehicle) flight based on flexible multi-body dynamics (MBD), a modified strip theory (MST) aerodynamic model, and a fluid-structure interaction (FSI) approach. The framework couples MSC.ADAMS for multi-body dynamics with ANSYS finite element models via Craig-Bampton component mode synthesis. A magpie-sized ornithopter model is designed and trimmed for longitudinal flight at $f = 12$ Hz, $U = 10.8$ m/s, and $\theta_{\text{MEAN}} = 9.2°$. The key finding is the observation of limit-cycle oscillations (LCO) in pitch attitude, altitude, and flight speed during trimmed flight, explained through the concept of a Zero Moment Point (ZMP) and the stability residual $\varepsilon$. The simulation of 10 flapping cycles with FSI takes approximately 2 minutes, making it practical for preliminary ornithopter design.

Introduction

Motivation

  • Ornithopters represent engineering realizations of biological flapping-wing flight, with applications as next-generation UAVs for surveillance and reconnaissance with natural camouflage
  • Ornithopter preliminary design differs fundamentally from fixed-wing aircraft due to coupled aerodynamic, structural, and kinematic complexities
  • Existing flight simulation studies mostly used rigid-wing insect models (e.g., Drosophila) with quasi-steady aerodynamics, lacking FSI coupling for bird-scale flexible-wing vehicles

Key Challenges

  • Flapping-wing aerodynamics are governed by unsteady, low Reynolds number flows with nonlinear, time-varying, periodic characteristics
  • Thin flexible membrane wings undergo significant structural deformation during flapping, requiring fluid-structure interaction
  • Wing kinematics (flapping, pitching, twisting, lagging) simultaneously affect both aerodynamics and structural dynamics
  • CFD approaches are computationally prohibitive for preliminary design: ~10 hours for only 4 flapping cycles of a hawkmoth model

Approach

  • Target organism is a magpie-like bird with single flapping frequency and a tail-wing for longitudinal stability, rather than an insect
  • Proposes a time-efficient integrative framework coupling flexible MBD, reduced-order aerodynamics, and FSI for trimmed longitudinal flight

Materials and Methods

Flexible Multi-Body Dynamics Framework

  • MSC.ADAMS models the kinematic motion of the ornithopter; ANSYS provides the finite element model of the flexible wing imported as a modal neutral file
  • Craig-Bampton component mode synthesis (dynamic sub-structuring) represents wing flexibility through a modal approach for each sub-structure
  • Main wing is modeled as flexible; fuselage and tail-wing are treated as rigid bodies for simplicity

Aerodynamic Model: Modified Strip Theory

  • Reduced-order MST model computes sectional aerodynamic loads (normal force, tangential force, moment) at the quarter-chord of each strip
  • Wing divided into 27 aerodynamic surfaces (strips) for the quarter-ellipse wing planform
  • Tail-wing uses thin-airfoil theory with finite-wing correction: $C_{L,T} = \frac{2\pi}{\displaystyle 2 + \frac{\pi \cdot AR_T}{AR_T}} (\theta - \delta_T)$ and induced drag $C_{D,T} = C_{D0,T} + \frac{C_{L,T}^2}{\pi e \cdot AR_T}$

Efficient Fluid-Structure Interaction (E-FSI)

  • Iterative coupling between ADAMS (structural deformation) and MST (aerodynamic loads) at each time step
  • Instantaneous wing sectional velocity and flow speed feed into the aerodynamic model; resulting loads feed back into ADAMS
  • Computational cost: ~2 minutes for 10 flapping cycles with $\Delta t = 0.005$ s integration time step

Wing Structural Model (Efficient Flapping Wing, EFW)

  • Quarter-ellipse planform with wingspan 540 mm and aspect ratio 5.58, modeled after magpie wing geometry
  • BEAM4 elements for leading-edge and stiffeners (carbon-fiber rod), SHELL63 for skin (thin membrane); total 1857 finite elements
  • Material properties: leading edge $E = 250$ GPa, $\rho = 1800$ kg/m$^3$; skin $E = 100$ GPa, thickness 0.04 mm

Trim Condition Search

  • Force and moment equilibrium in time-averaged sense: $\langle F_V \rangle = 0$, $\langle F_H \rangle = 0$, $\langle M_{CG} \rangle = 0$
  • Longitudinal equations of motion: $\dot{U} = -QW - g\sin\theta + F_{X_B}/m$ and $\dot{W} = QU + g\cos\theta + F_{Z_B}/m$ with pitch dynamics $\dot{Q} = M_{Y_B}/I_{Y_B}$
  • Aerodynamic map of mean vertical and horizontal constraint forces generated as a function of flight speed, flapping frequency, and mean pitch angle

Validation

  • Validated against Rectangular Flapping-Wing (RFW) wind tunnel data from Lee et al. (Ref. [9]) at $f = 4$ Hz, $U = 6$ m/s
  • RFW model: 540 mm wingspan, AR 5.58; carbon fiber beams (BEAM4, PIPE16, 118 elements) with point masses for skin (MASS21, 100 elements)
  • Vertical constraint force: sinusoidal at flapping frequency, peak at mid-downstroke; horizontal force: twice the flapping frequency

Results

Trim Flight Conditions

  • Trimmed configuration for the 0.118 kg model ornithopter: $\theta_{\text{MEAN}} = 9.2°$, $f = 12$ Hz, $U = 10.8$ m/s, tail-wing angle $\delta_T = 11.85°$
  • Pitch attitude oscillates from approximately 0° to 20° during each flapping cycle, with mean value at 9.2°
  • Vertical body oscillation: ~1 cm peak-to-peak at flapping frequency (short period mode), ~3 cm peak-to-peak for long period mode

Limit-Cycle Oscillation (LCO)

  • Phase portrait of pitch attitude vs. pitch rate forms a closed circular trajectory, characteristic of limit-cycle oscillation
  • LCO is independent of initial condition perturbation within certain ranges, confirming nonlinear dynamic behavior
  • FFT analysis reveals dominant frequency at 12 Hz in main-wing motion, pitch attitude, and altitude spectra

Zero Moment Point Analysis

  • Constrained (tethered) flight test: all DOFs fixed at center of gravity under trim conditions to measure constraint forces and moments
  • ZMP position varies periodically with flapping motion; the stability residual $\varepsilon = (x_{\text{ZMP}} - x_{\text{CG}})/c_{\text{MEAN}}$ is not exactly zero throughout the cycle
  • Non-zero $\varepsilon$ at the end of upstroke and downstroke is identified as the source of the pitch LCO

Validation Results

  • E-FSI simulation matches experimental wind tunnel data with reasonable accuracy for both vertical and horizontal constraint forces
  • Force waveforms correctly captured: vertical force sinusoidal at $f$, horizontal force at $2f$
  • Asymmetric distortions in horizontal force at $\theta_{\text{MEAN}} = 20°$ due to wing deformation also reproduced

Results Interpretation

Physics of Ornithopter Trim Flight

  • Unlike fixed-wing aircraft, ornithopter trim does not yield constant flight states; instead, all state variables (pitch, altitude, speed) exhibit periodic oscillations at the flapping frequency
  • The pitch attitude is inherently unsteady because the aerodynamic center of pressure shifts continuously relative to the center of gravity during each flapping cycle
  • Maximum pitch-up occurs when the wing passes through neutral position during the downstroke, where total aerodynamic force acting ahead of CG is maximized

Stability Residual Interpretation

  • The stability residual $\varepsilon$ provides a quantitative measure of how far the system is from instantaneous moment equilibrium
  • $\varepsilon = 0$ corresponds to static moment equilibrium where ZMP coincides with CG; this condition is never sustained throughout the full flapping cycle
  • The non-zero $\varepsilon$ at stroke transitions drives the pitch LCO, implying that passive trim alone cannot achieve time-invariant pitch attitude for flapping-wing vehicles

Design Implications

  • To suppress pitch LCO, one needs either: (a) a damping component, (b) active tail-wing elevation angle control, or (c) modulated flapping motion
  • The aerodynamic map approach (mean forces vs. $\theta_{\text{MEAN}}$, $f$, $U$) enables rapid identification of feasible trim envelopes during preliminary design
  • The 2-minute computation time per 10 cycles makes parametric design sweeps practical, unlike CFD-based approaches
  • Oscillatory tendencies match both real ornithopter flight data (Grauer and Hubbard, 2009) and independent numerical results (Dietl and Garcia, 2008)
