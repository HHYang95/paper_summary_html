Overview

This study establishes a unified scaling framework relating wing flexibility to the aerodynamic force generation and propulsive efficiency of flapping wings. Through dimensional analysis and high-fidelity aeroelastic computations of three canonical cases (chordwise flexible aerofoils, spanwise flexible wings, and isotropic Zimmerman wings), the authors derive the maximum relative wing-tip deformation parameter $\gamma$ as the key scaling variable. The normalized time-averaged force follows $\langle C_F \rangle / \Pi_1 \sim \gamma^{1.19}$ ($R^2 = 0.98$), and optimal propulsive efficiency occurs at $f/f_1 \approx 0.4$, well below resonance. The scaling extends to insect flyers and provides direct MAV design guidance.

Introduction

Motivation

  • Biological flyers showcase desirable flight characteristics that can inform the design of flapping wing micro air vehicles (MAVs), with wing flexibility likely having significant influence on resulting aerodynamics
  • Three key open questions: dominant non-dimensional parameters for flexible flapping wing performance, underlying mechanisms of force generation and propulsive efficiency, and existence/characterization of performance optima
  • Prior scaling efforts by Thiria and Godoy-Diana (2010) and Ramananarivo et al. (2011) focused on air-based FSI and neglected density ratio effects on force generation of flexible flapping wings

Key Observations from Literature

  • Maximum propulsive force is generally obtained near the resonance frequency, as shown by Masoud and Alexeev (2010), Michelin and Llewellyn Smith (2009), and Spagnolie et al. (2010)
  • Insects flap at frequencies well below natural frequencies: dragonfly frequency ratios range from 0.16 to 0.46, suggesting optimal efficiency occurs below resonance
  • Vanella et al. (2009) reported optimal propulsive efficiency at $f/f_1 = 0.33$ for a hovering flexible aerofoil, distinct from the force-optimal resonance condition

Related Works

Scaling Approaches

  • Shyy et al. (2010) considered the Navier-Stokes equation with out-of-plane motion of an isotropic flat plate; Ishihara et al. (2009) studied flexibility effects on wing pitch changes using linear isotropic elasticity
  • Ishihara et al. (2009b) introduced the Cauchy number (ratio of fluid-dynamic pressure to elastic reaction force) and correlated time-averaged lift to it using dynamically scaled flexible wing models
  • Thiria and Godoy-Diana (2010) introduced the elastoinertial number for air-based self-propelled flappers where elastic deformation is mostly balanced by wing inertia

Computational and Experimental Studies

  • Heathcote and Gursul (2007): water tunnel experiments on chordwise flexible aerofoils at $Re = 9 \times 10^3$, providing benchmark data for thrust coefficient validation
  • Heathcote et al. (2008): spanwise flexible wing experiments at $Re = 3 \times 10^4$ with documented thrust and efficiency measurements
  • Gogulapati and Friedmann (2011) coupled approximate aerodynamic models with nonlinear structural dynamics for composite anisotropic Zimmerman wings in forward flight

Materials and Methods

Computational Framework

  • Fluid solver: Loci-STREAM, a 3D unstructured pressure-based finite volume solver with implicit first/second-order time stepping, second-order upwind convection, and symmetric Gauss-Seidel/PETSc/BoomerAMG linear solvers
  • Structural solvers: Euler-Bernoulli beam model for 1D chordwise flexibility; co-rotational finite element with triangular shell elements for 3D wings with geometric nonlinearities (small strains, large rotations)
  • FSI interface: time-domain partitioned coupling with inner iterations until displacement convergence, accelerated by Aitken relaxation method

Three Canonical Cases

  • Case 1: Chordwise flexible aerofoil plunging in water at $Re = 9 \times 10^3$, rigid teardrop leading edge with elastic flat plate tail, five thickness ratios ($h_s^* = 0.56 \times 10^{-3}$ to $4.23 \times 10^{-3}$), density ratio $\rho^* = 7.8$
  • Case 2: Spanwise flexible rectangular wing (NACA0012) plunging in water at $Re = 3 \times 10^4$, $AR = 3$, two materials ($\Pi_1 = 121, \rho^* = 7.8$ and $\Pi_1 = 38, \rho^* = 2.7$)
  • Case 3: Isotropic Zimmerman wing hovering in air at $Re = 1.5 \times 10^3$, $k = 0.56$, 16 design points with Young's modulus and mass variations spanning $\rho^* = 10^1$ to $10^4$

Results

Force Generation

  • Chordwise flexible aerofoils: thrust increases with motion frequency (Strouhal number, reduced frequency) for stiffer configurations; the thinnest aerofoil ($h_s^* = 0.56 \times 10^{-3}$) generates maximum thrust at low St but deteriorates at higher frequencies due to excessive phase lag
  • Spanwise flexible wings: flexible wing ($\Pi_1 = 121$) generates higher thrust than very flexible wing ($\Pi_1 = 38$) at all Strouhal numbers; both exceed rigid wing performance at most conditions
  • Isotropic Zimmerman wing: lift generation depends on the interplay of effective stiffness and density ratio; cases with moderate flexibility show highest lift enhancement over rigid baseline

Unified Scaling

  • All three canonical cases collapse onto a single power-law curve: $\langle C_F \rangle / \Pi_1 = 10^{0.98} \gamma^{1.19}$ with $R^2 = 0.98$, despite spanning different Reynolds numbers, density ratios, and flexibility directions
  • Propulsive efficiency initially increases with St then plateaus; thinner/more flexible wings achieve higher peak efficiency; power input scales as $\langle C_P \rangle \sim \gamma^{2.13}$
  • Insect flyer data (hawkmoth, bumble bee, fruit fly) using body weight as lift approximation follow the same scaling trend, with structural parameters from Combes and Daniel (2003)

Results Interpretation

Force Regime Analysis

  • At $Re = O(10^3$-$10^4)$ and $k = O(1)$, the acceleration-reaction (added mass) force dominates: $C_{F,a} \sim St \cdot k \sim \omega^2$ for forward flight, explaining why vorticity fields look similar across Strouhal numbers while pressure and thrust differ substantially
  • The hydrodynamic impulse (vortex-related forces) becomes important only at lower Reynolds numbers or reduced frequencies, scaling as $St/(Re \cdot k)$ for the viscous term and $St^2/k$ for the vortex force term
  • The density ratio determines which mechanism drives wing deformation: added mass for $\rho^* h_s^* \ll 1$ (water) vs. wing inertia for $\rho^* h_s^* \gg 1$ (air), requiring different force normalizations

Efficiency Optimization

  • The optimal frequency ratio $f/f_1 = 0.4$ arises from the competition between thrust growth ($\sim \gamma^{1.17}$) and power input growth ($\sim \gamma^{2.13}$): below this ratio, increasing frequency improves both thrust and efficiency; above it, power input dominates
  • Phase lag between wing tip and root is monotonically related to relative tip deformation; when phase lag exceeds 90 degrees, the trailing edge moves out of phase with the leading edge, degrading thrust
  • The elastoinertial number proposed by Thiria and Godoy-Diana (2010) is shown to be a special case of $\gamma$ for $\rho^* h_s^* \gg 1$ and $f_1/f \gg 1$, demonstrating that the current framework subsumes prior scaling parameters

Conclusion

Principal Findings

  • A unified scaling parameter $\gamma$ (maximum relative wing-tip deformation) successfully collapses force data across three canonical cases and insect flyers onto a single power-law relationship with the normalized force coefficient
  • Maximum propulsive force peaks near resonance ($f/f_1 \approx 1$) while optimal propulsive efficiency occurs at $f/f_1 \approx 0.4$, reconciling the gap between resonance-based optimization and sub-resonance insect flapping frequencies
  • The scaling framework uses only a priori known parameters (wing geometry, structural properties, motion amplitude/frequency) to estimate aerodynamic forces, providing practical design guidance without requiring expensive simulations

Broader Impact

  • Smaller flyers must flap faster for efficiency but gain relative payload capacity because weight reduces faster ($\sim c_m^3$) than aerodynamic force ($\sim c_m^2$) with decreasing size
  • The framework accommodates both water and air environments through appropriate force normalization, enabling translation of water tunnel experiments to air-based MAV designs
  • Future extensions to anisotropic structures, active pitching, and damping effects will further broaden applicability to realistic bio-inspired wing designs
