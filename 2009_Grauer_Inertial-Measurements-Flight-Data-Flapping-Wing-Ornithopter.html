Overview

This paper presents an experimental investigation of flapping-wing ornithopter flight dynamics through onboard inertial measurements. A custom avionics package was developed for a modified Slow Hawk II ornithopter (446 g, 1.196 m wingspan) to collect magnetometer, gyroscope, and accelerometer data during straight and level flight. Time-domain and frequency-domain analyses reveal that the wing beat frequency and its harmonics dominate the inertial measurements, with pitch rates reaching 285 deg/s and heave accelerations up to 4.25 g. Ensemble averaging over 28 wing strokes produces representative waveforms with associated statistics, demonstrating that standard small-angle and low-acceleration assumptions used in conventional attitude estimation are invalid for flapping-wing vehicles.

Introduction

Motivation and Context

  • Ornithopters generate lift and thrust through wing flapping, offering advantages over fixed-wing and rotary-wing designs at small scales and low Reynolds numbers
  • Advances in MEMS and VLSI technologies have enabled practical realization of small ornithoptic aircraft with onboard sensing capabilities
  • Fixed-wing aircraft suffer from decreased aerodynamic efficiency at small scales where viscous effects dominate; rotary-wing vehicles have high power requirements and are limited to indoor flight
  • Ornithopters can achieve contextual camouflage with an avian appearance, making them suited for surveillance, atmospheric data collection, and wildlife monitoring missions

Research Gap

  • Prior ornithopter research focused primarily on numerical simulations based on first-principle models; experimental flight data with onboard inertial measurements were lacking
  • The inertial measurement unit (IMU) is critical for attitude estimation, but the applicability of standard estimation algorithms to flapping-wing vehicles had not been experimentally assessed

Objective and Approach

  • Develop a custom avionics package for a flapping-wing ornithopter to collect in-flight inertial measurements
  • Analyze flight data in time and frequency domains to characterize the dynamic environment experienced by the IMU
  • Apply ensemble averaging to construct representative waveforms over a wing stroke cycle and establish implications for attitude estimation

Materials and Methods

Test Vehicle

  • Modified Slow Hawk II ornithopter: 446 g mass, 0.781 m length, 1.196 m wingspan, 0.329 m$^2$ wing area, aspect ratio 4.36
  • Wing sail constructed from rip-stop polyester segments with carbon fiber spars; fuselage cut from 3.2 mm carbon fiber sheet with reinforced nose
  • Control via 72 MHz transmitter: throttle controls flapping frequency, longitudinal and lateral joysticks drive tail servomotors for pitch and roll/yaw control

Coordinate Frames and Mass Distribution

  • Standard aircraft notation with inertial frame $\mathcal{K}_I$ (north-east-down) and body frame $\mathcal{K}_B$ fixed to the center of mass
  • Wing angle $\theta_1 \in [-25^\circ, +25^\circ]$ defined as roll angle of the left wing; longitudinal tail deflection $\theta_2 \in [-50^\circ, -18^\circ]$; lateral tail deflection $\theta_3 \in [-40^\circ, +40^\circ]$
  • Center of mass migrates along the $e_{z_B}$ axis by 0.10 m (12.8% of overall length) during flapping; a multibody model tracks the configuration-dependent mass distribution

Accelerometer Correction

  • Accelerometer measurements corrected to center of mass using: $$a_B = R_{BS} a_S + \frac{1}{g}\left[\dot{\omega}_B \times + \omega_B \times \omega_B \times - S[\dot{\omega}_B]\right] r_{SB}$$
  • Rotational acceleration estimates computed by smooth differentiation of gyroscope measurements

Avionics Package

  • Custom avionics: 0.044 m x 0.070 m footprint, 27.9 g mass; PIC18F8722 microprocessor at 40 MHz
  • Data logged to removable memory card (>1 GB capacity) at 146 Hz sampling rate (limited by write speed)
  • First-order low-pass anti-aliasing filters with 50 Hz corner frequency; 16-bit analog-to-digital converter
  • MAG3 IMU from MemSense providing orthogonal triads of MEMS magnetometers, gyroscopes, and linear accelerometers

Ensemble Averaging

  • For periodic signal $x(t)$ with period $T$ and measurement $z(t) = x(t) + v(t)$, the ensemble average is: $$\bar{z}(t) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} z(t + nT)$$
  • Measurements interpolated to 100 evenly spaced locations per wing stroke; 28 complete wing strokes analyzed with 2-sigma confidence intervals

Results

Trimmed Flight Conditions

  • Throttle setting: 0.81 (normalized), indicating small margins of excess lift and thrust
  • Longitudinal tail deflection: -37 deg (balances weight for level flight); lateral tail deflection: -1 deg (corrects slight mass imbalance)
  • A 6.4 s segment containing 28 complete wing strokes selected for analysis (approximately 4.375 Hz flapping frequency)

Time-Domain Measurements

  • Wing angle: approximately sinusoidal with 25 deg amplitude, driven by simple crankshaft mechanism
  • Roll, pitch, and yaw rate maximum amplitudes: 265, 322, and 124 deg/s respectively
  • Pitch angle perturbation: approximately 9 deg oscillation per wing stroke (from integrated pitch rate)
  • Longitudinal, lateral, and heave accelerations: maximum amplitudes of 1.9, 1.2, and 4.7 g
  • Estimated heave velocity oscillation: 0.9 m/s amplitude; altitude oscillation: 0.030 m amplitude per wing stroke

Frequency-Domain Analysis

  • Wing beat frequency and its harmonics are the dominant frequencies across all inertial measurements
  • Pitch rate dominated by a single frequency component; roll and yaw rates contain significant higher-harmonic content
  • Heave acceleration dominated by one frequency; longitudinal acceleration exhibits rich multi-harmonic spectrum

Ensemble-Averaged Waveforms

  • Wing angle: zero at 0.00T and 0.50T; maximum at 0.25T (upstroke-to-downstroke transition); minimum at 0.75T
  • Pitch rate: maximum +285 deg/s at 0.40T; minimum -250 deg/s at 0.90T
  • Heave acceleration: maximum +4.25 g at 0.25T; minimum -2.22 g at 0.68T
  • Longitudinal magnetic projection, pitch rate, and heave acceleration lag the wing angle by 0.30T (strong cross-correlation)
  • Variance peaks occur predominantly after 0.75T (downstroke-to-upstroke transition), suggesting unsteady aerodynamic forcing at this phase

Results Interpretation

  • The sinusoidal, evenly divided wing stroke is characteristic of man-made flapping devices with crankshaft mechanisms, contrasting sharply with biological flyers where the downstroke is longer and actively modulated
  • Pitch rates of 285 deg/s and heave accelerations of 4.25 g represent extreme dynamic conditions even for straight and level flight, far exceeding regimes where conventional attitude estimation algorithms operate
  • Small-angle perturbation assumptions (e.g., heading from two-axis magnetometer) are completely invalid due to the 9 deg pitch oscillation per wing stroke
  • Accelerometers measure loads several times gravity, invalidating low-acceleration assumptions typically used to extract roll and pitch angles
  • Without valid orientation estimates, numerical integration drift in angular velocities cannot be corrected through complementary filtering; model-based state observers combining multiple measurement sources are necessary
  • Many high-frequency harmonics in gyroscope and accelerometer data are attributed to structural vibrations in the fuselage, degrading measurement quality and attitude estimation performance
  • Variance analysis reveals asymmetric aerodynamic forcing between upstroke-to-downstroke and downstroke-to-upstroke transitions, with larger disturbances occurring at the latter
  • Empirical bird scaling laws predict a flapping frequency of 2.67 Hz for this vehicle, roughly half the measured rate, indicating significant design optimization potential

Conclusion

  • Custom avionics package successfully collected wing angle, magnetic projection, rotational velocity, and linear acceleration data from a flapping-wing ornithopter in flight
  • Time-domain analysis characterized amplitudes and trends; frequency-domain analysis illuminated the harmonic structure of inertial measurements dominated by the wing beat frequency
  • Ensemble averaging over 28 wing strokes produced representative waveforms with statistical confidence intervals, enabling detailed phase-resolved characterization of the flight dynamics
  • Standard attitude estimation techniques (small-angle, low-acceleration assumptions, complementary filtering) are inapplicable to flapping-wing vehicles due to extreme dynamic loads
  • Model-based state observers combining multiple sensor measurements are recommended for ornithopter attitude estimation
  • Strengthening the vehicle structure is recommended to reduce fuselage vibrations that degrade measurement quality; if flight performance suffers, additional sensors can model structural flexibility
  • Future vehicle designs may include mechanisms to augment the wing stroke waveform for active control of aerodynamic forces and moments
