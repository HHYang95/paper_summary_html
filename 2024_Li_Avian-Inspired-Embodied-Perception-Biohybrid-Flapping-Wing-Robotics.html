Overview

This paper proposes an avian-inspired embodied perception approach for biohybrid flapping-wing robots by integrating natural goose feathers with PVDF piezoelectric films to create a lightweight mechanoreceptor. The feather-PVDF sensor leverages coupled oscillator interactions and feather collision dynamics to enable tactile perception of airflow velocity and pitch angle alongside proprioceptive perception of flapping frequency and wing morphology. Using 1D CNNs optimized by the Grey Wolf Optimizer, the system achieves regression identification with average errors of $0.043\,\text{Hz}$ (frequency), $0.064\,\text{m/s}$ (wind speed), and $0.910^\circ$ (pitch angle). Validated on a feathered flapping-wing aircraft ($28.465\,\text{g}$ total, PVDF at $0.79\%$ mass) through untethered flight with motion capture verification, achieving flight errors of $0.149\,\text{Hz}$, $0.137\,\text{m/s}$, and $2.360^\circ$.

Introduction

Motivation

  • Avian feather architecture provides multi-degree-of-freedom wing deformation enabling complex aerial behaviors; discrete feather-based designs can extend gliding distance by 60% and increase gliding time by 100%.
  • Achieving integrated somatic sensation within stringent weight constraints of flapping-wing robots remains a major challenge, motivating bio-inspired sensory system design.
  • Flexible sensing for FWRs has lagged behind fixed-wing UAVs due to more complex power principles and stricter weight limitations.

State of the Art

  • Avian mechanoreceptors (Herbst corpuscles) perceive vibration frequency and displacement amplitude through feathers, enabling flow velocity and stall/turbulence assessment.
  • PVDF piezoelectric films are lightweight, sensitive, self-powered, and biocompatible, showing promise for embodied sensing in aerial robots.
  • Adjacent vibratory feathers operate as coupled oscillators, producing nonlinear collision interactions that generate rich signal features across multiple frequency bands.

Gap & Contribution

  • Prior work has not achieved multisensory fusion (tactile + proprioceptive) in FWRs using a single structural sensing element under actual flight conditions.
  • This paper demonstrates that a feather-PVDF biohybrid mechanoreceptor can simultaneously perceive four flight parameters through distinct voltage signal patterns from coupled oscillator interactions.
  • The approach achieves embodied perception validated by motion capture during untethered flight, at only 0.79% weight overhead.

Materials and Methods

Biohybrid Mechanoreceptor Design

  • Goose remiges serve as the structural substrate, with PVDF piezoelectric films ($125\,\mu\text{m}$ total thickness: polymer + piezoelectric + silver electrode layers) affixed along the rachis direction.
  • Cross-sectional microscopy confirms close adhesion between PVDF and feathers in both transverse and longitudinal directions without obvious interface gaps.
  • Fibrous barb microstructures create gecko-like adhesion enhancement, with barbicels interlocking at the heterogeneous interface.

Interface Characterization

  • 180-degree peeling tests: average forces of $1.382\,\text{N}$ (PVDF-PET), $1.162\,\text{N}$ (PVDF-carbon fiber), $1.122\,\text{N}$ (PVDF-feather), confirming comparable adhesion strength.
  • Fatigue testing over $10{,}000$ vibration cycles shows no significant degradation in output voltage performance.
  • PVDF attachment enhances bending load-bearing capacity of feather structures in both positive and negative directions.

Perception Architecture

  • Three separate 1D CNN models for regression identification of flapping frequency, wind speed, and pitch angle; GWO optimizes hyperparameters for each network.
  • Optimal input sample window of $1\,\text{s}$ for signal feature extraction, balancing recognition accuracy with training complexity.
  • A quasi-steady flapping wing model accounting for translational forces (LEV), rotational forces, coupling effects, and added mass provides qualitative signal interpretation.

Flight Test Platform

  • Feathered FWR: gear-driven mechanism, flight control board, piezoelectric acquisition, and wireless transmission modules; total mass $28.465\,\text{g}$.
  • PVDF film: 0.79% of total mass; data acquisition and wireless module: 5.15% of total weight.
  • 8-camera motion capture system reconstructs 3D trajectory for ground-truth validation of flight speed and pitch angle per frame.

Results

Mechanoreceptor Performance

  • $V_{\text{rms}}$ increases monotonically with flapping frequency; multi-feather configurations produce higher outputs due to coupled oscillator interactions.
  • Multi-feather structures generate up to approximately $3\,\text{mV}$ (rms) at $3\,\text{m/s}$ wind speed.
  • Harmonic components and noise distribution vary systematically with wind speed, pitch angle, and flapping frequency, providing recognizable signal features.

Signal Recognition Accuracy

  • Flapping frequency: average absolute error $0.043\,\text{Hz}$ across the test set.
  • Wind speed: average absolute error $0.064\,\text{m/s}$, exploiting nonlinear vibration signal differences at different speeds.
  • Pitch angle: average absolute error $0.910^\circ$, utilizing harmonic amplitude and noise distribution variations.

Transfer Learning & Wing Morphology

  • Deep transfer learning improves average errors from 0.31/0.46 to 0.21/0.19 for varying frequency signals with less training data.
  • Wing morphology classification: 100% accuracy for three configurations (elliptical, high-lift, soaring) via CNN on PCA-reduced collision features ($\mathbb{R}^{26} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{3}$).

Flight Validation

  • Indoor flight (n=73): errors of $0.149\,\text{Hz}$ (frequency), $0.137\,\text{m/s}$ (velocity), $2.360^\circ$ (pitch) vs. motion capture.
  • Outdoor flight (n=217): indoor-trained CNN model applied directly, demonstrating generalization capability.
  • Real-time perception: 100% classification accuracy for four flight parameters with three categories each.

Results Interpretation

Multisensory Fusion Analysis

  • The homologous fusion of tactile and proprioceptive perception through a single PVDF-feather element reduces payload compared to multi-sensor configurations, achieving multifunctional perception at 0.79% weight overhead.
  • Coupled oscillator interactions between adjacent feathers generate nonlinear signal enrichment producing distinguishable voltage patterns, forming the physical basis for multi-parameter recognition from a single sensor.
  • Feather collision tactile feedback effectively serves as a proxy for proprioceptive awareness of discrete wing arrangements, validated by 100% morphology classification accuracy.

Flight Performance Analysis

  • Elevated pitch angle error during flight ($2.360^\circ$ vs. $0.910^\circ$ on bench) stems from concurrent yaw, roll, and flapping motions complicating small airflow directional changes.
  • Deep transfer learning demonstrates practical value for adapting perception models across configurations without extensive re-collection, critical for scalable deployment.
  • Energy harvesting from flapping motion provides a pathway toward self-powered environmental monitoring, though current efficiency requires optimization through structural and frequency improvements.

Conclusion
  • A feather-PVDF biohybrid mechanoreceptor was developed with excellent peeling strength ($1.122\,\text{N}$), fatigue durability ($10{,}000$ cycles), and consistent electromechanical performance.
  • Multisensory fusion achieved through a single sensing element: tactile perception of airflow velocity and pitch angle, proprioceptive perception of flapping frequency and wing morphology.
  • 1D CNN + GWO regression achieves errors of $0.043\,\text{Hz}$, $0.064\,\text{m/s}$, $0.910^\circ$ (bench); untethered flight validation yields $0.149\,\text{Hz}$, $0.137\,\text{m/s}$, $2.360^\circ$.
  • Wing morphology classification achieves 100% accuracy; deep transfer learning enables rapid model adaptation with reduced data requirements.
  • This work establishes a framework for biomechanically integrated embodied perception in flapping-wing robots with implications for reflex-based control and autonomous aerial robotics.
