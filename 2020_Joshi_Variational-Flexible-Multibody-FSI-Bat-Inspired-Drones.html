Overview

This paper develops a 3D partitioned aeroelastic framework for flexible multibody systems in incompressible turbulent flow. The structural domain uses a co-rotational formulation (Wiener-Milenković CRV parameterization) for small strain problems with multiple component types—rigid bodies, Euler-Bernoulli beams, and thin shells—connected by joints with Lagrange multiplier constraints. The fluid domain solves the incompressible Navier-Stokes equations with Spalart-Allmaras DDES turbulence via stabilized Petrov-Galerkin methods. Interface coupling across non-matching meshes employs radial basis function (RBF) interpolation with compact support, achieving approximately third-order convergence. A nonlinear iterative force correction (NIFC) scheme stabilizes the partitioned coupling at low mass ratios (m^* = 0.03). Validated on a pitching plate at Re = 10{,}000 and demonstrated on a bat wing model (Glossophaga soricina) at Re = 12{,}000, the framework shows 8% higher mean lift and 120% higher maximum unsteady lift for flexible flapping wings versus rigid non-flapping wings.

Introduction

Biologically Inspired MAVs

  • MAVs and drones at low Reynolds numbers (Re = 10^4–10^5) suffer degraded performance with rigid fixed wings; flapping of flexible wings offers superior lift-to-drag ratio, delayed stall, and enhanced stability
  • Large amplitude flapping induces strong inertial forces and unsteady effects including leading edge vortex (LEV) formation that generates significantly higher lift than steady-state aerodynamics
  • Among natural flyers, bats possess the most complex wing kinematics with over 40 degrees of freedom in wing articulation (bones, joints, membranes)

Need for Multibody FSI Framework

  • Existing approaches often treat wings as single flexible bodies without modeling multiple interconnected structural components and joint constraints
  • The ALE framework provides accurate interface tracking but faces challenges with non-matching meshes at the fluid–structure boundary
  • A partitioned scheme is preferred for modularity: independent fluid and structural solvers coupled through interface equilibrium conditions

Materials and Methods

Co-Rotational Structural Framework

  • Displacement decomposed as \boldsymbol{\eta}^s = \boldsymbol{\eta}^s_R + \boldsymbol{\eta}^s_E (rigid rotation + small elastic deformation); deformation gradient \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{R}\hat{\mathbf{F}}
  • Strain simplified for small elastic deformation: \mathbf{E} \approx \frac{1}{2}[\mathbf{R}^T\nabla\boldsymbol{\eta}^s_E + (\nabla\boldsymbol{\eta}^s_E)^T\mathbf{R}]
  • Rotation parameterized by Wiener-Milenković conformal rotation vector: \mathbf{R} = \frac{1}{(4-c_0)^2}[(c_0^2+8c_0-16)\mathbf{I}+2\mathbf{c}\mathbf{c}^T+2c_0\tilde{\mathbf{c}}]
  • Joint constraints enforced via Lagrange multipliers: displacement constraint (\mathbf{e}_m^A)^T\boldsymbol{\eta}^{B/A}-\eta_m=0 and rotation constraint s_{kk}\sin\varphi_m+s_{kl}\cos\varphi_m=0

Fluid Solver

  • Incompressible Navier-Stokes in ALE form with Spalart-Allmaras DDES turbulence model
  • Stabilized Petrov-Galerkin spatial discretization (SUPG + PSPG); generalized-\alpha time integration

RBF Interface Mapping

  • Wendland C^2 compactly supported RBF: \phi(r) = (1-r)^4_+(4r+1)
  • Globally conservative; avoids common-refinement surface construction for complex multibody geometries
  • Approximately third-order convergence demonstrated for traction transfer across non-matching interfaces

Partitioned Coupling

  • Staggered predictor-corrector with nonlinear iterative force correction (NIFC) and Aitken \Delta^2 acceleration for stability at low mass ratios

Results

Pitching Plate Validation (Re = 10,000)

  • 3D flexible plate with trailing-edge serration; Re = 10{,}000, m^* = 0.03, Ae = 1.575 \times 10^8
  • Interface convergence: mesh IC3 (289 nodes) achieves <0.1% error vs finest mesh
  • Mesh convergence: M3 (289 structural / 777,508 fluid nodes) achieves <1% error
  • Thrust coefficient C_T and propulsive efficiency \eta across St \in [0.17, 0.68] show close agreement with experimental data

Bat Wing Demonstration (Re = 12,000)

  • Glossophaga soricina geometry: wingspan b = 0.2369 m, root chord c_{\text{root}} = 0.0384 m; 6 revolute joints modeled
  • Bone fingers as Euler-Bernoulli beams (humerus EI = 1.56 \times 10^{-3} Nm2); membrane as thin shell (E = 7 \times 10^5 N/m2, h = 0.046 cm)
  • Fluid mesh: 4.04M grid points; structural: 792 quad + 74 beam elements per wing
  • At AOA = 10°: \bar{C}_L = 0.6059, \bar{C}_D = 0.1522, \bar{C}_L/\bar{C}_D = 3.98
  • 8% higher mean C_L and 120% higher C_{L,\max} (1.2838 vs 0.5822) compared to rigid non-flapping wing

Wake Dynamics

  • Horseshoe-like vortices in near and far wake generated by trailing-edge serration
  • Complex tip vortex and LEV interactions at both 10° and 15° angles of attack
  • Spanwise vorticity variation directly linked to anisotropic bone/membrane structural properties

Results Interpretation

  • The 8% mean lift improvement and 3.5% lift-to-drag ratio gain demonstrate measurable aerodynamic benefits of flexible multibody flapping over rigid wings at Re = 12{,}000
  • The 120% increase in maximum unsteady C_L confirms that unsteady aerodynamic mechanisms (LEV formation, pressure differential during downstroke) are the primary lift source for natural flyers
  • Anisotropic flexural rigidity along bone fingers (varying by over an order of magnitude) creates passive deformation gradients that shape vortex structures differently at each spanwise station
  • The RBF interface mapping with third-order convergence proves robust for complex multibody geometries where common-refinement construction becomes impractical
  • The NIFC partitioned coupling successfully handles the challenging low mass ratio (m^* = 0.03) regime typical of biological flight systems
  • The framework is generic: applicable to marine/offshore, rotor dynamics, dual aerial-aquatic vehicles, and other flexible multibody FSI applications beyond bio-inspired flight
