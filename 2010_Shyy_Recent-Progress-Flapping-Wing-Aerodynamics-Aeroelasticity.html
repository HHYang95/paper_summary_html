Overview

This comprehensive review consolidates recent progress in flapping wing aerodynamics and aeroelasticity for micro air vehicles (MAVs). The authors systematically examine unsteady lift enhancement mechanisms (LEV delayed stall, clap-and-fling, wake capture, rapid pitch rotation), Reynolds number effects on vortex structures from $O(10)$ to $O(10^4)$, and the role of wing flexibility in aerodynamic performance. Using surrogate modeling trained on CFD data, they map the multi-dimensional design space of kinematic parameters and reveal that tip vortices can enhance rather than degrade lift in low-Re flapping flight. The aeroelasticity sections demonstrate non-monotonic relationships between wing stiffness and thrust/lift generation, establishing that moderate flexibility optimally balances force production and efficiency.

Introduction

MAV Motivation and Challenges

  • MAVs have potential to revolutionize sensing and information gathering for environmental monitoring and homeland security
  • Below a few centimeters, fixed wing designs encounter fundamental challenges in lift generation and flight control due to Reynolds number effects
  • Flapping wings exploit large-scale vortical flow structures to enhance both lift and thrust, but scaling invariance of fluid and structural dynamics is fundamentally difficult

Scope of the Review

  • Bridges rigid flapping wing aerodynamics with flexible wing aeroelasticity across multiple Reynolds number regimes
  • Covers unsteady lift mechanisms, surrogate-based design space exploration, and coupled fluid-structure interaction
  • Identifies open issues including the coupling between aerodynamics, structural dynamics, and flight control

Related Works

Unsteady Lift Enhancement Mechanisms

  • Clap-and-fling mechanism discovered by Weis-Fogh in chalcid wasps; validated by Lehmann et al. with PIV and dynamically scaled robotic wings
  • Rapid pitch rotation at stroke ends: advanced rotation enhances lift (Magnus-like effect), delayed rotation reduces it
  • Wake capture generates additional force peaks when wings reverse direction and interact with previously shed wake
  • Delayed stall of LEV identified by Ellington et al. as a dominant lift enhancement in insect flight; creates low-pressure region on upper surface

Experimental and Computational Advances

  • PIV and smoke visualization provide 2-D and 3-D flow structures around biological flyers and scaled models
  • High-speed cameras and laser techniques enable measurement of wing kinematics including camber, twisting, and deformation
  • Community-wide benchmark efforts organized for SD7003 airfoil and flat plate comparing experiments and computations
  • Computational methods span Navier-Stokes solvers, immersed boundary methods, RANS with turbulence models, and inviscid panel methods

Materials and Methods

Governing Equations and Scaling

  • Fluid governed by unsteady incompressible 3-D Navier-Stokes equations: $$\frac{\partial \mathbf{U}}{\partial t} + \mathbf{U} \cdot \nabla \mathbf{U} = -\frac{1}{\rho_f}\nabla p + \frac{\mu}{\rho_f}\nabla^2 \mathbf{U}$$
  • Six dimensionless parameters: Reynolds number $Re$, Strouhal number $St$, reduced frequency $k = \pi f c_m / U_{ref}$, density ratio $\rho^*$, effective stiffness $\Pi_1$, effective rotational inertia $\Pi_2$
  • For hovering: $Re = \rho_f \cdot AR \cdot \Phi \cdot f \cdot c_m^2 / \mu$; proportional to flap amplitude, frequency, square of mean chord, and aspect ratio
  • Effective stiffness: $\Pi_1 = Eh_s^3 / [12(1-\nu^2)\rho_f U_{ref}^2 c_m^3]$ describes ratio of elastic bending to aerodynamic forces

Surrogate Modeling and FSI Framework

  • Surrogate modeling trained on CFD data to efficiently evaluate off-design points across the kinematic design space
  • Three design variables: normalized stroke amplitude $2h_a/c_m r_g$, pitch amplitude $\alpha_a$, phase lag $\phi$; Re = 65 (fruit fly regime)
  • Aeroelastic framework couples 3-D NS solver with nonlinear beam/plate solver for fluid-structure interaction
  • Experimental validation via water tunnel studies with dynamically scaled wings of varying flexibility (rigid, flexible, very flexible)

Results

Rigid Wing: 2-D vs 3-D Aerodynamics

  • Tip vortices at Re = 65 augment lift by creating low-pressure regions near tip and anchoring LEVs that would otherwise shed
  • 3-D low aspect ratio wings can produce higher lift than 2-D airfoils, contradicting classical stationary wing theory
  • Variable sensitivity hierarchy changes: 2-D ($\alpha_a > \phi > 2h_a/c_m r_g$) vs 3-D ($\phi > \alpha_a > 2h_a/c_m r_g$)
  • Delayed rotation with high AoA shows most prominent 3-D effects with TiV-LEV interaction enhancing performance

LEV and Reynolds Number Effects

  • LEV exhibits spiral structure with breakdown at Re = $O(10^3$-$10^4)$ (hawkmoth, honeybee); ordered structure connecting with TiV at Re = $O(10^2)$ (fruit fly)
  • LEV, TEV, and TiV form doughnut-shaped vortex rings with intense downward jet-flow at Re = 6300 (hawkmoth hovering)
  • LEV breakdown at approximately 70-80% of wing span length; vortex ring structures observed in hummingbird and bat studies

Aeroelasticity Findings

  • Chordwise flexibility: mean thrust increases with flexibility up to a critical point then decreases; non-monotonic $\Pi_1$-thrust response
  • Flexible wings achieve up to 30% lift increase at 1/3 of natural frequency via nonlinear resonance
  • Spanwise flexibility: moderate stiffness (25.7° phase lag) produces highest thrust; excessive flexibility (126.3° phase lag) degrades performance
  • Bio-inspired flexible wings: more thrust than rigid counterparts, 19% less peak torque, 34% less peak power

Results Interpretation

Flexibility-Performance Coupling

  • Chordwise flexibility primarily redistributes lift vs thrust by changing wing projection angle through camber deformation
  • Spanwise flexibility primarily increases total aerodynamic forces by creating higher effective angles of attack along the span
  • Phase lag between prescribed motion and wing deformation emerges as a unifying parameter explaining non-monotonic performance trends
  • More flexible wings are thrust-effective at lower frequencies; stiffer wings at higher frequencies, suggesting optimal actuation frequency exists

Design Space Implications

  • Surrogate models efficiently map regions where 2-D can approximate 3-D and where substantial 3-D effects preclude such comparison
  • Combined effect of TiVs, LEV, and jet can improve aerodynamics; TiV role varies from case to case depending on competing effects
  • Both spanwise and chordwise flexibility must be considered together to optimize performance under varying flight conditions
  • Environmental uncertainty (wind gusts, target movement) and time-scale disparity between physics domains create highly complex interactions

Conclusion

Key Findings

  • LEV is a common, critical feature at Re = $O(10^4)$ or lower; structure and spanwise flow change significantly with Reynolds number
  • Tip vortices in low-Re flapping flight contribute positively to lift through low-pressure regions and LEV anchoring
  • Wing flexibility introduces non-monotonic stiffness-performance relationships for both chordwise and spanwise deformation modes
  • Surrogate modeling enables efficient exploration of multi-dimensional design space connecting kinematics, geometry, and flexibility

Open Challenges

  • LEV as lift enhancement at Re = $O(10^5$-$10^6)$ remains uncertain; dynamic-stall vortices break away at higher Reynolds numbers
  • Closed-loop active control integrating aerodynamics, structural dynamics, and flight control is essential but largely unexplored
  • Vision-based sensing for real-time aeroelastic state estimation needs development
  • Significant progress required before MAVs achieve operational capability; multi-physics coupling remains a fundamental challenge
