Overview

This paper presents the numerical implementation and experimental validation of a nonlinear, unsteady, quasi-three-dimensional aerodynamic model for insect-like flapping wings in hover. The coupled wake integral equations derived in Part 1 are solved using discrete vortex methods with a time-marching algorithm implemented in Fortran. The model captures dual separation from both leading and trailing edges, producing free vortex wakes that interact with the wing during each stroke. Validation against Dickinson and Gotz flow visualization at $Re = 192$ and Robofly force measurements at $Re = 160$ demonstrates excellent agreement in both flow topology and force prediction. Mean lift discrepancy reaches only 6.8% in the last cycle, and spectral analysis confirms the model captures the correct vortex-shedding frequency of approximately 0.34 Hz. The work establishes that 2D sectional flows, aggregated via blade-element theory, adequately represent 3D flapping-wing aerodynamics at low Reynolds numbers of order $Re \approx 200$.

Introduction

Motivation

  • Insect-like flapping flight offers superior maneuverability for micro-air vehicle (MAV) design, but analytical aerodynamic models remain scarce
  • High angles of attack in insect flight produce dual separation from both leading and trailing edges, generating complex wake interactions
  • Part 1 derived exact but non-closed-form coupled nonlinear wake integral equations requiring numerical solution

State of the Art

  • Rosenhead (1931) pioneered 2D vortex sheet simulation using discrete point vortices convected by the Rott-Birkhoff equation: $q(\bar{z}) = -\frac{i}{2\pi} \oint_C \frac{d\Gamma'}{z(\Gamma,t) - z(\Gamma',t)} + U_E - iV_E$
  • Desingularization methods evolved from Lamb-Oseen vortices to Vatistas vortex model: $v_\theta = \frac{\Gamma}{2\pi} \frac{r}{(r_c^{2n} + r^{2n})^{1/n}}$
  • Vortex amalgamation techniques (Spallart criterion) address computational cost of growing vortex populations
  • Existing CFD and experimental studies (Ellington et al., Birch and Dickinson) provided LEV observations but lacked fast analytical prediction capability

Gap and Contribution

  • No prior model combined nonlinear wake interactions with both LEV and trailing-edge wake in an analytical framework amenable to fast computation
  • This Part 2 bridges the gap by implementing the Part 1 equations via vortex methods, establishing numerical parameters, and providing comprehensive experimental validation
  • The quasi-3D blade-element extension enables full-wing performance prediction without expensive 3D CFD

Related Works

Vortex Methods and Desingularization

  • Rosenhead (1931) introduced discrete vortex approximation of continuous vortex sheets for 2D unsteady flows
  • Oseen (1911) and Lamb (1932) developed viscous core models replacing singular point vortices with finite-core distributions
  • Vatistas et al. (1991) proposed a generalized vortex model unifying Rankine, Lamb-Oseen, and Scully profiles via parameter $n$
  • Spallart (1988) and Sarpkaya (1989) established vortex amalgamation criteria to manage computational cost

Insect Flight Experiments

  • Dickinson and Gotz (1993) performed flow visualization at $Re = 192$ in sucrose solution, identifying LEV formation at $\alpha \geq 22.5°$
  • Ellington et al. (1996) observed LEV spanwise transport and vortex breakdown on hawkmoth-scale flappers at $Re \approx 5000$
  • Birch and Dickinson (2001) demonstrated weak spanwise flow at low $Re \approx 200$, supporting 2D sectional analysis
  • Dickinson Robofly experiments provided benchmark force data over four complete flapping cycles at $Re = 160$

Kutta Condition in Unsteady Flows

  • Crighton (1985) examined validity of Kutta-Joukowski condition in unsteady flows, noting it generally holds for moderate kinematics
  • Giesing (1969) and Maskell (1972) showed separation surface direction depends on $d\Gamma/dt$ sign
  • Sun and Boyd (2004) demonstrated significant slip velocity at edges for $Re = 135$ flat plates, supporting K-J relaxation

Materials and Methods

Initial-Value Problem Formulation

  • Flow solved as time-marching initial-value problem: known initial conditions propagated forward with vortex shedding at each step
  • At each time step, a pair of vortices is shed from leading and trailing edges to satisfy Kutta-Joukowski conditions via Kelvin's theorem
  • Free vortices convect at local Kirchhoff velocity governed by the discretized Rott-Birkhoff equation

Discretized Wake Integral Equations

  • Wake-induced vorticity computed via: $\gamma_1(\theta,t)|_{wk+lv} = -\frac{\Gamma_0(t)}{2\pi R} - \frac{1}{2\pi R}\left[\sum_{i=1}^{n_{wk}} \Re\left(\frac{Z_{wk}+Re^{i\theta}}{Z_{wk}-Re^{i\theta}}\right)\gamma_{wk}dZ_{wk} + \sum_{i=1}^{n_{lv}} \Re\left(\frac{Z_{lv}+Re^{i\theta}}{Z_{lv}-Re^{i\theta}}\right)\gamma_{lv}dZ_{lv}\right]$
  • Aerofoil represented by Joukowski-type transformation from circle plane, providing tight spacing near edges
  • Aerofoil discretization $n_{foil} = 50$ elements chosen after convergence study

Numerical Techniques

  • New vortex placement uses one-third rule: $z_t = z_{edge} + \frac{1}{3}(z_{edge} - z_{t-1})$, accounting for both wing motion and prior vortex convection
  • Sub-time-stepping ($n = 1, 2, 4$) during stroke reversals based on ratio of normal kinematic velocity to chord-lengths-per-step
  • Vatistas desingularization with vortex-core radius of 2.5-5.0% chord
  • Time-step size of 0.01 s (mean angle per step of $0.6°$) after convergence study

Numerical Modifications

  • Kutta-Joukowski relaxation: circulation limits imposed during extreme kinematics when shed vortex strengths exceeded 3 orders of magnitude above prior values
  • Smoothing scheme: Gaussian low-pass filter applied to force/moment data to remove numerical spikes from wake re-entry, preserving frequency content
  • Blade-element method extends 2D sections to quasi-3D wing with 20 spanwise sections

Implementation

  • Implemented in Fortran (Intel Fortran Compiler v.8.0) exploiting native complex number arithmetic
  • All computations in double precision; program size ~2.0 MB with ~10 MB runtime memory for 16,000 section-time-steps
  • Computation time scales as $\sim n^{2.5}$ due to variable sub-time-steps; typical run of 6,400 section-time-steps completed in ~10 minutes

Results

Flow Visualization Validation (Dickinson and Gotz)

  • Simulated impulsive start at $\alpha = 45°$ with $Re = 192$; wing traverses 7.5 chord lengths before stopping
  • Good agreement with experimental flow topology: LEV roll-up, growth, lift-off, and breakdown all captured correctly
  • Model reproduces triangular LEV structure after 4 chord lengths of travel
  • Dual stopping vortices (from LE and TE) correctly predicted when wing halts

Force Validation (Robofly)

  • Benchmark simulation: 48,000 section-time-steps (20 sections $\times$ 2,400 time-steps) with up to 4 sub-time-steps
  • Mean lift discrepancy: 13% overall, reducing to 6.8% for last cycle as flow approaches periodicity
  • Mean thrust discrepancy: 7.2% overall, reducing to 3.3% for last cycle
  • Mean drag for last cycle: experimental $D = 0.0055$ N (0.9% of thrust); theoretical $D = 6.29 \times 10^{-4}$ N (0.1% of thrust) -- excellent hover condition
  • Stroke-reversal spikes in both lift and thrust captured at correct temporal positions without phase lag

Spectral Analysis

  • Power spectral density shows dominant blade-passing frequency: experimental 0.3418 Hz vs. theoretical 0.3483 Hz
  • Higher harmonics match well between experiment and simulation, especially for thrust
  • Smoothing scheme confirmed to preserve frequency content of the force data

Flow Characteristics

  • Lift grows rapidly from zero at stroke start, remains roughly constant mid-stroke, then exhibits opposite spike pairs at each reversal
  • Thrust exceeds lift by ~50% due to dominant horizontal wake component in flat figure-of-eight kinematics
  • Outboard sections experience earlier vortex breakdown due to greater chord-lengths traveled, producing von Karman-type alternating shedding

Results Interpretation

  • The temporal synchrony between experimental and theoretical force peaks confirms that the model correctly captures the unsteady flow physics, not merely mean values
  • Close spectral agreement implies that underlying vortex shedding mechanisms are well represented by the inviscid potential framework
  • The quasi-3D blade-element approach is validated: at $Re \approx 200$, 3D flow is essentially an aggregation of 2D sectional flows
  • Phase difference between spanwise sections (outboard sections "older" in chord-lengths) explains spanwise variation in LEV behavior, consistent with Ellington et al. observations
  • Vortex breakdown in outboard sections produces a tip-vortex-like effect, partially compensating for the absence of explicit 3D tip modeling
  • K-J relaxation during stroke reversals is physically justified: at extreme kinematics, flow velocity at edges becomes nearly perpendicular to the surface
  • Wagner's use of semi-chords as independent variable is confirmed as relevant; flow behavior strongly depends on number of chord-lengths traveled rather than absolute time
  • Mean drag approaching zero in the last cycle confirms hover equilibrium is achieved, validating the overall force balance
  • The inviscid model consistently underestimates both lift and thrust by the last cycle, suggesting viscous effects contribute modestly at these Reynolds numbers
  • Tip-vortex downwash effects (Leishman et al.) and LEV spanwise motion at higher $Re$ remain unresolved limitations of the current framework
