Overview

This paper develops a generalized viscous vortex model for rotor free-wake and aeroacoustic analyses. The authors extend the classic Lamb-Oseen laminar vortex diffusion model by incorporating Squire's hypothesis for turbulent eddy viscosity, validated against extensive experimental data spanning vortex Reynolds numbers from $10^3$ to $10^7$. A family of algebraic velocity profiles (swirl, axial, radial) is derived from a simplified form of the incompressible Navier-Stokes equations using the Vatistas swirl velocity profile parameterized by a single integer exponent $n$. The model with $n=2$ closely approximates the Lamb-Oseen/Newman exponential solution and shows strong agreement with both fixed-wing and rotating-wing tip vortex measurements, demonstrating self-similarity when scaled by the vortex core radius.

Introduction

Motivation and Context

  • Trailing tip vortices from lifting surfaces are central to both fixed-wing wake hazard problems and rotary-wing aeroacoustic predictions
  • The viscous core structure and temporal development of tip vortices remain a continuing modeling challenge in aerodynamics despite five decades of research
  • For helicopter rotors, blade tip vortex characteristics have significant impact on airloads, performance, and acoustics due to close proximity of vortices to rotating blades
  • Blade-vortex interactions (BVI) make it essential to properly represent tip vortex core structures; Gandhi & Tauszig (Ref. 14) demonstrated sensitivity of BVI predictions to viscous core models

Existing Models and Limitations

  • Free-vortex wake models are increasingly used in rotating-wing aeroacoustic analyses but rely on semi-empirical viscous core models
  • Full Navier-Stokes solutions are not analytically tractable and numerically prohibitive; closed-form solutions require simplifying assumptions
  • The Rankine vortex has discontinuous velocity/vorticity at the core boundary; the Scully (Kaufmann) model provides desingularization but limited physical basis
  • The Vatistas family of algebraic models parameterized by integer $n$ unifies Scully ($n=1$), Lamb-Oseen approximation ($n=2$), and Rankine ($n \to \infty$)
  • A complete three-component velocity model (swirl + axial + radial) with physically motivated core growth is needed for accurate aeroacoustic and free-wake applications

Paper Objective

  • Develop a generalized algebraic vortex model with all three velocity components derived from simplified Navier-Stokes equations
  • Validate the turbulent core growth model against comprehensive experimental data from both fixed-wing and rotating-wing measurements

Related Works

  • Lamb (1932) and Oseen (1912) derived the classic one-dimensional laminar vortex diffusion solution with core radius growing as $r_c = \sqrt{4\alpha\nu t}$
  • Squire (1965) extended the Lamb-Oseen model to trailing vortices using $t = z/V_\infty$ and introduced eddy viscosity $\delta\nu$ with $\delta = 1 + a_1 Re_v$ to account for turbulence
  • Burgers (1948) extended to three-dimensional vortex flows but assumed constant axial velocity, inconsistent with experimental wake-like profiles
  • Newman (1959) derived exponential solutions for all three velocity components from simplified 3D incompressible Navier-Stokes equations with good experimental correlation
  • Vatistas et al. (1991) proposed the desingularized algebraic swirl velocity family $V_\theta \propto r/(r_c^{2n} + r^{2n})^{1/n}$
  • Iversen (1976) correlated turbulent trailing vortex decay data showing turbulent effects become important at $Re_v > 10^5$
  • Bhagwat & Leishman (2000) established universal peak swirl velocity correlations for both fixed-wing and rotating-wing tip vortices supporting Squire's hypothesis
  • Cotel & Breidenthal (1999) analyzed vortex core growth rates showing $b_1 \propto Re_v^{-1/2}$ trend consistent with laminar-dominated inner core growth
  • Leishman & Bagai (1998) reviewed challenges in understanding vortex dynamics of helicopter rotor wakes
  • Martin, Pugliese & Leishman (2001) performed high-resolution trailing vortex measurements in hovering rotor wakes

Materials and Methods

Viscous Core Growth Model

  • Core radius modeled using extended Lamb-Oseen with Squire's eddy viscosity: $$r_c(\zeta) = \sqrt{r_{c0}^2 + 4\alpha\delta\nu\frac{\zeta}{\Omega}}$$ where $\delta = 1 + a_1 Re_v$ and $\alpha = 1.25643$ is the Oseen parameter
  • Squire's parameter $a_1$ empirically determined from experimental data; $a_1 = O(10^{-4})$ for rotating-wing and $O(10^{-5})$ for fixed-wing vortices
  • Empirical validation performed across vortex Reynolds numbers from $10^3$ to $10^7$ using multiple experimental datasets

Swirl Velocity Profile

  • Pre-assumed Vatistas algebraic profile: $$V_\theta(r) = \frac{\Gamma_v}{2\pi}\frac{r}{(r_c^{2n} + r^{2n})^{1/n}}$$ satisfying the one-dimensional Navier-Stokes swirl equation
  • Parameter $n$ takes integer values; $n=2$ provides best approximation to Lamb-Oseen exponential solution

Navier-Stokes Simplification

  • Full 3D incompressible Navier-Stokes equations written in cylindrical coordinates with axisymmetry assumption
  • Ordering scheme: swirl and axial velocities $O(\varepsilon)$, radial velocity $O(\varepsilon^2)$, pressure $O(\varepsilon^2)$, where $\varepsilon \sim 0.1$–$0.3$
  • The $\theta$-momentum equation is simplified by subtracting the 1D Lamb-Oseen form, yielding a reduced system of three coupled equations

Axial and Radial Velocity Solutions

  • Axial velocity: $V_z = -\frac{A}{z}\left(1 - \frac{r^2}{(r_c^{2n}+r^{2n})^{1/n}}\right)$ — wake-like or jet-like depending on sign of constant $A$
  • Radial velocity: $V_r = -\frac{Ar}{2z^2}\left(1 - \frac{r^2}{(r_c^{2n}+r^{2n})^{1/n}}\right)$
  • Constant $A$ determined from wing zero-lift drag: $A = D_0 / (4\pi\rho\delta\nu)$
  • Static pressure obtained by radial integration of centrifugal balance: $p(r) = \int_0^r V_\theta^2/r\,dr$

Results

Core Growth Validation

  • Hovering rotor wake geometry predictions highly sensitive to $a_1$: $a_1 = O(10^{-3})$ to $O(10^{-4})$ correctly captured blade passage effects and radial contraction
  • Laminar diffusion alone ($a_1 = 0$) gave excellent hovering wake geometry agreement due to absence of close vortex interactions
  • Forward flight inflow at $\mu = 0.15$ showed strong sensitivity: only $a_1 = O(10^{-3})$ and $O(10^{-4})$ properly represented azimuthal inflow variations

Empirical Turbulent Diffusion Evidence

  • Peak swirl velocity correlation $\bar{V}_{\theta_{max}}(\bar{d}+d_0)^{1/2} = k$ validated for both fixed-wing (Refs. 2) and rotating-wing (Refs. 25) measurements
  • Non-dimensional core growth rate $b_1 \propto \sqrt{a_1 + Re_v^{-1/2}}$; experimental data from 15+ studies confirmed Squire's hypothesis at high $Re_v$
  • Apparent viscosity coefficient $\delta$ approximately constant (laminar-like) at $Re_v < 10^5$ and increases linearly at higher Reynolds numbers

Velocity Profile Comparisons

  • Fixed-wing tip vortex (Ref. 2): $n=2$ model showed good agreement with measured swirl velocities at $z/c = 1.0$, $2.0$, and $4.0$, confirming self-similarity
  • Rotating-wing tip vortex (Ref. 25): swirl velocity showed strongly self-similar profile across wake ages $\zeta = 9.7^\circ$ to $232.0^\circ$; $n=2$ model matched well
  • Axial velocity showed wake-like deficit profile; some asymmetric deviations observed in rotating-wing data but overall agreement considered good
  • Radial velocities confirmed to be of much smaller magnitude ($O(\varepsilon^2)$); measurements from Ref. 2 showed scatter but agreed with $n=2$ trend
  • The $n=2$ algebraic model closely resembles the Newman exponential model, verifying it as an algebraic approximation to the Navier-Stokes solution

Results Interpretation

  • The self-similarity of velocity profiles when scaled by core radius $r_c$ is a key finding — it validates the use of a single algebraic function for the entire velocity field
  • Squire's eddy viscosity hypothesis ($\delta \propto Re_v$) is empirically confirmed across multiple decades of vortex Reynolds number, providing a physically motivated turbulent diffusion correction
  • The discrepancy between rotating-wing ($a_1 \sim 10^{-4}$) and fixed-wing ($a_1 \sim 10^{-5}$) values is attributed to implicit vortex straining effects from filament curvature and wake distortion in rotor measurements
  • Values of $\delta < 1$ observed in some experiments are physically impossible (less diffusion than laminar), highlighting measurement uncertainties in vortex flow experiments
  • The laminar-to-turbulent transition in vortex core growth is governed by a critical Richardson number: strong rotational velocity re-laminarizes turbulence entrained from outside the core
  • The integer parameter $n$ provides a flexible model family: $n=1$ (Scully), $n=2$ (best Lamb-Oseen approximation), $n \to \infty$ (Rankine); $n=2$ is recommended for practical applications
  • The three-component velocity model (swirl + axial + radial) is self-consistent as it satisfies the simplified Navier-Stokes equations, unlike ad hoc models combining independent profiles
  • Forward flight conditions are more sensitive to core growth model than hover, because tip vortices linger near the tip-path-plane longer, producing more severe BVI-related inflow variations
7

Conclusion

  • The Lamb-Oseen model extended with Squire's hypothesis provides a validated semi-empirical framework for viscous core growth that accounts for turbulent diffusion proportional to $Re_v$
  • An empirical value of $a_1 = O(10^{-4})$ to $O(10^{-5})$ is recommended, with rotating-wing applications trending toward the higher end
  • The proposed three-component algebraic velocity model yields continuous, bounded profiles that asymptote to zero at large distances from the vortex axis
  • The axial velocity exhibits a wake-like deficit at the vortex center; radial velocity is directed inward with magnitude much smaller than swirl or axial components
  • Free-vortex wake predictions are sensitive to core growth assumptions; the proposed model with empirically calibrated parameters improves hovering and forward-flight wake geometry and airload predictions
  • The model with $n=2$ is validated against fixed-wing and rotating-wing experiments, demonstrating self-similar velocity profiles consistent with Navier-Stokes-based solutions
  • The generalized model is suitable for integration into comprehensive rotor aeroacoustic analyses requiring accurate vortex-induced velocity fields
