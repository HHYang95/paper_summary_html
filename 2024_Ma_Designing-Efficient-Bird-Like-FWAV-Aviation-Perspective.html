Overview

This topical review provides a comprehensive survey of bird-like flapping-wing aerial vehicle (BFAV) design from the perspective of aviation engineering, covering overall parameter design, flapping wing design, and drive system design. The authors systematically analyze the scaling laws relating body mass $m$ to wing parameters (e.g., $b \propto m^{0.39}$, $S \propto m^{0.72}$, $f \propto m^{-0.33}$), review over 30 successfully flown BFAV prototypes spanning two decades, and catalog multi-mode motion capabilities. The paper identifies five key challenges: refining scaling laws, building universal design methods, advancing flapping wing optimization, improving drive system efficiency (currently only ~46.5% of input power generates aerodynamic force), and achieving miniaturization.

Introduction

Motivation

  • Flapping-wing flight achieves higher propulsive efficiency than fixed-wing or rotary-wing flight at low Reynolds numbers, motivating bio-inspired aerial vehicle development
  • Approximately one million flying species demonstrate highly adapted flight structures optimized through evolution
  • Birds outperform man-made vehicles at the same scale in flight speed per unit body length and roll rate (Shyy et al., 1999)

State of the Art

  • FAVs are categorized into insect-like (IFAVs) and bird-like (BFAVs), with BFAVs offering superior endurance, range, and load capacity
  • BFAVs consist of flapping wings, drive system, control system, energy components, and mission payload integrated through the airframe
  • Aerodynamic mechanism differs fundamentally from fixed-wing: flapping wings generate both lift and thrust simultaneously, making them inseparable
  • BFAV technology is more mature than IFAV for practical applications requiring long range and payload

Gap & Contribution

  • Existing reviews focus on biological flight mechanisms rather than practical engineering design methods for BFAVs
  • This review bridges the gap by focusing on solutions and proven tools for aviation designers
  • Covers three critical design domains: overall design, flapping wing design, and drive system design
  • Provides a comprehensive catalog of 30+ successfully flown prototypes with detailed parameter data

Related Works

Biological Flight & Scaling Laws

  • Rayner (1988) and Norberg (1990) derived power function relations between wing geometric parameters and flight parameters at different weights
  • Pennycuick (1996) revised flapping frequency estimates using extensive bird species data
  • Taylor et al. (2003) introduced the Strouhal number similarity principle: $St = fA/V$, with peak propulsive efficiency near $St = 0.3$
  • Weis-Fogh (1973) and Ellington (1984) characterized the "clap and fling" mechanism that boosts lift by 15%–25%

Computational Aerodynamics

  • Ghommem et al. (2012) optimized flapping wing motion using UVLM, showing second-order torsion/bending modes enhance efficiency
  • Stanford and Beran (2010) optimized chord torsion and spanwise bending under lift/thrust constraints
  • Du and Sun (2010) found spanwise twist reduced power by 5% and bending deformation increased lift by 10%
  • Heathcote and Gursul (2007) showed optimal propulsion efficiency at deformation phase angles of $95^\circ$–$100^\circ$

Key Prototypes

  • Microbat (2001): 15 cm wingspan, 10.5 g, first MEMS-fabricated BFAV
  • Dove/HIT (2018): 50 cm wingspan, 220 g, 30 min endurance, V-tail with autonomous flight
  • Cloud Owl (2023): 160 cm, 1310 g, Guinness World Record of 2 h 34 min flight endurance
  • HIT Hawk (2021): 200 cm, 1150 g, 65 min endurance, 358 g payload

Materials and Methods

Overall Parameter Design (Scaling Laws)

  • Wingspan $b \propto m^{0.39}$, wing area $S \propto m^{0.72}$, wing loading $Q \propto m^{0.28}$, aspect ratio $AR \propto m^{0.06}$
  • Flapping frequency $f \propto m^{-0.27}$ for birds excluding hummingbirds
  • Minimum power speed $V_{mp} = 9.73 m^{0.167}$ and maximum range speed $V_{mr} = 13.136 m^{0.167}$
  • Strouhal number $St = fA/V$ governs propulsive efficiency: peak ~70% at $St = 0.2$–$0.4$, optimum at $St = 0.3$
  • Weight distribution (400–800 g class): structure 72%, power plant 12%, battery 12%, avionics 4%

Layout Design

  • Two wing configurations: two-wing (superior range/endurance) and X-wing (clap-and-fling, better for hovering)
  • Three tail forms: fan-shaped (light, poor stability), T-tail (good stability, heavy), V-tail (balanced)
  • Guzman et al.: inverted V-tail achieved 3.7 m turn radius at 1.6 rad/s turning speed
  • Flapping wing wake significantly affects tail lift coefficient, shifting the neutral point

Flapping Wing Design

  • Bird wings: arm wing (curved, thick airfoil) and hand wing (flat, thin); hand wing length 40%–75% of span depending on species
  • Key kinematics: waving (fundamental), twisting (optimal $\sim 90^\circ$ phase with waving), sweeping (up to 10% efficiency gain), folding (~20% lift-to-drag improvement)
  • Parameters decomposed: geometric/flow → lift-drag; flapping/stiffness → thrust, enabling independent optimization
  • Three optimization model tiers: blade element theory, UVLM, unsteady Navier–Stokes equations

Drive System Design

  • Configurations: single crank rocker (simple, asymmetric), double crank rocker (symmetric, heavier), space crank rocker (large-scale)
  • Multi-DOF mechanisms: flapping-twisting coupling (Han et al.), flapping-twisting-sweeping (Ji et al.), conical rocker arm (Chen et al., 2022)
  • Elastic energy storage: springs reduce motor torque variation by up to 56.32% and peak torque by 12%; flexible mechanisms achieve 66% peak reduction
  • System modeling: bond graph (Jahanbin et al.), ADAMS + MATLAB co-simulation (Peng et al.), dynamic parameter identification (Nian et al.)

Results

Prototype Performance Trends

  • 30+ BFAV prototypes cataloged (2001–2023): wingspans from 10 cm (4.72 g) to 230 cm (880 g)
  • Endurance improved from 0.3 min (Microbat, 2001) to 154 min (Cloud Owl, 2023) — approximately 500x improvement
  • Payload capacity reached 520 g (Cloud Owl) and 358 g (HIT Hawk), enabling practical mission equipment
  • Design trend shifted from miniaturization (pre-2013, < 30 cm) to practical large-scale (post-2013, > 100 cm)

Multi-Mode Motion

  • Intermittent flapping-gliding increases endurance; latching structures enable power-off gliding
  • Perching mechanisms developed for energy conservation; solar cell integration extends mission time
  • Jumping take-off mechanisms (parallelogram, four-bar, five-bar) mostly remain at simulation stage
  • Hybrid quad-rotor + flapping configuration enables autonomous take-off/landing and extended fixed-point reconnaissance

Applications

  • Commercial success: GoGobird 201 achieved 200,000 annual sales as a BFAV toy
  • Military viability: Dove completed 3,000+ flights across 10+ regions, operating $-10^\circ$C to $35^\circ$C, level 4 wind, light rain
  • Image stabilization during flapping flight effectively resolved, enabling clear video transmission
  • X-wing BFAVs achieve vertical take-off without additional devices, with seamless hover-to-forward flight transition

Results Interpretation

Design Philosophy Evolution

  • The field has transitioned from biology-driven miniaturization to engineering-driven practical design after 2013
  • Small-scale (< 30 cm) performance has shown limited improvement over two decades, suggesting fundamental technical barriers
  • The lift-drag / thrust parameter decoupling is a key engineering insight simplifying multi-parameter optimization
  • Carbon fiber composites dominate skeleton material selection due to superior specific strength and modulus

Efficiency & Power

  • Only ~46.5% of input power converts to aerodynamic force (Nguyen et al.), indicating major improvement potential
  • Spring-based energy storage reduces motor torque variation by 56.32% and peak torque by 12%
  • Flexible mechanisms achieve up to 66% peak torque reduction compared to rigid mechanisms
  • Strong electromechanical coupling demands system-level (not component-level) optimization

Limitations

  • Scaling laws from biological data may not directly apply due to inferior aerodynamic performance of engineered wings
  • No consistent principle exists for take-off weight determination, causing iterative design cycles
  • Trial-and-error remains prevalent in flapping wing design, lacking systematic methodology
  • Multi-DOF drive mechanisms face weight, efficiency, and reliability challenges at small scales

Conclusion
  • Scaling law requires revision based on BFAV engineering data rather than pure biological bird data to reduce design iteration cycles
  • A universal overall design method tailored to BFAV characteristics (not fixed-wing methods) is critically needed
  • Flapping wing design must integrate numerical simulation and experimental testing; the gap between engineered and biological wings remains substantial
  • Drive system efficiency is a major bottleneck; multi-DOF systems and system-level electromechanical optimization are essential
  • Miniaturization remains significant for stealth applications but faces fundamental technical barriers at sub-30 cm scales
  • BFAVs are at a critical transition from prototype to practical application; autonomous operations, modular design, and intelligent decision-making are key enablers
  • Future priorities: perfecting design methods, improving component performance (especially motors and flexible structures), and promoting practical deployment
