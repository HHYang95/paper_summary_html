Overview

This paper describes the design, development, and flight testing of Dove, a biomimetic flapping-wing micro air vehicle (FWMAV) developed at Northwestern Polytechnical University. The vehicle evolved through three developmental stages spanning over a decade. The final Dove has a mass of 220 g, a wingspan of 50 cm, flapping frequency of 4–12 Hz, and endurance of 30 min. Key achievements include a passively deforming flexible membrane wing designed via CFD/CSD coupled analysis, an optimized four-bar linkage flapping mechanism, and an Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF)-based flight control system with total energy control for the strongly nonlinear vibrating environment of flapping flight. The platform operates fully autonomously and transmits live stabilized color video over 4 km.

Introduction

Context and Motivation

  • FWMAVs offer potential aerodynamic advantages at small scales. Representative platforms include AeroVironment's Nano Hummingbird (19 g, hovering), Festo's SmartBird (450 g, gull-inspired), and BionicOpter (175 g, dragonfly-inspired with 13 DOF).
  • Unlike hovering-focused or demonstrator platforms, Dove targets practical application with fully autonomous flight, live video surveillance, and 30 min endurance.
  • NPU's research program began in 2000 and progressed through three evolutionary stages with numerous prototypes (PY1 through Dove).

Design Emphases

  • Dove was focused on application performance: autonomous flight including climb after launch and descent before landing, live colored video with real-time stabilization.
  • The biological pigeon-inspired appearance was a final-stage design objective, applied to the ASN-211 variant to create the Dove.

Materials and Methods

Flexible Wing Design

  • Design loop: theoretical/experimental design → fabrication → test → refinement. Biological scaling laws from Shyy et al. for initial parameters.
  • Wind tunnel experiments confirmed thrust is strongly affected by unsteady flapping effects; lift depends primarily on speed and angle of attack ($Re \approx 5 \times 10^4$–$10 \times 10^4$).
  • Parametric studies: larger aspect ratios yield higher $L/D$ (set to 7); inner wing generates lift, outer wing generates thrust; positive camber helps lift but reduces thrust.
  • CFD/CSD coupled solver for aeroelastic analysis: triangular main frame with longitudinal ribs selected. Airfoil Eppler 378 with half-convex rib cross-section for asymmetric upstroke/downstroke deformation.
  • Final wing: 7 g each, 50 cm span, aspect ratio 7.4, composite and polymeric materials.

Flapping Mechanism

  • Multiple four-bar linkage variants explored; final design via fuzzy optimization: single-rack construction with unsymmetrical four-bar linkage. Average inter-wing phase lag: 2.0°.
  • Wind tunnel testing revealed fixed-amplitude operating region: $\Phi \approx$ 73° at $f =$ 8 Hz across $V =$ 6–10 m/s.
  • Final mechanism: POM/aluminum/titanium, 35 g (incl. 16 g brushless DC motor), drives 4–12 Hz.

Avionics and Flight Control

  • FCS: MEMS gyroscopes, accelerometer, magnetometer, barometer, GPS, microprocessor. Mass ≈35 g.
  • Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF) for attitude estimation + total energy control for adaptive longitudinal control.
  • Actuator narrow bandwidth acts as natural low-pass filter, converting periodic nonlinear dynamics to time-averaged linear characteristics.
  • Data link: QPSK modulation, 4 km range, combined telemetry + compressed video. Camera + data link: 25 g.

Results

Wing Aerodynamic Performance

  • Residual thrust curve crosses zero at $\alpha \approx$ 17°, where lift ≈230 g — exceeding 220 g takeoff weight. Confirms $L > G$ and $T = D$ at operating condition.
  • CFD/CSD solver validated against wind tunnel experiments for the flexible membrane wing.

Flight Test Performance

  • Fully autonomous flight demonstrated in 6–8 m/s wind with adequate controllability and autopilot effectiveness.
  • On-board camera (OV7725): spatial resolution 0.25 m at 50 m altitude, 0.51 m at 100 m, 0.76 m at 150 m.
  • Ground station image processing eliminates most flapping-induced oscillation for clear surveillance video.

Development Evolution

  • Stage I (PY1–PY12): Fundamental principles, wing structure, control philosophy. PY12 achieved 12 min RC flight.
  • Stage II (PY16–PY17): Enlarged interim prototypes for avionics testing. PY16 carried 80 g net payload.
  • Stage III (PY19, ASN-211, Dove): System integration. PY19 first autonomous flight; Dove achieved 30 min autonomous with biological pigeon appearance.

Results Interpretation

Design Philosophy

  • The three-stage iterative prototyping approach proved effective for FWMAV development where theoretical foundations are incomplete, with each stage building upon predecessors' lessons.
  • Co-design of wing aerodynamics and mechanism kinematics through the fixed-amplitude operating region avoids variable-amplitude actuation complexity.

Engineering Trade-offs

  • Four-bar linkage introduces 2.0° average phase asymmetry between wings, requiring careful design to prevent lateral/directional wobbling during high-speed oscillation.
  • UKF control originally for fixed-wing aircraft worked on the flapping platform because actuator bandwidth naturally filters high-frequency vibrations, effectively linearizing the system.
  • Downsizing from 1 m (PY16) to 50 cm (Dove) while maintaining payload capacity required substantial efficiency gains, achieved primarily through optimized flexible wing design.

Conclusion

Summary

  • Demonstrated a complete FWMAV system capable of 30 min fully autonomous flight with live video at 220 g mass and 50 cm wingspan.
  • Flexible membrane wing with triangular frame and longitudinal ribs achieves passive deformation for simultaneous lift and thrust. Airfoil E387 with half-convex ribs produces beneficial asymmetric deformation.
  • Four-bar linkage mechanism optimized via motor-wing compatible design, exploiting fixed-amplitude operating region across multiple flight speeds.
  • UKF-based flight control with total energy regulation successfully handles the strongly nonlinear vibrating environment of flapping flight.

Future Work

  • Increase bionic level in wing flexibility and flapping manner for improved agility and biological resemblance.
  • Reduce avionics mass and advance battery technology to extend endurance beyond 30 min.
