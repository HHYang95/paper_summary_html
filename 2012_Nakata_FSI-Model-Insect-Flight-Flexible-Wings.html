Overview

This paper presents a loosely coupled FSI model combining a finite element method (FEM)-based computational structural dynamics solver with a CFD-based insect dynamic flight simulator. The CSD solver models thin shell structures of insect wings using AT/DKT triangular elements with anisotropic material properties representing veins, membranes, and fibers. The CFD solver employs a multi-block, overset-grid, fortified Navier-Stokes approach. Applied to hawkmoth hovering, the model demonstrates that wing flexibility produces approximately 20% higher vertical force and 8.5% greater aerodynamic efficiency compared to rigid wings, achieved through delayed leading-edge vortex (LEV) breakdown, advanced wing rotation phase, and elastic energy release mechanisms.

Introduction

Context of Insect Flexible-Wing Aerodynamics

  • Insects generate high aerodynamic forces through flapping wings whose inherent flexibility causes considerable elastic deformations during flight, creating a strongly coupled FSI problem
  • Experimental approaches face difficulties in simultaneously measuring wing kinematics, deformation, flow fields, and aerodynamic forces
  • Combined experimental-computational methods can predict how deformation affects performance, but they do not couple flow and structure solutions and cannot capture mutual interactions

Gap in Existing Research

  • Previous FSI analyses were limited to 2D foils or highly idealized single 3D wing models without realistic insect morphology
  • No prior study integrated body-wing morphology, kinematics, unsteady aerodynamics, wing structural dynamics, and energetic evaluation (forces, power, efficiency) for a realistic insect model
  • The governing fluid equations are the 3D incompressible unsteady Navier-Stokes equations in strong conservation form

Materials and Methods

CFD-Based Insect Dynamic Flight Simulator

  • The flight simulator employs a multi-block, overset-grid, fortified Navier-Stokes solver with morphological model from digitized images of hawkmoth Agrius convolvuli
  • Simplified harmonic kinematics at wing base: $\phi = \phi'_{c1} \cos(\omega t)$, $\alpha = \alpha'_{s1} \sin(\omega t)$, $\theta = 0$
  • Reynolds number $Re \approx 6300$ based on mean chord $c_m = 18.3$ mm, wing length $R = 50.5$ mm, and flapping frequency $f = 26.1$ Hz; reduced frequency $k \approx 0.3$

FEM-Based CSD Solver for Thin Shell Structures

  • AT/DKT triangular shell elements with updated Lagrangian formulation for large displacements and rotations
  • Virtual work principle: $\int \delta \mathbf{E}^T \mathbf{S} \, dV_e + \int \rho_s \delta \mathbf{R}^T \ddot{\mathbf{R}} \, dV_e = \delta W$ where $\mathbf{S}$ and $\mathbf{E}$ are the 2nd Piola-Kirchhoff stress and incremental Green-Lagrange strain
  • Anisotropic material modeling via rule of mixture: $E_{c1} = V_f E_v + (1-V_f)E'_m$ (vein direction) and $1/E_{c2} = V_f/E_v + (1-V_f)/E'_m$ (transverse)

Loose Coupling Strategy

  • Conventional serial staggered (CSS) procedure with a second-order time-accurate predictor for structural displacement
  • Load transfer employs Galerkin-based interpolation of aerodynamic pressures from fluid surface to structural surface
  • Elastic-statics BEM approach handles grid regeneration for large deformations

Dimensionless Scaling Parameters

  • Five key parameters: Reynolds number $Re$, Strouhal number $St$, reduced frequency $k$, elastic parameter $\Pi_1$ (elastic-to-aerodynamic force ratio), and inertial parameter $\Pi_2$ (inertia-to-aerodynamic force ratio)
  • For the hawkmoth, spanwise and chordwise $\Pi_1$ are approximately 40 and 0.4, respectively

Results

Benchmark Validation Cases

  • Spanwise-flexible rectangular wing (NACA0012, AR=6) at $Re = 30{,}000$: computed wingtip displacements and thrust coefficients showed good agreement with measurements by Heathcote et al.
  • Chordwise-flexible wing at $Re = 9{,}000$: trailing-edge displacements and thrust coefficients agreed well with experimental data
  • Rotating hawkmoth forewing: simulated bending angles showed excellent agreement with low-order Fourier components of measured data by Combes and Daniel

In-Flight Wing Deformation

  • Flexible hawkmoth wing exhibited nose-down twist up to 12 degrees at wing tip, with low-stiffness hindwing model reaching 17 degrees, consistent with measured values of 15-20 degrees
  • Positive camber of less than 2% predicted, representing physically correct sign unlike negative cambers in some prior studies
  • Wing deformed significantly immediately after stroke reversal when inertial forces increase rapidly

Aerodynamic Force Enhancement

  • Flexible wing generated approximately 20% higher mean vertical force (18.4 mN vs 15.4 mN) compared to rigid wing; both exceeded hawkmoth weight of 14.7 mN
  • Flexible wing produced a more stable LEV that delayed breakdown and created larger negative pressure regions
  • Stronger downstroke vortex rings with more intense downwash observed for the flexible wing

Results Interpretation

Mechanisms of Aerodynamic Enhancement

  • Three synergistic mechanisms: (1) wing deformation stabilizes the LEV, delaying breakdown; (2) wing rotation phase is advanced, strengthening wake and rotational circulation; (3) elastic energy stored during deceleration is rapidly released, increasing angular velocities
  • Vertical force-to-horizontal force ratio consistently higher for flexible wing, indicating more efficient force vectoring

Energetic Analysis and Efficiency

  • Aerodynamic efficiency: 43.4% (flexible) vs 40.0% (rigid), approximately 8.5% improvement
  • Muscle-mass-specific aerodynamic power: 95.3 W/kg (flexible) vs 83.3 W/kg (rigid)
  • While flexible wing requires more power due to increased drag, the disproportionately larger increase in vertical force results in net efficiency improvement

Role of Wing Anisotropy and Fore-Hindwing Connection

  • Low-stiffness hindwing model showed decreased horizontal force with marginal effect on vertical force, leading to reduced aerodynamic power and enhanced efficiency
  • Positive camber prediction attributed to realistic anisotropic material properties, contrasting with negative cambers in isotropic models
  • Performance differences between original and low-stiffness hindwing models were smaller than between flexible and rigid, suggesting the unified model captures essential physics

Conclusion

Summary of Achievements

  • The FSI model successfully integrates a thin-shell FEM solver with anisotropic composite material modeling into a CFD-based insect flight simulator
  • Validation across three benchmark cases at Reynolds numbers from $O(10^3)$ to $O(10^4)$ demonstrates robustness and accuracy
  • Wing flexibility enhances hovering performance through multiple synergistic mechanisms

Implications for Bio-Inspired Flight

  • Approximately 20% increase in vertical force and 8.5% improvement in aerodynamic efficiency highlight the importance of passive deformation in insect flight
  • Elastic energy storage and release during stroke reversal suggests a passive mechanism insects may exploit without additional muscular effort
  • Results provide quantitative design guidance for micro air vehicles incorporating flexible wings

Limitations and Future Directions

  • Simplified symmetric wing kinematics produce non-zero net horizontal forces, requiring trimmed hovering kinematics in future studies
  • Future work should incorporate inherent wing camber (up to 4-5%), more detailed structural components, and asymmetric wing kinematics
