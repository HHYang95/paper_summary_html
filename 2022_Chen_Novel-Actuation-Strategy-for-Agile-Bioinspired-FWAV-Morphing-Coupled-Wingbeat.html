Overview

Core Contribution

This paper presents RoboFalcon, a bioinspired flapping-wing aerial vehicle (FWAV) with a 1.2 m wingspan and 600 g flight weight modeled after the peregrine falcon. The vehicle employs a novel Cross-shaft Rocker Mechanism (CRM) that mechanically couples wing morphing with flapping motion, reproducing the wingbeat kinematics observed in birds and bats where wings extend during the downstroke and tuck during the upstroke. A morphing decoupler enables independent bilateral wing control for agile rolling maneuvers via asymmetric downstrokes.

Significance

RoboFalcon is the first flyable bioinspired platform that achieves a morphing-coupled wingbeat pattern propelled by a single motor, demonstrating 90-degree rolling maneuvers within 2.5 wingbeat cycles (open loop) and peak rolling angular velocities of approximately 400 deg/s. The design bridges a critical gap between biological wingbeat kinematics and engineered flight platforms.

Introduction

Biological Inspiration and Engineering Gap

  • Flying vertebrates exhibit coupled morphing-flapping kinematics during level flight: they reduce wingspan and area during the upstroke via wing flexion and extend fully during the downstroke.
  • This strategy improves aerodynamic efficiency by minimizing negative lift during the upstroke.
  • Previous bioinspired platforms either actuate morphing independently from flapping or rely on passive unfolding, failing to reproduce the coupled pattern.
  • The engineering challenge lies in actuating morphing at the same frequency as flapping while keeping the wings lightweight.

Research Objectives

  • The authors aim to develop a flyable FWAV that (1) mechanically couples morphing and flapping via a novel mechanism, (2) enables independent bilateral morphing control for rolling maneuvers, and (3) incorporates a bioinspired wrist mounting angle to tune passive wing twist.
  • The equilibrium flight condition requires: $$\bar{T}_{net} = 0, \quad \bar{L} = W$$ where $\bar{T}_{net}$ is the cycle-averaged net thrust, $\bar{L}$ is the cycle-averaged lift, and $W$ is the vehicle weight.

Related Works

Morphing Wing Platforms

  • Previous morphing-wing FWAVs have applied bioinspired wing folding but typically use independent actuators for morphing or leave it passive.
  • Platforms such as bat-inspired robots with elastic membranes achieved morphing but without coupling it to the flapping cycle.
  • Wind tunnel studies and simulations have investigated coupled wingbeat kinematics, but translating these to a flyable platform remained an open challenge due to actuator power and weight constraints.

Lateral Control in FWAVs

  • Existing lateral control strategies for FWAVs rely on conventional surfaces (elevators, rudders) or propeller-based thrust modulation.
  • Some designs use split-cycle or asymmetric flapping amplitude for roll control, but these approaches have not been combined with bioinspired morphing-coupled wingbeat patterns.
  • The frequency-dependent rolling moment from bilateral asymmetric downstrokes had not been previously reported for bird- or bat-inspired FWAVs.

Materials and Methods

Platform Design

  • RoboFalcon is designed with reference to peregrine falcon morphology, featuring a maximum wingspan of 1.2 m and total flight weight of 600 g.
  • The body frame uses CNC-cut 2 mm carbon fiber composite boards assembled into a 3D puzzle structure.
  • Avionics include a PixHawk mini autopilot with IMU, RC receiver, pitot tube for airspeed sensing, GPS module, and power management module.
  • The vehicle is powered by a 3S 850 mAh Li-Po battery with a brushless DC motor (SunnySky X2212 KV980).

Bat-Style Morphing Wing

  • The wing follows a bat-style layout with an arm wing (humerus and radius spars) and a hand wing (three curved fingers).
  • The arm wing is a one-DOF multilink mechanism where extension and flexion are determined by the linear distance between the shoulder and lever 1.
  • A 3D-printed knuckle at the wrist joint introduces a tilted mounting angle $\theta$ around the radial spar, creating a pitch-up hand wing that partially offsets passive wrist pronation during the downstroke.
  • The wing skeleton kinematics are derived from vector closed-loop equations: $$\vec{OA} = \vec{OC} + \vec{CA}$$ $$\vec{CB} + \vec{BE} + \vec{ED} + \vec{DC} = 0$$

Cross-shaft Rocker Mechanism (CRM)

  • The CRM uses a cross-shaped shaft where the longer shaft serves as the flapping axis and the shorter as the shoulder rotation axis for morphing.
  • As the gear rotates, a rocker arm traces a conical trajectory, and a link rod converts this into linear reciprocating motion on a slider, coupling wing morphing to flapping with a 90-degree phase lag.
  • The mechanism allows adjusting flapping amplitude, dihedral angle, and stroke plane tilt by repositioning the cross shaft.

Morphing Decoupler

  • The decoupler comprises three sliders: Motor Input Slider (MIS), Output Slider (OS), and Servo Input Slider (SIS).
  • During the downstroke, the OS decouples from the MIS and is locked in the extended state by the SIS.
  • During the upstroke, the OS recouples with the MIS for motor-driven tucking. This enables independent bilateral servo control for asymmetric downstrokes without requiring high-frequency servo actuation.

Wind Tunnel Testing

  • Aerodynamic forces were measured using a six-axis load cell at 8 m/s airspeed for four wrist mounting angles ($\theta$ = 10, 15, 20, 25 degrees) across angles of attack from 2 to 12 degrees and various flapping frequencies.
  • Rolling moments were measured for bilateral asymmetric downstroke configurations.

Results

Wrist Mounting Angle Effects

  • Increasing the wrist mounting angle $\theta$ decreased the equilibrium angle of attack, indicating that wrist supination-pronation movements can trim the angle of attack in steady level flight.
  • The 25-degree configuration achieved equilibrium at the lowest angle of attack but required higher flapping frequency and power consumption.
  • The morphing-coupled wingbeat demonstrated higher lift efficiency compared to conventional wingbeat patterns but lower thrust, suggesting suitability for low-speed flight.

Rolling Moment Characteristics

  • The cycle-averaged rolling moment from bilateral asymmetric downstrokes correlated linearly with flapping frequency.
  • Rolling moment remained near zero during the upstroke and magnified only during the asymmetric downstroke, confirming that downstroke aerodynamics dominate roll moment generation.
  • Peak rolling moment magnitude increased with flapping frequency.

Rolling Maneuver Performance

  • Tethered tests demonstrated a 90-degree roll angle change within 2.5 wingbeat cycles (open loop).
  • In closed-loop PID-controlled outdoor flight, the vehicle achieved 90-degree rolls within 3-4 wingbeat cycles at the apex of sharp turns.
  • Peak rolling angular velocity reached approximately 400 deg/s with rolling angular acceleration up to 5000 deg/s$^2$.

Lateral Model Identification

  • A third-order state-space model was identified from flight data: $$\begin{pmatrix} \dot{p} \\ \dot{r} \\ \dot{\phi} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -2.08 & -4.08 & 0 \\ 0.92 & -0.07 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} p \\ r \\ \phi \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 4.17 \\ 0.08 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \delta_\phi$$
  • where $p$ is roll rate, $r$ is yaw rate, $\phi$ is roll angle, and $\delta_\phi$ is the roll command. The model showed reasonable agreement with flight data below 1 Hz.

Results Interpretation

Morphing-Coupled Wingbeat Advantages

  • The coupling of morphing and flapping through the CRM eliminates the need for independent high-frequency morphing actuators, solving a key engineering bottleneck.
  • The 90-degree phase lag between flapping and morphing ensures full wing extension at the optimal downstroke position.
  • The higher lift efficiency of the morphing-coupled pattern validates biological observations that wing tucking during the upstroke reduces negative lift contributions.

Roll Control Mechanism

  • The frequency-dependent rolling moment provides a natural gain scheduling effect: higher flapping frequencies yield stronger roll authority.
  • The morphing decoupler elegantly separates the coupled motion when maneuvering is required, allowing lightweight low-frequency servos to control rolling.
  • The rolling agility (90 degrees in 2.5 wingbeats) is comparable to flying vertebrates and exceeds many previous bioinspired platforms of similar scale.

Wrist Angle as a Design Parameter

  • The wrist mounting angle serves as a passive trim mechanism analogous to wrist supination/pronation in birds and bats.
  • This provides an engineering solution to optimize the power consumption and lift-thrust configuration without active control, suggesting that biological wrist kinematics serve a similar trimming function during steady flight.

Conclusion

Key Achievements

  • RoboFalcon demonstrates the first flyable implementation of a morphing-coupled wingbeat pattern using the novel CRM.
  • The platform achieves stable outdoor flight with a simple PID controller and exhibits high rolling agility through bilateral asymmetric downstrokes.
  • Wind tunnel results confirm that the wrist mounting angle can tune the equilibrium flight state, and the rolling moment scales linearly with flapping frequency.

Future Directions

  • The CRM architecture supports additional kinematic adjustments (flapping amplitude, dihedral angle, stroke plane tilt) that were not activated in this study but could enable more complex flight maneuvers.
  • The authors suggest that applying this mechanism to insect- or hummingbird-scale platforms could advance the field of bioinspired micro aerial vehicles.
  • Further investigation of the coupled wingbeat pattern at different flight speeds and Reynolds numbers would extend the applicability of the design.
