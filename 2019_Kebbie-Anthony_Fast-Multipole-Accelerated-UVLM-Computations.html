Overview

This paper presents the first integration of the fast multipole method (FMM) with an unsteady vortex lattice method (UVLM)-based nonlinear, unsteady aerodynamic simulator. The FMM accelerates wake velocity evaluation — the dominant computational cost (>90% of total time) — by decomposing the Biot-Savart velocity field into near-field (direct) and far-field (multipole-approximated) interactions using a hierarchical octree. Through systematic tuning of three parameters (truncation number p_F = 12, quadrature order N_q = 2, clustering parameter s = 300), the per-step complexity is reduced from O(K^2) to $O(K)$, yielding an approximately 90x speedup (15 min to 10 s) with relative L_2-norm error of order \sim 10^{-5}. This enables previously intractable domain sizes for aeroelastic analysis within the DDDAS paradigm.

Introduction

Motivation: Decision Support for UAS Aeroelasticity

  • Unmanned aircraft systems (UASs) with high-aspect-ratio wings (e.g., joined-wing SensorCraft) are susceptible to aeroelastic instabilities from unforeseen environmental conditions
  • Linear aeroelastic models cannot capture postcritical behavior or generate reliable stability margins; fully coupled nonlinear methods are required
  • A decision support system (DSS) within the DDDAS paradigm combines nonlinear aeroelastic simulations with sensor data for real-time mission planning and failure prediction
  • Faster simulations mean more scenarios can be sampled, and computational models can be updated more rapidly from measurement data

Computational Bottleneck in UVLM

  • In aeroelastic simulations of the joined-wing SensorCraft, over 90% of computation time is consumed by evaluating wake velocities via the Biot-Savart law
  • The velocity field computation has O(NM) cost per time step, where N is the number of vortex segments and M is the number of field points; both grow with time
  • Prior acceleration via GPU computing (Chabalko et al., Fleischmann et al.) is hardware-dependent and caps scalability for large problems

FMM as Algorithmic Solution

  • The FMM, one of the 10 most influential algorithms of the 20th century, reduces N-body problem complexity from O(N^2) to O(N) or O(N \log N)
  • Originally developed by Greengard and Rokhlin (1987) for gravitational/electrostatic potentials; extended to vortex methods by Gumerov and Duraiswami (2013) via Lamb-Helmholtz decomposition
  • This paper is the first study implementing and examining FMM acceleration for a UVLM-based aerodynamic simulator

Materials and Methods

UVLM Aerodynamic Model

  • Inviscid, incompressible, irrotational flow assumed except at solid boundaries and wakes; bound-vortex sheets replaced by lattices of straight vortex segments with circulation \Gamma(t)
  • No-penetration boundary condition: [\mathbf{V}_B + \mathbf{V}_W + \mathbf{V}_\infty - \mathbf{V}_{\text{Body}}] \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}} = 0
  • Aerodynamic loads computed via the unsteady Bernoulli equation; wake nodes convected by local fluid velocity using first-order time integration

Biot-Savart Velocity Computation

  • Velocity at field point \mathbf{r} from vortex segment L_i: \mathbf{V}_i = \frac{\Gamma_i}{4\pi}\frac{\mathbf{L}_i \times \mathbf{r}_{1i}}{\|\mathbf{L}_i \times \mathbf{r}_{1i}\|^2}(\mathbf{L}_i \cdot (\hat{e}_{1i} - \hat{e}_{2i}))
  • Smoothing parameter \delta prevents singularity near vortex lines; validated to produce negligible error in aerodynamic coefficients
  • Summing N segment contributions at M field points yields O(NM) cost, motivating FMM acceleration

Fast Multipole Method Implementation

  • Octree data structure: domain enclosed in cube, recursively subdivided until each leaf box contains at most s sources (clustering parameter)
  • System equation split into near-field (sparse, direct) and far-field (dense, multipole-approximated) interactions
  • Multipole expansion with truncation number p_F using regular and singular spherical basis functions R_n^m and S_n^m
  • Vortex velocity expressed as curl of vector potential; each Cartesian component is a scalar harmonic function amenable to standard FMM for Laplace equation
  • Expansion coefficients computed via Gauss-Legendre quadrature of order N_q; zero error guaranteed when N_q = \lceil(p_F-1)/2\rceil + 1

Parameter Tuning Strategy

  • Step 1: Vary p_F (with N_q from zero-error formula and fixed s) to find acceptable accuracy
  • Step 2: Reduce N_q as much as possible within required accuracy
  • Step 3: Adjust s to minimize wall-clock time (optimum where sparse and dense computation times balance)

Results

FMM Parameter Tuning

  • Test configuration: planar rectangular wing, AR = 4, chord = 1 m, span = 4 m, 9 \times 36 panels, V_\infty = 125 m/s, \alpha = 5°
  • Truncation number: L_2-norm error decreases monotonically; \sim 10^{-5} at p_F = 12, \sim 10^{-8} at p_F = 24; optimal p_F = 12 balancing accuracy and cost
  • Quadrature order: N_q = 2, 3, 4 produce nearly identical accuracy; N_q = 1 degrades; wall-clock times approximately equal — N_q = 2 is optimal
  • Clustering parameter: strong minimum in wall-clock time at s = 300 (27.92 s vs 45.34 s at s = 100); accuracy varies minimally across all s values

Computational Cost Reduction

  • Optimal configuration: p_F = 12, N_q = 2, s = 300
  • Per-step wake velocity complexity reduced from O(K^2) to $O(K)$
  • Cumulative complexity over K steps reduced from O(K^3) to O(K^2)
  • FMM outperforms standard UVLM after only ~8 time steps (N \approx 1500 sources)
  • Approximately 90x speedup: 15 min reduced to 10 s with L_2 error \sim 10^{-5}

Method Comparison

  • Three-scalar-harmonic approach vs Lamb-Helmholtz (two functions): latter is ~20% faster at same p_F but with larger errors
  • Overall speed-vs-accuracy performance is approximately equivalent between the two formulations
  • FMM parameters found to be general: applicable across varying aspect ratios (4-16), angles of attack (5-20 deg), and flow conditions

Results Interpretation

  • The FMM fundamentally changes UVLM scaling: total algorithm complexity grows as O(K^2) instead of O(K^3), enabling long-duration simulations previously intractable for full aircraft configurations
  • The early crossover at k \approx 8 time steps indicates minimal FMM overhead, making the method beneficial from nearly the start of any practical simulation
  • Truncation number p_F dominates both accuracy and cost; quadrature order N_q has negligible impact beyond N_q = 2 (quadrature error dominated by truncation error); clustering parameter s controls the sparse/dense computation balance
  • The three-component (standard FMM) approach is simpler to implement than Lamb-Helmholtz decomposition while achieving equivalent accuracy-speed tradeoff, lowering the barrier for adoption
  • With >90% of aeroelastic simulation time in wake velocity, the FMM directly addresses the primary bottleneck and enables the DDDAS paradigm for real-time, sensor-steered decision support
  • The $O(K)$ per-step scaling makes the method particularly advantageous for growing wake problems where the number of vortex segments increases linearly with time steps

Conclusion

  • First implementation of FMM for accelerating a UVLM-based nonlinear unsteady aerodynamic simulator, addressing the literature gap
  • Systematic three-step parameter tuning: p_F = 12, N_q = 2, s = 300 yields optimal accuracy-cost tradeoff; parameters are general across flow conditions and geometries
  • Wake velocity complexity reduced from O(K^2) to $O(K)$ per step; ~90x speedup with L_2 error \sim 10^{-5}
  • Enables simulation of complex systems like the joined-wing SensorCraft that were previously computationally intractable
  • Future work: accelerate additional UVLM processes, study full aeroelastic coupling benefits, and combine FMM with GPU computing for further speedup
