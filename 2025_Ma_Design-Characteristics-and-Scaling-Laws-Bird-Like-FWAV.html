Overview

This paper systematically investigates the design characteristics and scaling laws of bird-like flapping-wing aerial vehicles (BFAVs) from the perspective of aviation designers. Scaling laws of birds relevant to BFAV design are compiled, covering wingspan, wing area, wing loading, aspect ratio, flapping frequency, amplitude, flight speed, and power consumption. A comparative scaling analysis between birds and 27 BFAV prototypes (5.9 g to 2.1 kg) reveals that BFAVs compensate for lower lift coefficients by enlarging wing area ($S = 0.331m^{0.72}$, approximately twice that of birds) rather than increasing flight speed. The concept of periodic average angular velocity $\bar{\omega} = 2\varphi f$ is introduced to unify frequency and amplitude effects into a single metric with $R^2 = 0.86$. A novel initial parameter determination method based on BFAV-specific scaling laws is proposed and validated through the design of a 30 g sparrow-like prototype achieving 23.6 min autonomous flight.

Introduction

Motivation and Background

  • Bird-like flapping-wing aerial vehicles (BFAVs) offer advantages in flight duration, range, and payload capacity, attracting considerable attention for reconnaissance and surveillance missions
  • Scaling analysis is the primary tool for establishing initial design parameters, but most scaling studies come from biological rather than engineering perspectives
  • Aviation designers use bird scaling laws to set initial parameters and then optimize iteratively, but the differences in wing performance between birds and artificial systems lead to lengthy design iterations

Research Questions

  • Are there statistically significant differences in scaling laws between birds and BFAVs resulting from variations in wings and engines?
  • Is it feasible to establish general principles for parameter determination based on existing BFAV prototype data rather than bird empirical formulas?
  • Can the periodic average angular velocity concept unify the dispersed frequency and amplitude statistics observed in BFAV datasets?

Related Works

Bird Scaling Laws

  • Norberg (2007) established universal scaling frameworks: $b = 1.17m^{0.39}$, $S = 0.16m^{0.72}$, $f = 3.98m^{-0.27}$, and aspect ratio $AR = 8.56m^{0.06}$
  • Scholey (1982) provided amplitude scaling $\varphi = 63.3m^{-0.119}$ and Nudds et al. (2004) demonstrated Strouhal number tuning for propulsive efficiency
  • Liu et al. (2023) reviewed scaling, geometry, kinematics, and aerodynamics from an engineering perspective

BFAV Design and Classification

  • Singh et al. (2022) classified actuation mechanisms for flapping-wing drones with structural block diagrams
  • Hassanalian et al. (2017) proposed wing sizing methodology for bio-inspired flapping-wing MAVs but relied on bird scaling laws directly
  • No prior work established generalized BFAV-specific scaling laws from empirical vehicle data for initial parameter determination

Aerodynamic Power Models

  • Total mechanical power follows $P_{\text{mech}} = \alpha/v + \beta v^3 + P_{\text{iner}}$ producing a U-shaped speed-power relationship
  • Inertial power scales as $P_{\text{iner}} = 8I_w\varphi^2 f^3$ and can account for 53% of total mechanical power in hovering hummingbirds
  • The paper derives flight power as $P_f = P_i - P_b$ (metabolic input minus basal metabolic rate), argued to be more appropriate for predicting BFAV battery output power than minimum power or maximum range power

Materials and Methods

Dataset and Inclusion Criteria

  • 27 BFAV prototypes with mass 5.9 g to 2.1 kg; inclusion criteria: single wing pair, plastic/carbon fiber construction, minimum 1 min flight time
  • Notable prototypes: Microbat (14 g), Robo Raven (290 g, 100 degrees amplitude), Dove (256 g, Guinness record), Cloud Owl (1.3 kg, 154 min endurance), USTB-Hawk (1.27 kg)
  • Missing aspect ratio and wing loading values calculated from definitions where raw data were unavailable

Scaling Analysis Framework

  • All parameters fitted to power-law form $y = am^b$ with regression, assessed by $R^2$, F-statistic, and P-value
  • Bird geometric similarity baseline: $S \propto m^{2/3}$, $Q \propto m^{1/3}$, $AR \propto m^0$, $f \propto m^{-1/3}$
  • Speed-wing loading correlation analyzed separately: $v = 1.361Q^{0.58}$ ($R^2 = 0.86$)

Periodic Average Angular Velocity Derivation

  • Wing motion modeled as sinusoidal: $\varphi(t) = \frac{1}{2}\varphi\sin(2\pi ft)$, yielding angular velocity $\omega(t) = \pi\varphi f\cos(2\pi ft)$
  • Period-averaged absolute angular velocity: $\bar{\omega} = \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T|\omega(t)|dt = 2\varphi f$
  • This metric captures combined kinematic intensity and enables equivalent motion parameter allocation for mechanism design

Parameter Determination Workflow

  • Step 1: Estimate take-off weight from payload via $k_{\text{pl}} = 0.304m_{\text{pl}}^{0.182}$ ($R^2 = 0.99$, max $k_{\text{pl}} = 0.3$)
  • Step 2: Cascade calculations: $b = 1.545m_t^{0.44}$, $v = 10.727m_t^{0.167}$, $Q = 0.588v^{1.724}$, $S = m_tg/Q$
  • Step 3: Kinematic allocation via $\bar{\omega} = 8.749m_t^{-0.32}$; power estimation via $P_f = 72.544m_t^{0.78}$

Results

Key BFAV Scaling Laws

  • Wingspan: $b = 1.545m^{0.44}$ ($R^2 = 0.82$) — larger than birds at equivalent mass
  • Wing area: $S = 0.331m^{0.72}$ ($R^2 = 0.82$, $P < 0.001$) — approximately twice bird values
  • Wing loading: $Q = 31.252m^{0.28}$ ($R^2 = 0.52$) — roughly half that of birds
  • Aspect ratio: $AR = 6.252m^{0.14}$ ($R^2 = 0.60$) — positive mass correlation, lower than birds
  • Frequency: $f = 4.539m^{-0.31}$ ($R^2 = 0.55$); angular velocity: $\bar{\omega} = 8.749m^{-0.32}$ ($R^2 = 0.86$)
  • Power: $P_f = 72.544m^{0.78}$ ($R^2 = 0.86$) — exceeds bird power at equivalent weight

Design Strategy Findings

  • BFAVs operate near minimum power speed on the U-shaped curve; increasing speed is uneconomical due to cubic power dependence
  • Wing area enlargement is the primary lift compensation strategy; birds with same mass sustain twice the wing loading at comparable speed
  • Flapping amplitude shows no clear mass scaling due to mechanism design trade-offs; periodic average angular velocity resolves this scatter

Sparrow Prototype Validation

  • 30 g take-off weight, 32 cm wingspan, 80 degrees flapping amplitude, 6 m/s flight speed
  • BFAV method: wing area 0.023 m$^2$, wing loading 12.81 N/m$^2$; bird method: 0.0128 m$^2$, 22.97 N/m$^2$ (requiring extensive iteration)
  • Wind tunnel: lift 30.7 g at 3.5 V (11 Hz), 48 g at 4.4 V (14.5 Hz); autonomous flight 23.6 min with 8.2 g Li-ion battery

Results Interpretation

Physics of BFAV Design Compromises

  • From $L = \frac{1}{2}C_L\rho v^2 S$: either speed or area can compensate for low $C_L$, but $P_{\text{aero}} = \alpha/v + \beta v^3$ penalizes speed increases
  • BFAVs therefore settle near minimum power speed and compensate through enlarged wing area — a rational engineering trade-off
  • The lower BFAV aspect ratio compared to birds further reduces $C_L$ via increased induced drag fraction

Periodic Average Angular Velocity Advantages

  • Strouhal number approach fails at small scales: predicts 98 degrees amplitude for 15 cm wingspan, impractical for linkage mechanisms
  • $\bar{\omega} = 2\varphi f$ achieves $R^2 = 0.86$ vs. $R^2 = 0.55$ for frequency alone and no clear trend for amplitude alone
  • Enables equivalent aerodynamic performance under different frequency-amplitude allocations, validated by wind tunnel studies showing similar lift and thrust trends

Weight Distribution Insights

  • Three representative prototypes (Bigbird 35 g, Dove 256 g, Cloud Owl 1.3 kg) show structural weight ~40%, decreasing electrical weight coefficient with mass
  • Battery coefficient above 25% is critical for endurance; below this threshold performance declines rapidly
  • Payload coefficient follows $k_{\text{pl}} = 0.304m_{\text{pl}}^{0.182}$ with practical maximum of 0.3

Conclusion

Principal Findings

  • BFAVs differ systematically from birds: approximately doubled wing area, halved wing loading, lower aspect ratio with positive mass correlation, and higher power consumption
  • Periodic average angular velocity ($\bar{\omega} = 2\varphi f$) unifies dispersed frequency and amplitude data into a single metric with high correlation ($R^2 = 0.86$)
  • BFAV-specific scaling laws provide more accurate initial parameters than bird-derived formulas, reducing design iteration cycles

Validation and Applicability

  • Sparrow prototype validates the method: initial parameters closely match final design values; 23.6 min autonomous flight with 30 g weight
  • The framework extends to BFAV designs across the 5.9 g to 2.1 kg range; broader applicability expected as more prototypes are included
  • Future work: multi-objective optimization model for parameter trade-offs; extension to novel wing materials and multi-pair configurations
