Overview

This paper presents an object-oriented unsteady vortex lattice method (UVLM) implemented in C++ for simulating the aerodynamics of flapping flight. The method models plunging, pitching, twisting, and flapping motions of finite-aspect-ratio wings with applications to micro air vehicles (MAVs) and avian flight analysis. The code incorporates free-wake relaxation, vortex stretching, and wake dissipation effects. Validation is performed against Theodorsen's theory for oscillating airfoils and Halfman's experimental data for plunging and pitching cases. The flapping results are compared to Walker's quasi-steady theory, demonstrating agreement at low reduced frequencies and revealing the limitations of quasi-steady assumptions at high reduced frequencies. A parametric survey of flapping parameters (twist angle, flap angle, flapping frequency) for a pigeon-like wing configuration provides insight into lift and thrust generation across the flapping cycle.

Introduction

Motivation and Context

  • Flapping flight exploits unsteady aerodynamic effects and is efficient for small flying objects including bats, birds, and insects
  • Growing interest in flapping-wing micro air vehicles (MAVs) drives need for predictive aerodynamic tools
  • Existing models of flapping avian flight remain limited in their ability to design devices relying on unsteady high-lift at low forward speeds
  • The relevant parameter space spans reduced frequency $k = \omega c / (2V_\infty)$ from quasi-steady ($k < 0.05$) to highly unsteady regimes
  • Reynolds numbers range from $Re \approx 20$ (tiny insects) to $Re \approx 10^5$ (large birds), spanning a gap in classical aerodynamic knowledge

Biological Observations

  • Hummingbird experiments by Chai et al. showed that wingbeat amplitude (not frequency) is the primary mechanism for increasing lift
  • Hummingbird hovering exhibits highly symmetrical wing motions with downstroke-to-upstroke muscle mass ratio of approximately 2:1 (vs 10:1 for other birds)
  • Spedding's wake visualization of kestrel flight at $k \approx 0.27$ revealed undulating tip vortex pairs with estimated $C_L \approx 1.2$ at $Re = 4 \times 10^4$
  • Weis-Fogh concluded no animal heavier than approximately 100 g can hover continuously

Analytical Foundations

  • Walker's theory (1925) provides quasi-steady flapping analysis valid for low reduced frequencies ($k_W < 1$)
  • Osborne's theory is the most complete quasi-steady formulation, successfully explaining insect flight including induced flow effects
  • Rayner's vortex ring model describes wakes as stacks of coaxial vortex rings (circular in hover, elliptic in forward flight)
  • The present UVLM is developed to go beyond quasi-steady limitations and capture high reduced frequency flapping aerodynamics

Materials and Methods

UVLM Formulation

  • Wing surface discretized into rectilinear vortex ring elements; each ring composed of four vortex filaments with circulation $\Gamma$
  • Induced velocity at any point computed via the Biot-Savart law applied to each finite-length vortex filament
  • Zero normal-flow boundary condition enforced at each panel control point, yielding a linear system: $$\sum_{n=1}^{N} A_{Kn} \Gamma_n = -\left[\mathbf{n} \cdot (U, V, W) + \mathbf{n} \cdot (u, v, w)_W\right]_K$$
  • Aerodynamic loads computed from the unsteady Bernoulli equation; pressure jump across panel $ij$: $$\Delta p_{ij} = \rho \left[\mathbf{Q} \cdot \boldsymbol{\tau}_i \frac{\Gamma_{i,j} - \Gamma_{i-1,j}}{c_{ij}} + \mathbf{Q} \cdot \boldsymbol{\tau}_j \frac{\Gamma_{i,j} - \Gamma_{i,j-1}}{b_{ij}} + \frac{\partial \Gamma_{ij}}{\partial t}\right]$$
  • The $\partial\Gamma/\partial t$ term is critical for accurate high-reduced-frequency flapping simulation (unsteady contribution)

Wake Model

  • Free-wake relaxation: shed vortex rings convected by local velocity (sum of all ring-induced velocities)
  • Wake aging (dissipation): circulation decays with parameter $K_{\text{decay}}$; value of 60 reduces oldest ring circulation by ~30%
  • Vortex stretching: filament strength modified as wake elements deform
  • Partial wake option: user selects number of wake rows $p_w$ for load calculations, reducing cost from $O(N^3)$ to manageable levels
  • Old wake rows can be frozen (not convected) or removed entirely to save memory and CPU time

Object-Oriented Implementation

  • Written in C++ with class hierarchy: Filament → Ring → Lattice → Wing/Wake
  • Encapsulation ensures each filament tracks its own properties; rings contain filaments plus normal vectors
  • Linear system solved via LU decomposition or conjugate gradient (CG) method with optional MPI parallelization
  • Richardson extrapolation applied using 5- and 10-chordwise-ring runs to improve moment coefficient accuracy

Kinematic Model

  • Dynamic twist: $\alpha = \alpha_{\text{base}} - \alpha_0 (r/R) \sin(\omega t)$, where $r/R$ is spanwise fraction
  • Flap angle: $\beta = \beta_0 \cos(\omega t)$, out of phase with pitch to avoid separation
  • Pigeon-like parameters: $V_\infty = 11$ m/s, span $b = 0.89$ m, chord $c = 0.08$ m, NACA 83XX cambered airfoil

Results

Validation: Plunging and Pitching

  • Impulsive start from rest: lift values including initial transient agree with Katz and Plotkin reference solutions
  • Plunging oscillation ($h_0 = 1$ in.): peak $C_l$ and phase shift match Halfman's experimental data and Theodorsen's theory across reduced frequencies $k = 0.05$–$0.4$
  • Pitching at $\alpha_0 = 6.7°$: good agreement with experiments for both lift amplitude and phase shift
  • Pitching at $\alpha_0 = 13.5°$: results match theory; slight deviation from experiment at higher $k$ attributed to viscous effects
  • Moment coefficient $C_M$ about 37% chord: agreement with both experimental and theoretical values after Richardson extrapolation

Flapping Validation

  • Comparison to Walker's theory for cambered wing ($A = 8$): good agreement for $k_W < 1$
  • Steady $C_L$ verified against Xfoil: VLM gives $C_L = 0.660$ vs Xfoil inviscid corrected $C_L = 0.686$ (NACA 8306)
  • At $k_W = 0.08$, unsteady $\partial\Gamma/\partial t$ contribution is approximately zero; at $k_W = 1.0$ it contributes nearly as much as the steady part
  • Weis-Fogh and Jensen criterion confirmed: flapping contributes to lift only when $k_W > 0.66$

Parametric Survey: Flapping + Twist

  • Varying twist angle $\alpha_0$ ($\omega = 25$ rad/s, $\beta_0 = 45°$): average $C_L$ and thrust increase with $\alpha_0$ up to optimum before effective angle of attack causes unrealistic conditions
  • Varying flap angle $\beta_0$ ($\omega = 25$ rad/s, $\alpha_0 = 41°$): negative thrust at low $\beta_0$ due to excessive twist relative to flap velocity
  • Varying frequency $\omega$ ($\beta_0 = 45°$, $\alpha_0 = 41°$): downstroke thrust increases steeply with $\omega$; for $\omega < 30$ rad/s both upstroke and downstroke produce positive lift
  • Wake qualitatively matches Spedding's kestrel wake visualization at $k \approx 0.19$–$0.27$

Computational Efficiency

  • Partial wake ($p_w$) reduces wake velocity calculation time by 49% over last 160 time steps while $C_L$ difference < 0.5%
  • Wake aging with $K_{\text{decay}} = 60$ reduces tip vortex roll-up intensity, improving physical realism

Results Interpretation

  • The $\partial\Gamma/\partial t$ term in the unsteady Bernoulli equation is the primary mechanism distinguishing UVLM from quasi-steady methods; its dominance at $k_W > 1$ explains why Walker's theory fails at high reduced frequencies
  • The asymmetry between upstroke and downstroke forces arises from the combination of cambered airfoil geometry and the base pitch angle $\alpha_{\text{base}}$, mimicking biological mechanisms where birds reduce upstroke effort
  • Dynamic twist is essential for high-frequency flapping to prevent massive flow separation at the wing tips, where effective angle of attack is largest due to the vector sum of freestream and flapping velocities
  • The qualitative agreement between computed wakes and Spedding's stereophotogrammetry validates the free-wake model's ability to capture undulating tip vortex structures characteristic of avian flight
  • Results at low $\omega$ showing positive lift on both strokes support biological observations that high-speed, low-$k$ flight produces lift throughout the entire cycle, whereas low-speed flight concentrates lift in the downstroke
  • The partial wake approximation demonstrates that computational cost can be reduced significantly without sacrificing aerodynamic accuracy, enabling longer simulations approaching hovering regimes
  • Object-oriented C++ architecture with class inheritance (Filament → Ring → Lattice) makes the code extensible for multi-surface configurations (tail, body) and parallelization
  • The VLM inherently assumes inviscid, attached flow; results at high effective angles of attack (large $\alpha_0$ with small $\beta_0$) may indicate conditions where flow separation invalidates the potential flow assumption

Conclusion

  • An object-oriented UVLM code in C++ has been developed and validated for simulating plunging, pitching, twisting, and flapping motions of finite-aspect-ratio wings
  • Lift and phase shift for oscillating airfoils agree with Theodorsen's theory and Halfman's experimental data
  • Flapping lift agrees with Walker's quasi-steady theory at low reduced frequencies ($k_W < 1$) and correctly predicts departure from quasi-steady behavior at higher $k_W$
  • Free-wake relaxation with wake aging and stretching improves physical accuracy; partial wake option provides 49% CPU time reduction with < 0.5% lift error
  • Parametric survey of flapping + dynamic twist reveals the interplay between $\alpha_0$, $\beta_0$, and $\omega$ in generating net positive lift and thrust over the flapping cycle
  • Wake structures qualitatively match experimental flow visualization from kestrel flight studies
  • Object-oriented design enables easy extension to multi-body configurations; MPI-parallel CG solver developed for scalability
  • Future work targets hovering flight simulation over many wingbeat cycles through further parallelization
