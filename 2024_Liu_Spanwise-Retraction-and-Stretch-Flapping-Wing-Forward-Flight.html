Overview

This paper presents a CFD-based numerical investigation of dynamical spanwise retraction and stretch, a bio-inspired morphing mode observed in birds and bats, on flapping-wing aerodynamic performance in forward flight. Using 3D RANS simulations with the SU2 solver at $Re = 1.5 \times 10^5$ and $St = 0.3$, the study demonstrates that this morphing mode yields absolute aerodynamic advantages: simultaneously increased lift, increased thrust, and reduced power consumption compared to non-morphing baselines. Three desirable scenarios are confirmed for plunging wings over a range of mean angles of attack, and these advantages are further validated on a more complex two-section flapping wing model with local sweep-based retraction. The mechanism analysis reveals that asymmetric flow acceleration and reduced wing area in the upstroke are key drivers.

Introduction

Motivation

  • Flapping-wing propulsion is increasingly attractive for micro aerial vehicles, yet man-made ornithopters still fall short of natural fliers' capacities due to low-Reynolds-number performance deterioration
  • Birds and bats dynamically retract and stretch their wings during each flap across a wide range of forward flight speeds, suggesting beneficial aerodynamic mechanisms shaped by millions of years of evolution
  • Understanding these bionic morphing behaviors could provide critical design inspiration for improving artificial flapping-wing vehicles

State of the Art

  • Wing morphing in natural fliers is categorized into three types: insects (passive flexible deformation), birds (active planform reconfiguration with significant span variation), and bats (complex retraction-stretch kinematics)
  • Previous studies consistently showed that spanwise retraction and stretch improves lift generation and power economy but is detrimental to thrust generation for flapping/plunging wings
  • Prior work deduced that this morphing mode is more suitable for low-speed flights, contradicting observations that birds use spanwise retraction across nearly all forward flight speeds

Gap & Contribution

  • No previous study has evaluated whether spanwise retraction and stretch can achieve absolute advantages in all three fundamental aspects (lift, thrust, power) simultaneously
  • This paper introduces an associated analysis methodology: rather than comparing at fixed angle of attack, it compares performance at matched lift or matched thrust conditions
  • First study to reveal that the bionic morphing achieves advantages without detriment in any aspect, explaining why birds employ it across all flight speeds

Materials and Methods

Problem Setup

  • Primary model: finite rectangular wing with aspect ratio $AR = 6$, NACA0012 cross section, chord $c = 0.2\,\text{m}$, span $s_0 = 1.2\,\text{m}$
  • Basic motion: cosinusoidal plunging $h(t) = h_0 \cos(\Phi)$ where $\Phi = 2\pi f t$ and plunging amplitude $h_0 = c$
  • Morphing defined as: full span in downstroke; cosine-profile retraction in upstroke with peak span loss percentage $M$ (tested $M = 0, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5$)
  • Secondary model: two-section wing with GOE803 airfoil, $s_0 = 0.6\,\text{m}$, section ratio $L_1:L_2 = 2:3$, flapping angle $\psi_0 = 31°$, local sweep angles for retraction
  • Mean angle of attack varied from $0°$ to $12°$ to achieve lift-thrust interconversion

Governing Equations

  • 3D Reynolds-averaged incompressible Navier-Stokes equations: $\frac{\partial \bar{u}_i}{\partial x_i} = 0$ and $\frac{\partial \bar{u}_i}{\partial t} + \bar{u}_j \frac{\partial \bar{u}_i}{\partial x_j} = -\frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\partial \bar{p}}{\partial x_i} + \frac{\partial}{\partial x_j}\left(\nu \frac{\partial \bar{u}_i}{\partial x_j} - \bar{\sigma}_{ij}\right)$
  • Turbulence closure: Spalart-Allmaras (S-A) one-equation model
  • Non-dimensional aerodynamic coefficients: $L_{\text{non}} = \frac{L}{q_\infty \cdot A_0}$, $T_{\text{non}} = \frac{T}{q_\infty \cdot A_0}$, $P_{\text{non}} = \frac{P}{q_\infty \cdot A_0 \cdot u_\infty}$ where $q_\infty = \frac{1}{2}\rho u_\infty^2$
  • Reynolds number $Re = \frac{\rho_\infty u_\infty c}{\mu_\infty} = 1.5 \times 10^5$; Strouhal number $St = \frac{2fh_0}{u_\infty} = 0.3$

Numerical Approach

  • Solver: SU2 V7.0.7 (Stanford University Unstructured), open-source CFD, finite volume method framework
  • Spatial discretization: second-order Jameson-Schmidt-Turkel (JST) scheme
  • Temporal discretization: second-order dual-time-stepping method with $\Delta t = T/200$ per cycle
  • Deforming dynamic mesh for plunging and morphing motions
  • Hybrid unstructured grid: approximately 4.14 million volume cells (Medium grid) with first layer $< 1 \times 10^{-4}c$ ensuring $y^+ < 1$
  • Farfield boundary placed approximately $300c$ from the wing surface
  • Standard sea-level conditions: $\rho_\infty = 1.225\,\text{kg/m}^3$, $\mu_\infty = 1.789\,\text{kg/(m \cdot s)}$

Validation Strategy

  • Case 1: Rigid finite-span wing in plunging motion at $Re = 3 \times 10^4$ (benchmark by Heathcote et al., 2008). Thrust coefficient matched well with Liu (2009) and Lang et al. (2022)
  • Case 2: Deformable flapping-twisting wing at $Re = 1 \times 10^6$, $AR = 4$, NACA0014. Lift coefficient showed good agreement with Lin and Hu (2005) and Chang et al. (2019)
  • Grid convergence study: Coarse/Medium/Fine grids ($\sqrt[3]{2}$ refinement ratio); Medium grid closely matched Fine grid results
  • Time-step convergence: $\Delta t = T/100$, $T/200$, $T/400$ tested; $T/200$ nearly coincided with $T/400$

Results

Time-Averaged Properties (Plunging Wing)

  • At fixed AOA, morphing wings produce more lift and consume less power but generate less thrust than baseline, consistent with prior literature
  • Associated analysis reveals three absolutely desirable scenarios: (A) more lift + less power at given thrust, (B) more thrust + less power at given lift, (C) more lift + more thrust + less power simultaneously
  • Scenario A example ($M = 0.5$): at matched thrust $\bar{T}_{\text{non}} \approx 0.163$, morphing wing produces 15.27% more lift with 17.33% less power
  • Scenario B example ($M = 0.5$): at matched lift $\bar{L}_{\text{non}} \approx 0.646$, morphing wing generates 6.05% more thrust with 20.27% less power
  • Scenario C example ($M = 0.5$): morphing wing produces 6.93% more lift and 15.27% more thrust while consuming 16.86% less power than baseline
  • All desirable trends are positively associated with morphing amplitude $M$

Phase-Averaged Properties

  • Morphing impact is concentrated in the upstroke; downstroke discrepancies are marginal
  • Morphing wing generates less lift in downstroke but compensates with more lift in upstroke; thrust situation is reversed
  • Power consumption reduced in both strokes, with upstroke dominating the power economy
  • Periodic lift and thrust load fluctuations are alleviated by morphing, implying improved stability and steadiness

Mechanism Analysis (Flow Visualization)

  • LEV and WTV (Wing-Tip Vortices) are the primary flow structures; morphing significantly alters their distribution in the upstroke
  • Morphing wing's LEV span length is nearly halved in upstroke despite slightly increased local LEV strength
  • WTVs of morphing wing undergo a pull-and-push process during retraction-stretch, becoming more three-dimensional and weakened
  • Quantitative circulation analysis at $t/T = 0.25$: morphing wing LEV circulation $\Gamma/(u_\infty c) = 1.91$ vs baseline $2.46$; WTV circulation $1.28$ vs $1.49$
  • Vortex rings in wake are weaker and dissipate faster for morphing wings, indicating less energy consumption
  • Morphing provides a new form of asymmetric flow acceleration enabling net lift generation even at $\text{AOA} = 0°$, reducing reliance on inclined inflow and associated drag penalty

Two-Section Flapping Wing Validation

  • GOE803 airfoil, two-section wing ($L_1:L_2 = 2:3$) with local sweep-based retraction at $Re = 1.5 \times 10^5$, $St = 0.3$
  • All three absolute advantage scenarios confirmed on the more complex model, demonstrating robustness of the bionic concept
  • Phase-averaged profiles closely mirror the plunging wing patterns, with upstroke-dominated power economy
  • Morphing wings at minimum span ratio $s_{\min}/s_0 = 0.7$ show strongest advantages, consistent with amplitude-dependent trend

Results Interpretation

Physical Mechanisms

  • The key insight is the associated analysis approach: previous studies compared at fixed AOA, showing mixed results; comparing at matched lift or thrust reveals unambiguous advantages
  • Morphing wings achieve equivalent or superior performance at lower AOA than baseline, which reduces body drag and further benefits overall flight performance
  • The asymmetric area/span between downstroke and upstroke provides a fundamentally new lift generation mechanism distinct from the traditional inclined-inflow approach
  • Reduced wing area in upstroke simultaneously decreases both negative lift and drag resistance, explaining the coupled benefits in lift, thrust, and power

Comparison with Prior Work

  • Results at fixed AOA are fully consistent with Guan and Yu (2015), Wang et al. (2014, 2015), and Bahlman et al. (2013, 2014): more lift, less thrust, less power
  • The novel associated analysis resolves the long-standing paradox of why birds use retraction-stretch at all flight speeds despite the apparent thrust penalty at fixed AOA
  • Flow visualization confirms the LEV manipulation mechanism reported by Wang et al. (2014, 2015) while adding quantitative circulation comparisons

Limitations

  • Simplified rectangular planform and sinusoidal kinematics do not fully represent real bird/bat wing complexity
  • No chordwise twist or pitching motion is included, which generally contributes positively to thrust generation in real fliers
  • Turbulence model (S-A) may not capture all transitional flow phenomena at $Re = 1.5 \times 10^5$
  • Two-dimensional morphing (spanwise only) neglects coupled chordwise camber changes observed in natural wings

Conclusion
  • Dynamical spanwise retraction and stretch yields absolute aerodynamic advantages for flapping wings in forward flight: (1) more lift + less power at given thrust, (2) more thrust + less power at given lift, (3) more lift + more thrust + less power simultaneously
  • These advantages are positively correlated with morphing amplitude ($M$ up to 0.5) and hold across the tested AOA range ($0°$-$12°$)
  • The bionic morphing provides a new asymmetric flow acceleration mechanism for net lift generation, reducing dependence on inclined inflow and associated drag penalties
  • Periodic aerodynamic load fluctuations are alleviated, suggesting improved stability and steadiness for flapping-wing fliers
  • All advantages confirmed on a more complex two-section flapping wing with GOE803 airfoil, demonstrating robustness of the concept for practical ornithopter design
  • The study provides the first evidence that the bionic spanwise morphing is affirmatively desirable across all fundamental performance aspects, supporting its adoption in artificial flapping-wing vehicles
  • Future work should incorporate chordwise morphing, pitching kinematics, and experimental validation to further assess the concept's applicability
