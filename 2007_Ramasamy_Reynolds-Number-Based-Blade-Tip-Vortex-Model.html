Overview

This paper develops a mathematical model for predicting the temporal growth and induced velocity distribution of helicopter blade tip vortices as a function of vortex Reynolds number $Re_v = \Gamma_v / \nu$. The model combines a vortex filament strain model with a transitional flow model that accounts for rotational stratification (Richardson number) effects on the eddy viscosity distribution within the vortex core. Unlike classical models (Lamb-Oseen, Squire, Iversen) that assume either fully laminar or fully turbulent interiors, this model captures the multi-region structure: laminar core, transitional zone, and turbulent outer region. A parsimonious exponential series approximation provides the swirl velocity profile at low computational cost for use in free-vortex wake analyses. Predictions are validated against tip vortex measurements from model-scale and full-scale rotors.

Introduction

Motivation

  • Blade-vortex interaction (BVI) is a primary source of noise and vibration on helicopter rotors, requiring high-fidelity tip vortex models for accurate airload predictions
  • Temporal development of tip vortices (core growth, swirl velocity distribution) is governed by turbulent flow structures inside the vortex core
  • Existing models assume either fully laminar (Lamb-Oseen) or fully turbulent (Squire, Iversen) interiors, which is inconsistent with experimental observations

State of the Art

  • The Lamb-Oseen model provides an exact laminar solution to the 1D incompressible Navier-Stokes equations with core growth $r_c = \sqrt{4\alpha_L \nu t}$, but substantially underpredicts measured growth rates
  • Squire's model adds a uniform eddy viscosity parameter $\delta = 1 + a_1 Re_v$, but assumes radially uniform turbulence and produces a Reynolds-number-independent velocity profile
  • Iversen's model hypothesizes linearly varying eddy viscosity with radial distance but overpredicts core growth by assuming fully turbulent flow throughout
  • Semi-empirical models by Tung et al. and Hoffman and Joubert recognized multi-region vortex structure but were not generalizable to arbitrary vortex Reynolds numbers
  • Prior strain models by Ananthan and Leishman addressed filament stretching effects on core properties using conservation of mass and momentum

Gap

  • No existing model simultaneously accounts for vortex filament strain and rotational stratification effects on turbulence as a function of vortex Reynolds number
  • Dimensional scaling between full-scale and model-scale rotors spans several orders of magnitude in $Re_v$, but prior models do not capture this scaling
  • The constant $a_1$ in Squire's model lies within a broad range ($5 \times 10^{-5}$ to $4 \times 10^{-4}$), requiring more precise determination for accurate airload, vibration, and noise predictions

Related Works

Classical Vortex Models

  • Lamb (1932) and Oseen (1912) derived the foundational laminar vortex solution; singularity at $t=0$ with infinite kinetic energy is physically unrealistic
  • Squire (1965) introduced turbulent eddy viscosity as a function of circulation and kinematic viscosity, enabling Reynolds-number-dependent core growth
  • Iversen (1976) correlated turbulent trailing vortex decay data assuming fully turbulent interior flow

Multi-Region Vortex Structure

  • Tung et al. first hypothesized the three-region vortex structure: inner laminar, transitional, and outer turbulent regions
  • Ramasamy and Leishman (Refs. 11, 13) confirmed multi-region structure through 3-component LDV measurements and flow visualization on model-scale rotors
  • Cotel and Breidenthal (1999) proposed the stratification threshold $Ri = Re_v^{1/4}$ below which turbulence can develop inside the vortex

Strain and Stretching Effects

  • Ananthan and Leishman (2004) developed a filament strain model using conservation of mass and momentum, validated with measurements in known strain fields
  • Bhagwat and Leishman (2000, 2002) correlated helicopter tip vortex measurements and developed generalized viscous vortex core models for free-wake applications

Full-Scale and Forward Flight Measurements

  • Cook (1972) made hot-wire measurements on a full-scale S-58 rotor; Boatwright measured the wake of a hovering full-scale OH-23B rotor
  • HART II (Ref. 22) provided 3-component PIV measurements at $Re_v$ representative of larger-scale rotors in forward flight ($\mu_R = 0.15$)

Materials and Methods

Vortex Strain Model

  • Filament stretching model from Ananthan and Leishman quantifies the effect of positive/negative strain on core vorticity: positive strain reduces core size while increasing vorticity
  • Conservation of mass and momentum yields core radius under strain: $r_c = \sqrt{r_0^2 + 4\alpha_L \nu \delta \int_{\zeta_0}^{\zeta} (1+\varepsilon)^{-1} d\zeta}$

Transitional Vortex Model

  • Richardson number defined as the ratio of turbulence produced/consumed by centrifugal forces to turbulence produced by shear: $Ri = \frac{2V_\theta}{r^2} \frac{\partial(V_\theta r)}{\partial r} \Big/ \left(r \frac{\partial(V_\theta/r)}{\partial r}\right)^2$
  • Vortex Intermittency Function (VIF) models the smooth transition from laminar core to turbulent outer region: $\text{VIF} = \frac{1}{2}\left(1 + \text{erf}\left(b\left(\frac{\sqrt{\bar{\eta}}}{\bar{\eta}_1} - \eta_a\right)\right)\right)$
  • Eddy viscosity incorporates VIF and the R-L empirical constant $\alpha_{RL} = 0.0655$: $\nu_t = \text{VIF} \cdot \alpha_{RL}^2 \cdot r^2 \left|\frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial r}\right|$
  • Governing momentum equation for axisymmetric vortex flow with variable total viscosity $\nu_T = \nu + \nu_t$: $\frac{\partial \gamma}{\partial t} = r\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(\nu_T r \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(\frac{\gamma}{r^2}\right)\right) + 2\nu_T \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(\frac{\gamma}{r^2}\right)$

Combined Reynolds-Number-Based Model

  • The viscosity ratio $\delta$ is derived from the transitional model: $\delta = \frac{Re_v \alpha_{RL}^2}{2\pi \alpha_L}\left(\frac{\Gamma_{v,\text{Lamb}}}{V_1}\right)^2$
  • Unique average value $a_1 = 6.5 \times 10^{-5}$ determined by comparing transitional model $\delta$ with Squire's uniform eddy viscosity formulation
  • Final core growth model: $r_c = \sqrt{r_0^2 + 4\alpha_L \nu (1 + a_1 Re_v) \int_{\zeta_0}^{\zeta} (1+\varepsilon)^{-1} d\zeta}$ where $\zeta_0 \approx 30°$

Swirl Velocity Approximation

  • Parsimonious exponential series: $V_\theta = \frac{\Gamma_v}{2\pi r}\left(1 - \sum_{n=1}^{3} a_n \exp(-b_n r)\right)$ with coefficients tabulated for $Re_v$ from 1 to $10^6$

Results

Core Growth Validation

  • Core growth predictions validated against measurements from 6 experimental datasets spanning model-scale to full-scale rotors (Table 1)
  • Predicted $\delta$ correlates well with measurements across $Re_v$ from sub-scale ($Re_v \sim 10^4$) to full-scale ($Re_v \sim 10^6$) experiments
  • The transitional model $\delta$ lies within the range suggested by Bhagwat and Leishman at all vortex Reynolds numbers

Swirl Velocity Profiles

  • Exponential series approximation accurately reproduces the numerically predicted velocity profiles at $Re_v = 10^2$, $4.8 \times 10^4$, and $10^6$
  • At $Re_v = 100$, the profile is essentially identical to the Lamb-Oseen model ($a_1 = 1.0$, $b_1 = 1.2515$)
  • At $Re_v = 10^6$, pronounced deviation from Lamb-Oseen with three-term exponential series required ($a_1 = 0.1231$, $a_2 = 0.7667$)

Strain Effects

  • At $Re_v = 5 \times 10^5$, positive filament strain rate reduces core growth, while negative strain rate enhances core growth
  • Model predictions in a known strain field (from Ref. 11 measurements at $Re_v = 48{,}000$) showed the core size reduction at older wake ages coinciding with regions of significant strain
  • Substituting effective $Re_v \approx 1.2 \times 10^5$ (matching actual growth rate) produces good correlation with measurements
  • Forward flight measurements by Mahalingam and Komerath showed higher core growth at young wake ages than expected, attributed to velocity gradients in the rotor wake

Results Interpretation

  • The multi-region vortex concept (laminar core, transition, turbulent outer region) is essential for accurate modeling: neither fully laminar nor fully turbulent assumptions are sufficient
  • The interdependence of strain and diffusion means that core growth measurements in forward flight cannot be directly compared to hover predictions without strain correction
  • Squire's model with a single empirical constant $a_1 = 6.5 \times 10^{-5}$ can reproduce the transitional model's core growth behavior, but cannot capture the Reynolds-number-dependent velocity profile
  • The Iversen model significantly overpredicts core growth compared to both the transitional model and measurements, due to its fully turbulent interior assumption
  • Full-scale rotor measurements (Cook, Boatwright) are scarce and subject to environmental noise, making laboratory-scale validation with known $Re_v$ essential
  • The model's underprediction at $Re_v = 48{,}000$ is attributed to empirical constants derived from a wide range of Reynolds numbers; using an effective $Re_v$ resolves this discrepancy
  • Core size changes with both the sign and magnitude of vortex filament strain and with vortex Reynolds number, highlighting the need for coupled strain-diffusion modeling
  • The exponential series approximation provides a computationally efficient alternative to repeatedly solving the governing differential equation, critical for free-vortex wake analyses
  • Implications extend to aeroacoustic predictions: accurate velocity profiles at appropriate $Re_v$ are necessary for predicting BVI noise levels

Conclusion

  • A vortex Reynolds number dependent model was successfully developed and validated, combining filament strain and rotational stratification (Richardson number) effects on turbulence
  • Vortex filament strain and diffusion are interdependent: negative strain enhances growth even at low $Re_v$, while positive strain can reduce growth despite high $Re_v$
  • Core growth rate increases with $Re_v$ for $Re_v > 1{,}000$ due to increased turbulence; at lower $Re_v$, the model reduces to the laminar Lamb-Oseen model with finite initial core radius
  • The parsimonious exponential series captures Reynolds-number-dependent swirl velocity profiles at low computational cost, reducing to Lamb-Oseen at low $Re_v$
  • Strain effects can favor, balance, or counter diffusion depending on the nature and magnitude of stretching
  • Forward flight vortex measurements must be corrected for filament strain effects to properly validate general-purpose vortex models
  • The model enables improved predictions of unsteady airloads, vibration, and noise on helicopter rotors across the full range of geometric scales
