Overview

This paper presents the design, fabrication, and comparative analysis of two bird-inspired wing mechanisms based on a crank-rocker four-bar linkage: a Flapping Motion (FM) mechanism and a simultaneous Flapping and Folding Motion (FFM) mechanism. The authors derive kinematic relationships, formulate an optimization problem to maximize the flapping angle (total range of motion (ROM)), and fabricate prototypes using MDF frames with Esaki tissue surfaces. The FFM mechanism achieves folding without an additional actuator by exploiting coupled inner/outer spar kinematics. Force measurements at motor inputs from 44% to 62% demonstrate that FFM produces both higher average lift and higher wing frequency than FM, confirming the aerodynamic benefit of incorporating folding motion.
2

Introduction

Context and Motivation

  • Flapping-wing platforms offer advantages at bird scale: better maneuverability, lower noise, reduced power cost, and biomimetic concealment compared to fixed- and rotary-wing aircraft.
  • Bird main wing motion during strokes is comprised of flapping, folding, bending, and twisting. This work focuses on the first two: flapping and folding.
  • The folding motion can be implemented by wing spar movement alone without additional actuators, helping meet strict Size, weight, and power (SWaP) constraints.

Research Gap

  • Most prior FM mechanisms used heuristic link length selection or commercial platforms without systematic kinematic optimization.
  • Existing FFM studies lacked quantitative force measurement to validate the benefit of folding motion.
  • This paper combines systematic kinematic optimization with comparative force measurement experiments for both FM and FFM.

Related Works

Flapping Mechanism Studies

  • Rongfa et al. (2016) — Motion capture reconstruction of 3D flapping kinematics on a commercial platform; limited design applicability.
  • Mazaheri et al. (2011) — Force measurements vs. wing frequency, angle of attack (AOA), and wind tunnel speed on a commercial ornithopter.
  • Liang et al. (2017), Lin et al. (2005), and Folkertsma et al. (2017, Robird) — Designed main wings for trajectory analysis and force measurement, but without detailed link length optimization.

Folding Wing Studies

  • Yun (2017) and Jiang et al. (2016) — Considered FFM in wing mechanisms but did not include force measurements validation.
  • Kim et al. (2018) — Articulated ornithopter capable of FFM but without link parameter optimization.
  • Mueller et al. (2009, 2010) — Compliant wing designs where folding arises passively from flexible surface inertia.
  • Widhiarini et al. (2016) — Bird-mimetic wing system with autonomous flight control but compliant (not active spar) folding.

Theoretical Foundations

  • Djojodihardjo et al. (2016) and Lentink et al. (2009) — Lift is proportional to flapping angle, motivating ROM maximization.
  • Gerdes et al. (2012) — Review of bird-inspired flapping-wing MAV designs identifying key mechanism challenges.

Materials and Methods

Four-Bar Linkage Mechanism

  • Both mechanisms use a crank-rocker four-bar linkage — the simplest closed chain converting rotary to oscillatory motion. The crank rotates fully while the rocker (wing spar) undergoes bounded oscillation.
  • FM mechanism: single wing spar toward wingspan direction. Flapping angle is a function of crank angle and four link lengths.
  • FFM mechanism: wing spar split into Top Inner Spar (TIS), Down Inner Spar (DIS), and outer spar connected through a folding joint with fixed $35^\circ$ offset angle.

Optimization

  • Objective: maximize the total range of wing angle subject to Grashof constraints and spur gear radius constraints, solved with MATLAB.
  • Safety Margin (SM) parameter prevents dead points from fabrication imperfections. Five SM values (0.5--4 mm) were evaluated; SM = 2 mm selected as practical compromise yielding ROM = $80.07^\circ$.
  • Optimized link lengths: with crank length $l_1 = 7\,\text{mm}$ and spur gear radius $r_{spur} = 11.17\,\text{mm}$: $l_2 = 11.17\,\text{mm}$, $l_3 = 11\,\text{mm}$, $l_4 = 13.17\,\text{mm}$.

Fabrication and Gear Design

  • Wing frames: MDF (3 mm), laser-cut (ILS12.75, 0.1 mm precision). Four airfoils per wing (8 total), Esaki tissue covering, carbon rod reinforcement (2 mm diameter).
  • Gear box: 10-teeth motor gear, 54-teeth spur gear, 9/54 compound gear (0.4 module, 20° pressure angle). BLDC motor (AP-05-5000 KV) at 3.7 V, ideal output frequency $5.9\,\text{Hz}$.
  • Ball bearings at link joints and gear shafts; 1.5 mm metal rod for joint shafts.

Experimental Setup

  • Force sensor: Futek LSB200 at 600 Hz sampling rate, 10 s per test. Mechanisms mounted on 3D-printed mounts fixed to aluminum board.
  • Motor input range: 44% to 62% at 3% intervals (7 datasets per mechanism). Arduino-Uno generating PWM signals via MX-3A ESC.
  • Low-pass filter at 10 Hz cutoff for noise removal; second-order Fourier series fitting for periodic characterization.

Results

Simulation Validation

  • FM: flapping angle range $-52.08^\circ$ to $27.99^\circ$ (ROM = $80.07^\circ$); arc-shaped wing tip trajectory.
  • FFM: folding angle range $84.24^\circ$ to $178.8^\circ$ (ROM = $94.56^\circ$); bowling pin-shaped wing tip trajectory.
  • LINKAGE software simulation trajectories identical to analytical reconstruction, validating kinematic formulation. No dead points observed.

Force Measurement Comparison

  • At 62% input: FM wing frequency = 3.24 Hz, FFM = 3.74 Hz. Both > 30% below ideal 5.9 Hz due to component loading and rolling friction.
  • FM: mass amplitude grows with input but average mass shows negligible increase — upstroke drag negates downstroke gains.
  • FFM: both amplitude and average mass increase proportionally with input — folding reduces upstroke drag by decreasing horizontal wing area.

Key Quantitative Advantages of FFM

  • Shorter wing stroke period (higher frequency) despite more mechanical components.
  • Larger average lift force during each wing stroke across all motor input levels.

Results Interpretation

Mechanism Design Insights

  • The Safety Margin concept bridges theoretical optimization and practical fabrication: SM = 0.5 mm gives highest ROM ($122.35^\circ$) but unnatural near-90° downstroke; SM = 2 mm balances ROM and biological plausibility.
  • Passive folding (no extra actuator) satisfies weight constraints critical for lightweight aerial vehicles, making FFM attractive for SWaP-constrained ornithopters.

Aerodynamic Interpretation

  • FFM's lift advantage stems from two coupled effects: reduced upstroke drag from wing area reduction, and consequent higher wing frequency from that drag reduction.
  • The proportional increase of average lift with motor input in FFM (but not FM) indicates folding provides scalable lift enhancement rather than a fixed offset.
  • The bowling pin wing tip trajectory more closely resembles natural avian kinematics than the simple arc of FM.

Limitations

  • Still-air measurements only; no wind tunnel testing separating lift from thrust components.
  • Rigid-body kinematics only — wing surface flexibility (bending, twisting) not modeled.
  • Flapping-folding timing relationship and its aerodynamic effects not investigated.

Future Directions

Mechanism Extensions

  • Incorporate bending and twisting motions into the mechanism for more complete biomimetic wing kinematics.
  • Investigate the effect of flapping-folding phase timing on lift and thrust generation.

System Integration

  • Develop a fully-assembled robotic bird integrating the optimized FFM mechanism with onboard sensing and flight control systems.
  • Conduct wind tunnel tests to characterize thrust generation and lift-thrust coupling under forward flight conditions.
