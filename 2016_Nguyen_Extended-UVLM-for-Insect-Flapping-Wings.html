Overview

This paper develops an extended unsteady vortex-lattice method (UVLM) capable of simulating insect flapping-wing aerodynamics in both hovering and forward flight. Two key extensions are incorporated into a conventional UVLM: (1) a vortex-core growth model based on the Lamb–Oseen vortex with eddy viscosity, where the core radius evolves as $r_c(t) = \sqrt{4\alpha_L \nu \delta t}$, to resolve wing–wake interaction singularities; and (2) a leading-edge suction analogy model adapted from Polhamus's delta-wing theory, computing vortex force as $F_s = \frac{\pi \eta_s \rho \Gamma_{LE}^2}{16 \Delta x_{LE} \cos\Lambda_{LE}}$, to capture LEV-induced lift augmentation. Validated against experimental data from Lua et al. (2010) at $Re = 12{,}000$ and CFD results from Aono and Liu (2006) at $Re = 6{,}300$ for hawkmoth (Manduca sexta) wing models, the method achieves good agreement in forces and pitching moment at a fraction of CFD computational cost (~14 s per cycle with 8-thread parallel computing). In forward flight at $V_\infty = 2.1$ m/s, the computed mean vertical force reaches 98% of body weight. Directly relevant to Dr. Yang's research on flapping-wing aerodynamics, multibody dynamics, and ornithopter flight mechanics at KAIST, originating from the same research group (PI: Prof. Jae-Hung Han).

Introduction

Motivation

  • Insects possess extraordinary flight characteristics — hovering in place and extreme agility — that are highly desirable for flapping micro air vehicle (MAV) design.
  • Ellington et al. (1996) identified the leading-edge vortex (LEV) as the primary high-lift mechanism in hawkmoth flight, fundamentally different from conventional fixed-wing aerodynamics.
  • Dickinson et al. (1999) confirmed three governing contributions to insect aerodynamics using a scaled robotic wing: delayed stall, rotational circulation, and wake capture effects.
  • CFD approaches solving the Navier–Stokes equations provide highly accurate flow-field solutions but incur significant computational cost when dealing with complex insect wing motions, motivating the search for efficient alternatives.

State of the Art

  • Quasi-steady models are widely used due to low cost and acceptable fidelity (validated by Sane and Dickinson, 2002), but they are semi-empirical, strip-based (ignoring spanwise flow), and unvalidated for aerodynamic moment estimation — they assume center of pressure fixed at the quarter-chord point.
  • Two-dimensional discrete vortex methods (Ansari et al., 2006) express chordwise load distribution via conformal mapping between a circle and a Joukowski-type airfoil but still perform per-strip simulations, lacking three-dimensional spanwise flow characteristics.
  • Conventional UVLMs offer intermediate cost-fidelity balance and have been applied to fixed-wing aircraft (Belotserkovskii, 1977), helicopter rotors (Padakannaya, 1974; Yoo et al., 2011), and flapping wings (Fritz and Long, 2004; Stanford and Beran, 2010; Ghommem et al., 2014).
  • Smith (1996) applied an unsteady panel method to moth wing aerodynamics but initially neglected LEV effects; a later correction improved results but only for a single flight speed of 3.36 m/s.
  • Roccia et al. (2013) modified UVLM for flapping wings in hover but encountered wing–wake interaction difficulties that complicated multi-cycle simulations.

Gap & Contribution

  • Previous UVLMs cannot handle insect hovering flight due to singularities from wing–wake collisions and lack of LEV modeling — the present work addresses both limitations simultaneously.
  • The extended UVLM incorporates vortex-core growth (Bagai and Leishman, 1996) and leading-edge suction analogy (Polhamus, 1971) into a conventional UVLM framework, enabling stable multi-cycle hovering and accurate force/moment predictions at insect-relevant Reynolds numbers ($Re \sim 6{,}000$–$12{,}000$).

Related Works

Experimental Studies

  • Ellington et al. (1996) discovered spiral LEV structures on hawkmoth wings using smoke visualization, establishing the LEV as the dominant lift mechanism at $Re \sim 6{,}000$–$12{,}000$.
  • Dickinson et al. (1999) quantified translational, rotational, and wake capture force contributions using a dynamically scaled robotic fruit fly wing.
  • Dickinson and Gotz (1993) measured unsteady aerodynamic performance of model wings at low Reynolds numbers, demonstrating LEV formation and delayed stall on translating airfoils.
  • Birch and Dickinson (2001) demonstrated spanwise flow and LEV attachment on insect wings, showing the LEV stabilization mechanism via axial spanwise flow.
  • Lua et al. (2010) measured aerodynamic forces on hovering rigid and flexible hawkmoth-like wings at $Re = 12{,}000$, providing the primary experimental benchmark for this work.
  • Willmott and Ellington (1997) measured detailed wing kinematics of hawkmoth Manduca sexta in hovering and forward flight, supplying kinematic inputs for all simulations.

Computational Methods

  • Liu et al. (1998) and Aono et al. (2008) performed Navier–Stokes CFD simulations of hawkmoth hovering; Aono and Liu (2006) provided the CFD benchmark (forces + pitching moment) at $Re = 6{,}300$.
  • Roccia et al. (2013) developed a modified UVLM for flapping wings in hover with $\alpha_c = 10°$–$20°$ but struggled with wake singularities beyond one stroke cycle.
  • Bagai and Leishman (1996) introduced vortex-core growth models for helicopter tandem/tilt-rotor free-wake analysis, adapted here for insect-scale applications.
  • Kim and Han (2014) developed a multibody approach for 6-DOF flight dynamics of Manduca sexta from the same KAIST research group, directly connected to this aerodynamic solver.

Analytical Models

  • Polhamus (1971) developed the leading-edge suction analogy for delta wings: vortex force from a separated LEV equals the suction force predicted by attached-flow potential theory, provided flow reattaches on the upper surface.
  • Sane (2003) comprehensively reviewed insect flight aerodynamics and noted the adoption of Polhamus's suction analogy for insect flapping wings by Pedersen (2003) and Traub (2004).

Materials and Methods

Problem Setup

  • Three right-handed Cartesian coordinate systems: ground-fixed $(X_G, Y_G, Z_G)$ with $Z_G$ vertical, stroke-plane-fixed $(x_{sp}, y_{sp}, z_{sp})$ inclined at stroke plane angle $\beta$ to horizontal, and wing-fixed $(x_w, y_w, z_w)$ with $y_w$ along wing span and $x_w$ parallel to chord direction.
  • Wing orientation defined by three angles via 3-1-2 rotation sequence: sweep angle $\phi$ (in stroke plane), elevation angle $\theta$ (out of stroke plane), and rotation angle $\alpha$ (pitch about wing span axis).
  • Only two wings modeled (body aerodynamics neglected), justified by Aono and Liu (2006) showing the body's contribution to total aerodynamic force is quite small. Kinematics assumed symmetric about the $X_G$–$Z_G$ plane.
  • Simple harmonic motion for convergence study: $\phi(t) = -\phi_0 \cos(2\pi f t)$ and $\alpha(t) = 90° - \alpha_0 \sin(2\pi f t)$, with $\phi_0 = 60°$, $\alpha_0 = 45°$, wing-tip distance $R = 0.3615$ m, $f = 0.1$ Hz, $S = 0.01963$ m², $\bar{c} = 0.07581$ m.
  • Real hawkmoth kinematics from Willmott and Ellington (1997) reconstructed via third-order Fourier series for hovering ($A_\phi = 111°$, $\beta = 15°$) and forward flight ($V_\infty = 2.1$ m/s, $\beta = 37.6°$, $R = 0.0521$ m, $f = 24.8$ Hz).

Governing Equations

  • No-penetration boundary condition at collocation points: $$[\mathbf{V}_\infty + \mathbf{V}_{ib}(\mathbf{r},t) + \mathbf{V}_{iw}(\mathbf{r},t) - \mathbf{V}_w(\mathbf{r},t)] \cdot \mathbf{n} = 0$$ where $\mathbf{V}_{ib}$ and $\mathbf{V}_{iw}$ are induced velocities from bound vortices and wake, $\mathbf{V}_w$ is wing velocity.
  • Biot–Savart law for velocity induced by a straight vortex segment with circulation $\Gamma(t)$: $$\mathbf{V}(\mathbf{r},t) = \frac{\Gamma}{4\pi} \frac{(|\mathbf{r}_1| + |\mathbf{r}_2|)(|\mathbf{r}_1||\mathbf{r}_2| - \mathbf{r}_1 \cdot \mathbf{r}_2)}{|\mathbf{r}_1||\mathbf{r}_2||\mathbf{r}_1 \times \mathbf{r}_2|^2} (\mathbf{r}_1 \times \mathbf{r}_2)$$
  • Pressure difference from the unsteady Bernoulli equation: $$\Delta p_i(t) = \rho \left[(\mathbf{V}_i(t) \times \boldsymbol{\gamma}_i(t)) \cdot \mathbf{n}_i + \frac{\partial \Gamma_i(t)}{\partial t}\right]$$ integrated over the entire wing surface for forces and moments.
  • Vortex-core growth model: $r_c(t) = \sqrt{4 \alpha_L \nu \delta t}$, where $\alpha_L = 1.25643$ (Lamb constant), $\nu$ is kinematic viscosity, and the eddy viscosity parameter $\delta = 1 + a_1 \frac{\Gamma}{\nu}$ with Squire's parameter $a_1$.
  • Modified Biot–Savart law with finite core radius $r_c$: denominator includes $|\mathbf{r}_2 - \mathbf{r}_1|^2 r_c^2$ term to eliminate singularity when vortex lines are in close proximity or the wing collides with its own wake.

Leading-Edge Suction Analogy

  • Surface vorticity in the leading-edge panel follows $\gamma(\xi) = \frac{A}{\xi^{1/2}}$, with constant $A$ derived from leading-edge panel circulation: $A = \frac{\Gamma_{LE}}{2c \cos\Lambda_{LE}\, \Delta\xi_{LE}^{1/2}}$.
  • Leading-edge suction force with efficiency parameter: $$F_s = \frac{\pi \eta_s \rho \Gamma_{LE}^2}{16\, \Delta x_{LE} \cos\Lambda_{LE}}$$ where $\eta_s$ is suction efficiency ($0 < \eta_s \le 1.0$) and $\Lambda_{LE}$ is the local leading-edge sweep angle.
  • Flow separation criterion: when local angle of attack $\alpha > \alpha_c = 12°$, suction force is rotated 90° to be normal to the wing surface (modeling LEV vortex lift). When $\alpha \le \alpha_c$, suction force remains parallel to the wing (attached flow).
  • Only leading-edge panel circulation is required, unlike Lan and Roskam (1972) and Smith (1996) who needed extrapolation from all chordwise panels — simplifying implementation.

Numerical Approach

  • Wing discretized into $N$ vortex ring panels with collocation points at centroids; Kutta condition enforces zero load at trailing edge with vortex sheets shed into free wake each time step.
  • Reference velocity $U_{ref} = 2R A_\phi f$, lift coefficient $C_L = \frac{2L}{\rho U_{ref}^2 S}$, Reynolds number $Re = \frac{U_{ref}\,\bar{c}}{\nu}$.
  • OpenMP parallel computing distributes independent wake node velocity calculations across threads; cost grows with time as wake segments accumulate each step.

Validation Strategy

  • Convergence analysis: four mesh types ($6 \times 12$, $12 \times 24$, $18 \times 36$, $24 \times 48$) with $n_s = 50$–$400$ time steps/cycle, benchmarked against Lua et al. (2010) at $Re = 12{,}000$.
  • Hovering: simple harmonic + real hawkmoth kinematics ($Re = 11{,}115$) vs. Lua et al. (2010) over four cycles; CFD benchmark vs. Aono and Liu (2006) at $Re = 6{,}300$.
  • Forward flight: hawkmoth F2 at $V_\infty = 2.1$ m/s, comparing mean forces against measured body weight ($0.0196$ N).

Results

Convergence Analysis

  • Both $C_{Lp}$ (potential lift) and $C_{Lv}$ (vortex lift) converge asymptotically with increasing mesh fineness and time resolution; coarser meshes converge more rapidly than finer ones (Fig. 4, Fig. 5).
  • Mesh B ($12 \times 24$ panels) with $n_s = 200$ time steps/cycle selected as optimal compromise between computational effort and fidelity.
  • Spanwise resolution study: holding chordwise panels at 12, $n_{sp} = 18$ is sufficient; coarser $n_{sp} = 12$ produces undesirable spikes in $C_{Lp}$ (Fig. 6). Final mesh resolution: $12 \times 18$.
  • Squire's parameter sensitivity: $a_1 = 10^{-1}$ prevents non-physical spikes; lower values produce spike artifacts especially in $C_{Lp}$ (Fig. 7).

Hovering Flight Validation

  • Simple harmonic hovering: without leading-edge suction analogy, UVLM $C_L$ and $C_D$ are noticeably smaller than Lua et al. (2010) experiments; with $\eta_s = 0.5$, results show marked improvement confirming the LEV's critical role in lift augmentation (Fig. 8).
  • Real hawkmoth kinematics ($A_\phi = 111°$, $Re = 11{,}115$, $\nu = 9.570 \times 10^{-7}$ m²/s): aerodynamic forces are highly periodic across four stroke cycles with good experimental agreement (Fig. 10).
  • CFD comparison at $Re = 6{,}300$ ($f = 26.1$ Hz, $\bar{c} = 0.0183$ m, $\beta = 15°$): vertical force, horizontal force, and pitching moment show very good agreement during downstroke; upstroke agreement slightly reduced due to complex wing–wake interaction (Fig. 11).

Forward Flight Validation

  • Hawkmoth F2 at $V_\infty = 2.1$ m/s ($\beta = 37.6°$, $R = 0.0521$ m, $f = 24.8$ Hz): mean vertical force $= 0.0192$ N (98% of body weight $0.0196$ N), mean horizontal force $= 0.003$ N (15% of body weight) (Fig. 13).
  • Downstroke generates most vertical force due to higher relative wing-to-air velocity; upstroke contribution minimal because wings are nearly vertical during that phase.
  • Wake pattern shows characteristic trailing vortex structure with clear downstroke/upstroke asymmetry (Fig. 14).

Computational Performance

  • 8-thread parallel: ~14 s for first cycle (vs. 46 s serial, ~$3.3\times$ speedup), 9.6 min for four cycles (vs. 40.6 min serial).
  • 28-thread parallel: four cycles in 6.0 min (vs. 40.6 min serial, ~$6.8\times$ speedup), demonstrating good scalability with thread count.

Results Interpretation
  • The vortex-core growth model with eddy viscosity ($a_1 = 0.1$) eliminates wing–wake interaction singularities that plagued all previous UVLMs for insect hovering; at $Re \sim 10{,}000$ the flow is not completely laminar, so eddy viscosity is needed to grow $r_c$ faster than the pure Lamb–Oseen model predicts.
  • The LEV is confirmed as essential for insect weight support: without the leading-edge suction analogy, computed lift is insufficient to sustain body weight — consistent with Ellington et al. (1996), Dickinson and Gotz (1993), and Liu et al. (1998).
  • The suction analogy works well during the translational phase where flow is relatively steady; during the rotational phase, unsteadiness reduces its reliability, but the UVLM's native rotational circulation prediction dominates there (Dickinson et al., 1999), compensating for the analogy's weakness.
  • Near the wing tip, the LEV detaches and is no longer reattached (Nagai et al., 2009), so the suction analogy likely overestimates forces in this region; however, the wing-tip area is small relative to total wing area, limiting impact on resultant forces.
  • The efficiency $\eta_s = 0.5$ indicates approximately half the theoretical suction force is realized, reflecting viscous losses, leading-edge shape effects, and incomplete LEV reattachment.
  • Good pitching moment agreement with Aono and Liu (2006) CFD validates center-of-pressure prediction — a significant advantage over quasi-steady strip models that assume a fixed quarter-chord loading point.
  • Upstroke–downstroke asymmetry in forward flight arises from relative velocity differences: the downstroke is the advancing phase with higher resultant velocity, generating proportionally more lift.
  • Slight upstroke disagreement (hovering, CFD comparison) is attributed to complex wing–wake interaction as the wing passes through its own shed wake from the preceding downstroke.
  • The $12 \times 18$ mesh with $n_s = 200$ represents a practical engineering compromise; finer meshes converge to the same values but with diminishing returns relative to cost increase.
  • Using only leading-edge panel information for $F_s$ avoids numerical difficulties associated with extrapolation from all chordwise panels (Lan and Roskam, Smith approaches).

Conclusion
  • The extended UVLM with vortex-core growth and leading-edge suction analogy successfully simulates hovering and forward flight aerodynamics of insect flapping wings with good agreement to both experimental and CFD benchmarks.
  • The vortex-core growth model ($a_1 = 0.1$, eddy viscosity via $\delta = 1 + a_1 \Gamma/\nu$) overcomes the wing–wake interaction problem at $Re \sim 10{,}000$ that had challenged all previous UVLM implementations.
  • LEV effects are captured through the suction analogy ($F_s \propto \Gamma_{LE}^2$); its inclusion produces noticeable lift improvement, confirming the LEV's critical importance in insect high-lift mechanisms.
  • At higher flight speeds, downstroke contribution to vertical force becomes increasingly dominant, as demonstrated for hawkmoth forward flight at $V_\infty = 2.1$ m/s.
  • Computational cost is very low — only several seconds per stroke cycle with parallel computing — making the method suitable for parametric optimization and flight dynamics studies.
  • The method shows considerable advantages over previous UVLMs and provides an efficient computational alternative to CFD for insect flapping-wing aerodynamics.
  • Future work should incorporate wing structural flexibility, as $\alpha$ varies along the span due to sectional deflection, essential for more accurate forward flight simulations.
  • The UVLM framework is naturally suited for coupling with multibody dynamics codes for 6-DOF flight dynamics and stability analyses of flapping MAVs, connecting to Kim and Han (2014) from the same research group.
