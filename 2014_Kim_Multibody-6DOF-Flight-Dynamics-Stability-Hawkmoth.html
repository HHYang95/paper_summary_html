Overview

This paper presents a multibody dynamics framework for the hawkmoth Manduca sexta that performs direct time integration of fully coupled 6-DOF nonlinear equations of motion, incorporating time-varying inertia tensors of all body segments including wings. Conventional insect flight stability analyses rely on eigenmode analysis applied to linearized time-invariant (LTI) systems, requiring three major simplifications: neglecting time-varying wing inertia, wingbeat-cycle-averaging of aerodynamics, and linearization about hovering equilibrium. The multibody approach captures within-wingbeat dynamics and cross-plane coupling effects that eigenmode analysis inherently misses. All six independent DOFs exhibit passive aerodynamic damping, with sideslip translation being the least damped translational DOF ($t_{\text{half},v} = 17.95$ strokes) and pitch rotation being the least damped rotational DOF ($t_{\text{half},q} = 2.39$ strokes). A critical finding is the asymmetric cross-coupling between longitudinal and lateral planes: lateral disturbances excite longitudinal instability within 5-6 wingbeat strokes, a phenomenon invisible to linearized models.

Introduction

Insect Flight Stability Problem

  • Flying insects exhibit remarkable maneuverability and stability that surpasses current micro air vehicles (MAVs)
  • Understanding dynamic flight stability provides quantified plant characteristics for control law identification and biologically inspired MAV design
  • Insect flight dynamics is characterized as a nonlinear time periodic (NLTP) system, making direct eigenmode analysis inapplicable without simplifying assumptions

Prior Approaches and Limitations

  • Previous eigenmode studies (Taylor and Thomas (2003); Sun et al. (2007); Zhang and Sun (2010a); Cheng and Deng (2011)) identified longitudinal and lateral modes for various insect species
  • These required linearization and wingbeat-cycle-averaging, which can omit energy transfer from high-frequency to low-frequency modes (Taha et al. (2012))
  • More direct approaches using Floquet theory or CFD-coupled simulations exist but are computationally expensive

Related Works

Eigenmode Analysis Studies

  • Sun et al. (2007) identified three longitudinal modes for the hawkmoth: one unstable oscillatory mode and two stable subsidence modes
  • Zhang and Sun (2010a) and Zhang et al. (2012) extended to lateral dynamics; different studies reported conflicting stability results due to eigenvalue sensitivity near the imaginary axis
  • Cheng and Deng (2011) performed a full 6-DOF eigenmode analysis, revealing high sensitivity to modeling assumptions

Periodic System and Direct Simulation Approaches

  • Taylor and Zbikowski (2005) constructed an NLTP model for the desert locust with Fourier-series-based instantaneous aerodynamics
  • Gao et al. (2010) used CFD-based aerodynamics with 6-DOF rigid body equations for the fruit fly
  • Wu et al. (2009) directly coupled CFD with rigid body dynamics
  • Orlowski and Girard (2011a) employed a multibody dynamics approach for a rigid-winged flapping MAV but without aerodynamic modeling

Materials and Methods

Multibody Hawkmoth Model

  • The model consists of five rigid bodies: head, thorax, abdomen (connected by fixed joints), and two wings (attached via 3-DOF revolute joints)
  • Total mass $m_{\text{total}} = 1456.33$ mg, wing span $R = 48.30$ mm, mean chord $\bar{c} = 18.09$ mm
  • Wing kinematics use three rotational DOFs: stroke positional angle $\phi(t)$, feathering angle $\alpha(t)$, deviation angle $\theta(t)$ at flapping frequency of 29.46 Hz

Aerodynamic Model

  • Blade element theory decomposes instantaneous aerodynamic forces into translational and rotational components on five aerodynamic strips per wing
  • Force components: $dF_{\text{lift},i} = \frac{1}{2}\rho V_i^2 \, dS_i \, C_L$, $dF_{\text{drag},i} = \frac{1}{2}\rho V_i^2 \, dS_i \, C_D$, $dF_{\text{rot},i} = \rho V_i C_{\text{rot}} \dot{\alpha}_i \bar{c}_i \, dS_i$
  • Aerodynamic coefficients from Usherwood and Ellington (2002) revolving-wing experiments on hawkmoth wings
  • The 6-DOF body flight states incorporated through velocity transformations to the stroke-plane frame

Simulation Framework and Analysis Methodology

  • Simulation environment uses MSC.Adams for direct time integration of the DAE system via the GSTIFF integrator with variable step size
  • Two analyses performed: (1) passive damping analysis on each independent DOF with simplified sinusoidal kinematics, and (2) natural modes of motion analysis on fully coupled 6-DOF responses
  • Hovering reference condition found by adjusting Fourier coefficients until all time-averaged velocity states satisfy the zero-drift criterion within 1% tolerance

Results

Passive Damping Characteristics

  • Half-life results in wingbeat strokes: $t_{\text{half},u} = 7.10$, $t_{\text{half},v} = 17.95$, $t_{\text{half},w}^{\text{asc}} = 7.13$, $t_{\text{half},w}^{\text{desc}} = 5.77$
  • Rotational half-lives: $t_{\text{half},p} = 0.68$, $t_{\text{half},q} = 2.39$, $t_{\text{half},r} = 0.25$
  • Sideslip translation is approximately three times less damped than forward/backward translation
  • Pitch rotation is approximately five times less damped than roll and yaw rotations
  • Yaw rotation exhibits the highest rotational damping, requiring only a quarter wingbeat stroke to halve the initial angular velocity

Longitudinal Modal Dynamics

  • Mode 1 (in-phase $\delta u_b$, $\delta q_b$): diverging oscillatory dynamics at 1.22 Hz with $t_{\text{double}} = 7.5$ strokes
  • Mode 2 (out-of-phase $\delta u_b$, $\delta q_b$): initial fast subsidence transitioning to unstable oscillation
  • Mode 3 ($\delta w_b$ alone): initial subsidence ($t_{\text{half}} = 2.5$) followed by divergence through $u_b$-$q_b$ coupling

Lateral Modal Dynamics

  • In-phase $\delta v_b$-$\delta p_b$: monotonic divergence ($t_{\text{double}} = 6.0$)
  • Out-of-phase $\delta v_b$-$\delta p_b$: stable subsidence
  • $\delta p_b$-$\delta r_b$: fast subsidence transitions to divergence through $v_b$ excitation

Results Interpretation

Cross-Plane Coupling Effects

  • Longitudinal disturbances do not affect lateral states, maintaining decoupled dynamics
  • However, lateral disturbances excite longitudinal states within 5-6 wingbeat strokes, triggering the $u_b$-$q_b$ coupled longitudinal instability
  • This asymmetric cross-coupling arises from higher-order terms neglected during linearization and has direct implications for flight control design

Limitations of Eigenmode Analysis

  • Wingbeat-cycle-averaging eliminates within-wingbeat dynamics, causing underestimation of coupling between states
  • Stability derivatives such as $N_v$ (yaw moment due to sideslip velocity) and $Y_r$ (sideslip force due to yaw rate) are underestimated
  • The nonlinear multibody approach reveals all modes eventually converge to a combined longitudinal-lateral instability pattern, invisible to linear analysis

Biological Implications

  • Instability growth timescales (less than 7 wingbeat strokes to double) compared against sensory latencies
  • Angular rate sensing via antennae: less than 0.5 stroke latency; visual orientation detection: 1-3 strokes latency
  • The hawkmoth has sufficient time to sense and correct perturbations before instability growth becomes unrecoverable

Conclusion

Principal Results

  • The multibody dynamics approach successfully captures 6-DOF flight dynamics without simplifying assumptions required by eigenmode analysis
  • All six independent DOFs possess passive aerodynamic damping, with quantitative imbalances driving coupled instabilities
  • Longitudinal instability primarily from $u_b$-$q_b$ coupling (forward velocity and pitch rate)
  • Lateral instability from $v_b$-$p_b$ coupling (sideslip velocity and roll rate)
  • The lateral-to-longitudinal cross-coupling effect is a significant finding with implications for MAV flight control design

Methodological Significance

  • Direct time integration of fully coupled nonlinear multibody equations avoids linearization artifacts and captures dynamics across both short and long time windows
  • This framework provides a more complete picture of insect flight stability than eigenmode analysis, particularly for lateral dynamics where linearized models show the greatest discrepancies
  • The approach is extensible to other insect species and flight conditions beyond hovering
