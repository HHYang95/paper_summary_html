Overview

This paper develops a novel reduced-order fluid-structure interaction (FSI) framework for flapping, flexible insect wings that accommodates arbitrary wing geometry and any fluid loading model. The framework uses Lagrangian mechanics for structural dynamics and the principle of virtual work for aerodynamic modal forces, enabling either unilateral or bilateral FSI coupling. Applied to an idealized hawkmoth (Manduca sexta) forewing, the authors compare blade element theory (BET) against RANS CFD and find that BET estimates modal forces five orders of magnitude faster than CFD with reasonable accuracy. The study reveals that inertial-elastic and aerodynamic forces are of the same order of magnitude but excite different deformation modes: inertial-elastic forces primarily drive bending while aerodynamic forces primarily drive torsion. This finding is directly relevant to Dr. Yang's ornithopter FSI research, as it provides a computationally efficient framework for parametric studies of flapping wing aeroelasticity and challenges the prevailing assumption that wing deformation is dominated solely by inertial forces.

Introduction

Motivation

  • Flapping wing micro air vehicles (FWMAVs) offer flight capability at centimeter scales and milligram weights where conventional fixed-wing and rotary aircraft suffer aerodynamic inefficiency
  • Insect wings deform under both aerodynamic and inertial-elastic forces through fluid-structure interaction (FSI), and this deformation serves critical functions including energy management and angular rate sensing
  • Flexible wings achieve higher lift-to-drag ratios than rigid counterparts and store strain energy that can be recycled to improve inertial power economy
  • Wing mechanoreceptors (campaniform sensilla) provide low-latency mechanical feedback for flight control, making understanding of FSI essential for bio-inspired sensor design

State of the Art

  • Most FSI models couple FEA with CFD, producing accurate results but at enormous computational cost unsuitable for parametric studies
  • Large wing rotations cause time-varying FEA stiffness matrices, creating DOFs on the order of $N_{\text{elements}} \times N_{\text{timesteps}}$
  • BET has been used to study insect flight aerodynamics but is conventionally applied to rigid wings and not widely used for FSI
  • Prior BET-FSI models by Wang et al. (2017) could handle torsion but not spanwise bending; Stanford et al. (2012) captured bending but with coarse discretization

Gap & Contribution

  • No existing reduced-order FSI model accommodates both arbitrary wing geometry and general fluid loading while permitting either unilateral or bilateral coupling
  • The relative contribution of aerodynamic vs inertial-elastic forces to wing deformation at moth scale was unknown, with conflicting evidence from Combes and Daniel (2003) and Norris (2013)
  • This paper provides a general FSI framework and quantifies the accuracy of BET as a low-order fluid model for flapping wing FSI

Related Works

Experimental Studies

  • Combes and Daniel (2003) flapped hawkmoth wings in air and helium with SDOF rotation and concluded deformation was primarily inertial-elastic
  • Norris (2013) conducted similar experiments in coarse vacuum with full-field displacement measurement and contradictorily asserted fluid loading cannot be neglected
  • Young et al. (2009) demonstrated that wing deformation details enhance aerodynamic function and flight efficiency
  • Du and Sun (2010) found flexibility increased averaged lift by only 10% in hoverflies, suggesting modest flow field effects from deformation

Computational FSI Models

  • Fairuz et al. (2013) studied unsteady aerodynamics of flapping wings at low Reynolds number using coupled FEA-CFD
  • Ishihara et al. (2009) investigated FSI-caused wing pitch changes in dipteran flight
  • Nakata and Liu (2012) computed aerodynamic performance of hovering hawkmoth with flexible wings, estimating flexibility increased peak vertical forces by ~22%
  • Pfeiffer et al. (2010) simulated ornithopter flight based on flexible multi-body dynamics

Reduced-Order Approaches

  • Wang et al. (2017) developed BET-based FSI for twistable wings showing torsional flexibility improves power efficiency
  • Stanford et al. (2012) parameterized shape, structure, and kinematics for power-optimal hovering
  • Berman and Wang (2007) used BET to identify energy-minimizing kinematics in hovering insect flight
  • Jankauski and Shen (2014) developed the original structural framework using Lagrangian dynamics for 3D-rotating insect wings, neglecting aerodynamic forces

Materials and Methods

Problem Setup

  • Idealized hawkmoth Manduca sexta forewing: constant thickness $t = 45\,\mu\text{m}$, density $\rho_w = 1200\,\text{kg/m}^3$, neglecting venation, camber, and spatial variation
  • Wing mass $m_w = 47\,\text{mg}$, wingspan $R = 54\,\text{mm}$, aspect ratio $AR = 3.48$, surface area $A = 867\,\text{mm}^2$, mean chord $\bar{c} = 15.5\,\text{mm}$
  • Rotating reference frame with three Euler rotations: roll $\alpha$ (about $X$), pitch $\beta$ (about $y''$), yaw $\gamma$ (about $z'$)
  • Unilateral coupling: fluid affects structure but deforming structure does not affect fluid; appropriate when wing deflections are small

Governing Equations

  • Angular velocity vector: $\vec{\Omega} = \Omega_x \vec{e}_x + \Omega_y \vec{e}_y + \Omega_z \vec{e}_z$ from Euler rotation rates
  • Elastic deformation as modal series: $W(\vec{r}_1, t) = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \phi_k(\vec{r}_1) q_k(t)$ with orthonormal mode shapes
  • Equation of motion: $\ddot{q}_k + [\omega_k^2 - (\Omega_x^2 + \Omega_y^2)] q_k = \dot{\vec{\Omega}} \cdot \vec{b}_k - \Omega_z \vec{\Omega} \cdot \vec{a}_k + Q_k$
  • Aerodynamic modal force via virtual work: $Q_k = \int_{S_w} F_N(\vec{r}_1, t) \phi_k(\vec{r}_1)\, dS_w$
  • BET differential force: $dF = \frac{1}{2} C \rho_f |\vec{V}|^2 dS_w$ with lift/drag coefficients $C_L = C_{L,\max} \sin(2A)$, $C_D = \frac{C_{D,\max}+C_{D,0}}{2} - \frac{C_{D,\max}-C_{D,0}}{2}\cos(2A)$

Numerical Approach

  • FEA model in ABAQUS with 611 shell elements (converged); first two modes retained: bending ($\omega_1 = 59\,\text{Hz}$) and torsion ($\omega_2 = 75\,\text{Hz}$)
  • BET: 20 blade elements in MATLAB, 25 wingbeats at 100 time intervals each; aerodynamic coefficients $C_{L,\max} = 1.68$, $C_{D,\max} = 3.06$, $C_{D,\min} = 0.07$
  • CFD: STAR-CCM+ v12.04, Spalart-Allmaras RANS with chimera overset mesh, ~2M elements, $\Delta t = 0.001\,\text{s}$, 10 rotation periods
  • Hovering kinematics: roll amplitude $\alpha_0 = 60°$, pitch amplitude $\beta_0 = 45°$, pitch/roll phase $\phi_{\alpha\beta} = \pi/2$, flap frequency $\omega = 25\,\text{Hz}$
  • Light modal damping $\zeta = 0.05$ in both modes; approximate analytic solution via harmonic balance method for verification

Validation Strategy

  • CFD mesh convergence: RMS error 1.5% at ~2M elements; time-step convergence at $\Delta t = 1.25 \times 10^{-4}\,\text{s}$
  • BET vs CFD comparison across net normal force, modal forces ($Q_1$, $Q_2$), and modal responses ($q_1$, $q_2$)
  • Sensitivity study varying $\alpha_0$, $\beta_0$, $\phi_{\alpha\beta}$ by $\pm\pi/8$ in $\pi/16$ increments, and driving frequency at 15, 25, 35 Hz

Results

BET vs CFD Force Comparison

  • Net aerodynamic normal force $F_N$: good agreement; BET overestimated primary response magnitude by ~30%; both models show oscillation at $\omega$ with harmonic at $3\omega$
  • Second modal force $Q_2$: excellent agreement at $\omega$ (BET: 1.92, CFD: 1.91, 0.26% difference); $3\omega$ also close (BET: 0.34, CFD: 0.35)
  • First modal force $Q_1$: BET overestimated $\omega$-magnitude by ~59% (BET: 4.52, CFD: 2.84) due to spanwise force center discrepancy (BET $r \approx 3.94\,\text{cm}$ vs CFD $r \approx 3.52\,\text{cm}$)
  • BET computed modal forces five orders of magnitude faster than CFD

Sensitivity Analysis

  • Small kinematic deviations ($\pm\pi/16$): BET performed fairly well, maximum magnitude error ~125%
  • Large deviations ($\pm\pi/8$): significant errors in $3\omega$ components (>300% for $Q_1$ at reduced pitch), but these correspond to non-flyable conditions
  • Results insensitive to driving frequency changes (15, 25, 35 Hz)
  • Flow separation at increased roll amplitude only modestly affected agreement: max error ~100%, phase difference ~$\pi/10$

Modal Force Decomposition

  • First (bending) mode: inertial-elastic forces ~1.8 times larger than aerodynamic at $\omega$; forces out of phase by $\pi/4$, interacting constructively
  • Second (torsional) mode: aerodynamic forces ~1.6 times larger than inertial-elastic at $\omega$; forces nearly out of phase by $\pi$, interacting destructively
  • In-air first modal response ~35% larger (peak-to-peak) than vacuum; $3\omega$ component 3 times larger in air
  • In-air second modal response dominated by $3\omega$ (near $\omega_2 = 75\,\text{Hz}$); in-vacuum dominated by $\omega$; phase differs by nearly $\pi$

Results Interpretation

Physical Reasoning

  • Aerodynamic forces contribute significantly to modal responses, contradicting Combes and Daniel (2003) who used only SDOF rotation
  • For SDOF rotation, fluid loading contribution may be even larger because induced flow remains normal to wing throughout the cycle
  • SDOF BET simulation confirms first modal response 25% larger in air than vacuum
  • Different force types governing different modes suggests insects may decouple aerodynamic and inertial sensing via strategic mechanoreceptor placement

Implications for Bio-Inspired Design

  • Analogy to halteres (gyroscopic organs shielded from aerodynamic forces) suggests wings of moths/butterflies may achieve similar sensing through mode-selective campaniform sensilla placement
  • BET with added mass captures critical aerodynamic features for FSI despite lacking absolute quantitative accuracy, making it a powerful tool for FWMAV parametric design

Limitations

  • Unilateral coupling: average steady-state displacement ~1.2 cm may affect flow field; Nakata and Liu (2011) estimated ~22% increase in peak vertical forces from wing flexibility
  • BET cannot model dynamic stall, clap-and-fling, or wing-wake interactions
  • Rotational correction factors did not improve predictions across all parameter sets
  • Du and Sun (2010) found only 10% lift increase in hoverflies, suggesting unilateral coupling may be justified in some cases

Conclusion
  • Developed a novel reduced-order FSI framework that is general enough for arbitrary wing geometry, any fluid model, and unilateral or bilateral coupling
  • BET provides reasonable aerodynamic modal force approximations five orders of magnitude faster than CFD
  • For hovering kinematics, BET-CFD agreement fair in magnitude (largest error ~50% for $Q_1$ at $\omega$) and phase; degrades for large kinematic deviations but those regimes are generally non-flyable
  • Inertial-elastic and aerodynamic forces are of the same order of magnitude: inertial-elastic dominates bending, aerodynamic dominates torsion
  • Aerodynamic forces contribute significantly to wing deformation, contradicting prior SDOF experimental findings
  • Framework enables high-dimensional parametric design of FWMAVs and lays foundation for advanced studies in insect sensorimotor control
  • Future work: bilateral FSI coupling, forward flight regimes, more realistic FEA models with venation and spatial property variation
