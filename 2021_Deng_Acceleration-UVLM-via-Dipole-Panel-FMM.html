Acceleration of unsteady vortex lattice method via dipole panel fast multipole method

Overview

This paper presents a Dipole Panel Fast Multipole Method (DPFMM) that accelerates the induced velocity computation in the Unsteady Vortex Lattice Method (UVLM) from $O(N^2)$ to $O(N)$ complexity. The key insight exploits the mathematical equivalence between a constant-strength dipole panel and a vortex ring, allowing the velocity field to be expressed via a single scalar harmonic function instead of the three required by conventional vortex filament FMM. The authors derive analytical multipole expansion coefficients for quadrilateral dipole panels in spherical coordinates and propose two octree space-partitioning strategies (loosening and shrinking methods) for volumetric panels. Benchmark tests demonstrate DPFMM is approximately two times faster than FMM for vortex filaments across $N \in [10^3, 10^6]$, reducing a multirotor UVLM simulation ($N \approx 10^4$) from 100 minutes to 2 minutes. This work is directly relevant to Dr. Yang's research on ornithopter aerodynamic simulation using UVLM, where computational acceleration is critical for design iteration and coupled aeroelastic analysis.

Introduction

Motivation

  • The global UAV market is rapidly expanding, with VTOL multi-rotor configurations widely deployed in civil and military applications
  • Aerodynamic analysis tools are essential for performance optimization, vibration/noise reduction, and flight dynamics modeling
  • CFD is powerful but computationally prohibitive: a quadcopter simulation required 48-72 hours on a NASA supercomputer
  • UVLM provides medium-fidelity aerodynamic analysis at significantly lower cost, but the induced velocity evaluation via the Biot-Savart law scales as $O(N^2)$, becoming the bottleneck (over 90% of computation time)

State of the Art

  • Panel methods discretize lifting surfaces with singularity elements (source, dipole, vortex) to solve Laplace's equation under potential flow assumptions
  • The Doublet Lattice Method (DLM) operates in the frequency domain with harmonically oscillating surfaces, limiting applicability to non-periodic transient responses
  • UVLM formulated in the time domain allows arbitrary lifting surfaces under general motions with free wake distortion
  • GPU parallelization provides hardware-dependent acceleration; algorithmic approaches include $O(N \log N)$ treecodes (Barnes and Hut, 1986) and $O(N)$ FMM
  • FMM for the Biot-Savart kernel uses three independent Laplacian harmonics; Gumerov and Duraiswami (2013) reduced this to two via Lamb-Helmholtz decomposition
  • Kebbie-Anthony et al. (2019) recently integrated FMM for vortex filaments with UVLM simulation models

Gap & Contribution

  • Existing FMM implementations for UVLM treat vortex lattices as collections of vortex filaments, requiring multiple scalar harmonic functions
  • This paper exploits the dipole-vortex ring equivalence to reduce the problem to a single scalar harmonic, yielding approximately 2x speedup
  • Analytical multipole expansion coefficients for quadrilateral dipole panels are derived in spherical coordinates for the first time
  • Two novel space-partitioning methods handle volumetric (non-point) source panels in the octree structure

Materials and Methods

Problem Setup

  • Lifting surfaces modeled as thin (zero-thickness) panels with quadrilateral vortex lattices (vortex rings)
  • Bound vortex lattices: leading line at 1/4 chord, rear line at 1/4 chord of following element; collocation point at 3/4 chord center
  • Wake vortex lattices convected from trailing edge with free wake distortion; corner points transported with local velocity
  • Non-penetration boundary condition enforced at all collocation points: $(\mathbf{v}_b + \mathbf{v}_w + \mathbf{v}_\infty - \mathbf{v}_k) \cdot \mathbf{n} = 0$

Governing Equations

  • Velocity potential satisfies Laplace's equation: $\nabla^2 \Phi = 0$
  • Induced velocity of vortex filament computed via Biot-Savart law with Vatistas vortex core model (smoothing constant $n = 2$) to regularize singularity
  • Dipole panel potential: $\Phi(\mathbf{y}) = -\int_S \frac{\mu \, \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{L}}{4\pi L^3} \, dS$ where $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{y} - \mathbf{x}$
  • Far-field multipole expansion: $\Phi(\mathbf{y}) \approx \sum_{n=0}^{P-1} \sum_{m=-n}^{n} \frac{M_n^m}{r^{n+1}} Y_n^m(\theta, \phi)$ valid for $r > R$
  • Sparsity property: nonzero multipole coefficients $\approx P^2/4$ vs. dense $P(P+1)/2$, arising when $n + m + 1$ is even

Numerical Approach

  • Octree construction: Two methods for volumetric panels — (1) loosening method enlarges cell radii to contain all children/bodies; (2) shrinking method finds minimum bounding spheres via post-order traversal
  • P2M (Panel to Multipole): Analytical derivation of coefficients in panel-fixed local frame via Chebyshev polynomial expansion and partial fraction decomposition; then translated (Rico et al., 2013) and rotated (Pinchon and Hoggan, 2007) to global frame
  • FMM pipeline: P2M → M2M → M2L → L2L → L2P for far-field; P2P direct Biot-Savart for near-field
  • Multipole Acceptance Criteria (MAC): $(r_1 + r_2)/L < \eta$ determines well-separated cell pairs for M2L translation
  • Panel area integral decomposed into four triangles, each parameterized in spherical coordinates with analytical indefinite integrals using recursive residue formulas from Ma et al. (2014)

Validation Strategy

  • P2M accuracy: potential and velocity on sampling spheres at $d \in [R, 10R]$ compared against analytical Katz-Plotkin solutions
  • FMM benchmark: direct Biot-Savart evaluation as ground truth across $N \in [10^3, 10^6]$
  • Two distribution patterns: stack (uniform) and ring (clustered) with $N = 10164$ bodies
  • Optimal cost-accuracy analysis via $(P, \eta)$ parameter sweeps with $P \in [5, 12]$

Results

Validation Results

  • Mean relative error of both potential and velocity decreases with increasing distance ratio $d/R$, confirming multipole convergence
  • Velocity from one scalar potential (DPFMM) matches accuracy of three vortex potentials (filament FMM) at the same truncation degree $P$
  • P2M crossover at $P = 6$: panel P2M slower for $P < 6$ but faster for $P > 6$, with $O(P^3)$ complexity dominated by coefficient operations

Parametric Studies

  • Optimal cost-accuracy line constructed by connecting minimal computation time at each accuracy level across $(P, \eta)$ sweeps
  • Shrinking method outperforms loosening: average 5.46% faster (stack distribution), 19.63% faster (ring/clustered distribution)
  • Maximum performance advantage of shrinking method: 26.29% for ring distribution in relative error range $[10^{-9}, 10^{-4}]$

Key Findings

  • DPFMM achieves confirmed $O(N)$ complexity (unit slope on log-log wall time plot)
  • Average runtime ratio (filament FMM / DPFMM) = 2.01 for $N \in [10^3, 10^6]$; DPFMM 1.76x faster at $N = 10^6$
  • 8-rotor UVLM ($N \approx 10^4$, 1400 lattices/rotor): 100.3 min (direct) reduced to 2.1 min (DPFMM) with relative velocity error $\sim 10^{-5}$
  • Time integration: explicit RK4, step size 0.001 s, 1-second rotational speed ramp; induced velocity evaluated 4 times per step

Results Interpretation
  • The approximately 2x speedup is consistent with reduction from three to one scalar harmonic; ratio is less than 3 because P2P (near-field direct) computation remains identical
  • The shrinking method provides tighter bounding spheres, enabling more M2L operations with fewer P2P evaluations, particularly beneficial for clustered distributions common in rotor wakes
  • The sparsity property of dipole panel coefficients ($P^2/4$ nonzero terms) contributes to additional savings in M2M and M2L translation operations
  • Crossover at $P = 6$ for P2M indicates that for typical engineering accuracy requirements ($P \geq 6$), DPFMM's P2M stage is already more efficient than filament P2M
  • The method maintains identical accuracy to filament FMM at any truncation degree, providing a pure speedup without accuracy trade-off
  • All benchmarks conducted on a single desktop (Intel i7-8700, 3.20 GHz, 16 GB RAM) using C++ with ExaFMM framework, demonstrating practical accessibility
  • Three-harmonics filament FMM used as baseline (rather than two-harmonics Lamb-Helmholtz version) since overall performance was reported comparable by Kebbie-Anthony et al. (2019)

Conclusion
  • DPFMM exploits dipole panel-vortex ring equivalence to express the velocity field via one scalar harmonic function instead of three
  • Analytical multipole expansion coefficients derived in spherical coordinates with accuracy matching the Biot-Savart kernel at identical truncation degree $P$
  • Shrinking method for octree space partitioning of volumetric panels outperforms loosening method, especially for highly clustered source distributions
  • Average runtime ratio of approximately 2x between filament FMM and DPFMM validated across four orders of magnitude ($N \in [10^3, 10^6]$)
  • Practical demonstration: 8-rotor UVLM simulation reduced from 100 min to 2 min on desktop hardware
  • Limitation: assumes constant dipole strength per panel; higher-order panel distributions would require new derivations
  • Future potential: coupling DPFMM-accelerated UVLM with structural dynamics for large-scale aeroelastic simulations of multi-rotor or flapping-wing configurations
