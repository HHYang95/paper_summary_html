Overview

Research Motivation and Gap

Insect-like flapping-wing micro-air vehicles (FWMAVs) have been the subject of extensive research in aerodynamics and flight dynamics, yet few studies have comprehensively examined how wing flexibility affects flight performance across multiple aspects simultaneously. Previous works addressed individual aspects such as aerodynamic force enhancement or power savings, but a unified treatment covering trim conditions, power requirements, and dynamic stability for flexible-winged FWMAVs across a range of flight speeds was lacking. This study addresses that gap by performing a systematic numerical analysis using coupled fluid-structure-flight dynamics simulation.

Core Contribution

The paper develops an integrated simulation framework that couples a potential-based aerodynamic model (combining the unsteady panel method and extended unsteady vortex-lattice method) with a flexible multibody dynamics solver (MSC.Adams) to analyze the hawkmoth Manduca sexta FWMAV at flight speeds from 0.0 to 4.0 m/s. The framework captures the full interaction between wing structural deformation, unsteady aerodynamics, and 6-DOF body dynamics, enabling a unified assessment of flexibility effects on trim, power, and stability.

Key Findings Summary

Wing flexibility reduces flapping frequency by up to 14% and mechanical power by up to 20.6% during hovering, with these benefits diminishing as flight speed increases. At 4.0 m/s, flexible wings become detrimental, requiring higher flapping frequency and more power than rigid wings. Wing flexibility has negligible effects on longitudinal dynamic stability but significantly enhances lateral stability during low-speed flight through changes in the stability derivatives $L_v^+$ and $N_v^+$, attributed to bending-up deformations of flexible wings.

Introduction

Flapping-Wing MAV Development Context

  • Insect-like FWMAVs represent a new generation of micro-scale aircraft inspired by insect flight, offering efficiency advantages at small scales and capabilities for hovering and high maneuverability.
  • Advances in computational fluid dynamics and experimental methods have clarified many aerodynamic aspects of insect flight, while dynamic stability characterization has been investigated using linearization methods based on the cycle-average approach.

Wing Flexibility Research Landscape

  • Previous numerical studies by Singh and Chopra, Senda et al., Gogulapati et al., and Stanford and Beran investigated wing flexibility effects on flapping-wing performance, with most employing quasi-steady or two-dimensional aerodynamic models.
  • Higher-fidelity CFD-based approaches by Nakata and Liu showed that wing flexibility can increase lift by approximately 20% for hawkmoth wings.
  • However, comprehensive analysis covering trim conditions, power requirements, and dynamic stability for flexible FWMAVs across multiple flight speeds had not been conducted.

Related Works

Aerodynamic and Structural Modeling for Flexible Wings

  • The extended unsteady vortex-lattice method (UVLM) with leading-edge suction analogy was developed by the authors (Nguyen et al., 2016) for insect flapping wings, providing a computationally efficient yet physically faithful aerodynamic model.
  • The leading-edge suction analogy translates the suction force from attached flow around a round leading edge into a force perpendicular to the wing surface, modeling the effect of spiral spanwise leading-edge vortices observed on insect wings.
  • The vortex-core growth model incorporates viscous diffusion effects.

Flight Dynamics and Stability Studies

  • Kim and Han (2014) developed a multibody approach for 6-DOF flight dynamics of the hawkmoth using quasi-steady blade element theory.
  • Nguyen et al. (2016) investigated the effect of body aerodynamics on hawkmoth dynamic flight stability.
  • The trim search algorithm and linearization methods for longitudinal and lateral dynamics used in the present study were established in these earlier works.

Materials and Methods

FWMAV and Wing Structural Models

  • The hawkmoth Manduca sexta serves as the reference insect. Wing mass $m_{\text{wg}} = 46.87$ mg, span $R = 48.50$ mm, mean chord $\bar{c} = 16.81$ mm, wing area $S = 815.33$ mm$^2$, and second moment of area radius $r_2 = 0.53$.
  • Body mass $m_{\text{bd}} = 1485.0$ mg and body length $L = 44.80$ mm.
  • Wing kinematics (sweep $\phi$, elevation $\theta$, rotation $\alpha$) are parameterized as third-order Fourier series from experimental measurements by Willmott and Ellington.
  • The flexible wing structure is modeled in ANSYS Mechanical APDL using BEAM188 elements for veins and SHELL181 elements for membrane.
  • The first three vibration modes have natural frequencies of 65.8 Hz (bending), 122.2 Hz (torsion), and 175.3 Hz (saddle), validated against biological wing measurements by O'Hara.

Coupled Aerodynamic-Structural-Dynamics Framework

  • The aerodynamic model combines the unsteady panel method (UPM) for the streamlined body and the extended UVLM for the wings.
  • The leading-edge vortex force per unit length of leading edge is: $$F_V = \frac{\pi}{16}\eta_s \rho \Gamma_{L.E.}^2 \frac{x_{L.E.}}{\cos\Lambda_{L.E.}}$$ where $\eta_s = 0.5$ is the suction efficiency coefficient.
  • The vortex core radius grows as: $$r_c(t) = \sqrt{4\alpha_L\nu\left(1 + a_1\frac{\Gamma}{\nu}\right)t}$$ with Lamb constant $\alpha_L = 1.25643$ and Squire parameter $a_1 = 0.1$.
  • The flexible multibody dynamics solver in MSC.Adams integrates the coupled equations: $$M\ddot{\xi} + \dot{M}\dot{\xi} - \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial M}{\partial\xi}\dot{\xi}^T\dot{\xi} + K\xi + f_g + \frac{\partial\Upsilon}{\partial\xi}^T\lambda = Q, \quad \Upsilon = 0$$
  • using the GSTIFF integrator with adaptive time stepping. Aerodynamic loads are transferred to the structural mesh via a nearest-neighbor interpolation scheme.

Trim Search and Stability Analysis

  • Trimmed flight is obtained by iteratively tuning the flapping frequency $f$, sweep angle $\phi$, and rotation angle $\alpha$ until the mean velocities match reference values across the flight speed range 0.0--4.0 m/s.
  • The linearized 6-DOF dynamics uses the cycle-average approach to derive longitudinal and lateral stability matrices from nondimensionalized equations of motion.

Results

Convergence Study and Validation

  • Convergence studies determined appropriate simulation parameters: 72 aerodynamic panels, 1222 FEA nodes, 100 time steps per stroke cycle, and 3 vibration modes.
  • The rigid and flexible wing lift coefficients show good agreement with CSD/CFD coupling results by Nakata and Liu for the related species Agrius convolvuli, with wing flexibility increasing lift by approximately 18% (reference value: 20%).

Trim Condition Variations

  • Wing flexibility reduces the trim flapping frequency at all speeds below 4.0 m/s, with the maximum reduction of 3.9 Hz (14%) occurring at hovering.
  • The reduction diminishes with increasing speed: only 0.5 Hz at 3.0 m/s. At 4.0 m/s, the flexible FWMAV requires a higher flapping frequency than the rigid case.
  • The mean sweep angle $\phi$ increases slightly due to flexibility at speeds 0.0--2.0 m/s. The mean rotation angle $\alpha$ increases due to flexibility at speeds below 3.0 m/s.

Mechanical Power Requirements

  • The mean mechanical power reductions due to flexibility are 20.6% at 0.0 m/s, 10.3% at 1.0 m/s, and 18.6% at 2.0 m/s.
  • At 3.0 m/s the power difference is negligible, while at 4.0 m/s the flexible FWMAV consumes approximately 1.4 times more power than the rigid model due to the 1.12 times higher flapping frequency.
  • The power trend correlates with the stroke plane angle $\beta$: at low speeds with small $\beta$, wing deformations enhance the downward induced flow beneficial for lift; at 4.0 m/s with near-vertical stroke plane, deformations intensify an upward flow region detrimental to lift generation.

Dynamic Stability Characteristics

  • Wing flexibility has almost no effect on longitudinal dynamic stability characteristics. Both rigid and flexible FWMAVs exhibit two stable subsidence modes and one unstable oscillatory mode in the longitudinal plane.
  • In contrast, wing flexibility significantly enhances lateral stability at speeds below 3.0 m/s, primarily through changes in the stability derivatives $L_v^+$ and $N_v^+$.
  • At 4.0 m/s, the lateral stability enhancement becomes unnoticeable.
  • The bending-up deformations of flexible wings generate an extra force $F$ during lateral translation that produces stabilizing negative roll and yaw moments during the downstroke and upstroke respectively.

Results Interpretation

Speed-Dependent Flexibility Benefits

  • The speed dependence of flexibility effects is governed by the stroke plane angle $\beta$.
  • At low speeds, the small stroke plane angle directs the airflow created by wing deformations nearly vertically downward, making a significant contribution to lift production via the momentum conservation mechanism.
  • As speed increases, $\beta$ grows and the induced flow direction tilts, reducing the lift contribution from wing deformations.
  • At 4.0 m/s with a nearly vertical stroke plane, wing deformations create an upward flow at the beginning of the downstroke that is actively detrimental to lift.

Mechanism of Lateral Stability Enhancement

  • The lateral stability improvement at low speeds is attributed to the interaction between wing bending deformations and lateral body velocity.
  • During lateral translation, the bending-up wing deformations produce an extra aerodynamic force that generates stabilizing moments.
  • The wing surfaces are nearly perpendicular to the body axis during upstroke (causing negative yaw moment $N$) and nearly parallel during downstroke (causing negative roll moment $L$). These moment contributions modify the stability derivatives $L_v^+$ and $N_v^+$, making the lateral dynamics more stable.
  • At high speeds, wing deformations are less severe because inertial forces (which primarily drive the deformations) become relatively smaller.

Asymmetric Flexibility Impact on Longitudinal versus Lateral Dynamics

  • Wing deformations are concentrated in the wing tip regions, which generate the largest aerodynamic forces during translational motion.
  • The tip regions affect lateral moment components ($L$ and $N$) more strongly than the pitching moment $M$, explaining why flexibility has a more profound effect on lateral stability than on longitudinal stability.
  • The body pitch oscillation amplitude shows an interesting reversal: it increases with flexibility at low speeds (due to reduced pitch damping from wing torsion) but decreases at high speeds (where aftward wing positioning reverses the damping effect).

Conclusion

Principal Results

  • Wing flexibility offers significant performance benefits for the hawkmoth-like FWMAV at hovering and low flight speeds: up to 14% reduction in flapping frequency, up to 20.6% reduction in mechanical power, and enhanced lateral dynamic stability.
  • These advantages are governed by the stroke plane angle and diminish as flight speed increases. At 4.0 m/s, flexibility becomes detrimental to both power efficiency and lift generation.
  • The longitudinal dynamic stability is essentially unaffected by wing flexibility across all flight speeds tested.

Design Implications for FWMAVs

  • The findings suggest that biomimetic flexible wings are advantageous for FWMAV designs intended primarily for hovering and low-speed flight regimes, offering both energy savings and improved stability.
  • For high-speed applications, designers should consider that flexible wings may increase power demands and reduce lift efficiency.
  • The speed at which flexibility transitions from beneficial to detrimental (approximately 3.0 m/s for this hawkmoth model) provides a useful design reference for selecting wing stiffness in insect-scale FWMAVs.
