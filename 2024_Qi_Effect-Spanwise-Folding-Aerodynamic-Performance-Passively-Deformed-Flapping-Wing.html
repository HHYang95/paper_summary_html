Overview

This paper presents a coupled aeroelastic method for the rapid analysis of folding flapping wings with passive torsional deformation, targeting the early design stage. The aerodynamic solver employs the unsteady three-dimensional panel method (UPM) while structural deformation is modeled via a linear beam element model, coupled through a Newmark-$\beta$ time-marching scheme. The study systematically investigates 10 kinematic parameter combinations—varying the flapping phase angle $\phi_\beta$, inner wing flapping amplitude $\beta_{in\_max}$, and mean folding angle $\beta_{mean}$—on a Canadian-goose-inspired wing at $Re \approx 37{,}500$–$183{,}750$, $St = 0.167$. Key results show that the phase angle $\phi_\beta$ most strongly governs thrust and propulsion efficiency, while $\beta_{mean}$ primarily affects lift by creating asymmetric flapping trajectories that reduce upstroke lift loss. The best propulsion efficiency ($\eta_P = 0.632$) is achieved in Case 7 ($\phi_\beta = 45°$, $\beta_{in\_max} = 30°$, $\beta_{mean} = 0°$). This work is directly relevant to Dr. Yang's research on ornithopter parameter optimization and folding wing mechanism design, particularly the interaction between spanwise folding kinematics and passive aeroelastic response—complementing Yang et al. (2024) on foldable flapping-wing mechanism optimization (Ref. [8]).

Introduction

Motivation

  • Birds exhibit multi-degree-of-freedom wing motions including active flapping, twisting, passive torsional/bending deformation, and spanwise folding—all contributing to exceptional flight performance.
  • Spanwise folding is a primary characteristic of large birds in flight, enabling efficient lift and thrust generation.
  • Understanding the impact of folding kinematics combined with passive torsional deformation on performance is pivotal for flapping-wing design.

State of the Art

  • Prior experimental studies: Ryu et al. (2020) built four-bar linkage folding wings; Chen et al. (2021) measured vertical aerodynamic force with different wing-folding amplitudes; Qin et al. (2021) used PIV to study tip vortex evolution.
  • Prior computational studies: Han (2009) applied the panel method to seagull wing folding; Yang et al. (2024) used UVLM to optimize ornithopter folding parameters; Lang et al. (2022) solved N-S equations for flat-panel folding wings.
  • The single-layer vortex lattice method (VLM) cannot compute surface pressure coefficients, limiting design insight.
  • N-S equation solvers are accurate but computationally expensive, especially with fluid–structure coupling, making them impractical for early design stages.

Gap & Contribution

  • Existing research lacks efficient methods that capture surface pressure while accounting for passive torsional deformation in folding wings.
  • The impact of folding on propulsion efficiency has not been adequately addressed.
  • Most studies ignore wing torsional deformation and geometric parameters, focusing solely on folding motion and leading to unrealistic separated-flow conditions.
  • This paper proposes an unsteady 3D panel method + linear beam element coupled approach for rapid aeroelastic analysis of folding flapping wings.

Materials and Methods

Problem Setup

  • Wing geometry inspired by the Canadian goose: half wingspan = 0.8 m, aspect ratio = 12, root chord = 0.3 m, root-to-tip ratio = 2.
  • Inner-to-outer wing length ratio: 0.45 (inner) : 0.55 (outer). Inner wing is torsionally rigid; outer wing undergoes passive torsion.
  • Airfoils: NACA 8412 (root) and NACA 0012 (tip), linearly interpolated along span. Root angle of incidence = 4°.
  • Flight velocity $V_\infty = 15$ m/s; $Re$ from 37,500 to 183,750; flapping period $T = 0.33$ s; Strouhal number $St = 0.167$; reduced frequency $k = 0.0952$.
  • Beam placed at 10% chord behind the leading edge; lift center at ~25% chord; mass distributed at 10% chord behind beam.
  • Torsional stiffness $GJ$ varies with wingspan to the fourth power (linear relationship between beam cross-sectional diameter and wingspan).

Governing Equations

  • Aerodynamic model: Unsteady 3D panel method with source + dipole panels on wing surface and dipole-only wake panels. Neumann (velocity) boundary condition: $\vec{V} \cdot \hat{n} = 0$ on wing surface.
  • Unsteady Kutta condition at trailing edge: $[V_{t_{up}}]_k^2 - [V_{t_{down}}]_k^2 = 2\left[\frac{\partial(\Phi_{down} - \Phi_{up})}{\partial t}\right]_k$
  • Structural model: Linear beam element with $6 \times 6$ stiffness matrix incorporating bending stiffness $EI$ and torsional stiffness $GJ$.
  • Aeroelastic equilibrium: $[K]\vec{q}_{k+1} + [M]\ddot{\vec{q}}_{k+1} = \vec{F}_{k+1}$, solved via Newmark-$\beta$ method (unconditionally stable).
  • Unsteady Bernoulli theorem for pressure coefficient: $C_p = \left(\frac{V_{move}}{V_\infty}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{V}{V_\infty}\right)^2 - \frac{2}{V_\infty^2}\frac{\partial \Phi}{\partial t}$

Kinematic Parameterization

  • Inner wing flapping: $\beta_{in}(t) = \beta_{in\_max} \cos(\omega t)$
  • Outer wing (relative to inner): $\beta_{out}(t) = \beta_{mean} - \beta_{out\_max} \cos(\omega t - \phi_\beta)$
  • Three key parameters varied: flapping phase angle $\phi_\beta \in \{0°, 45°, 90°\}$, inner wing amplitude $\beta_{in\_max} \in \{20°, 30°\}$, mean folding angle $\beta_{mean} \in \{0°, 30°\}$. Outer wing amplitude fixed at $\beta_{out\_max} = 30°$.
  • 10 parameter combinations (Cases 1–10) studied systematically (Table 1).

Validation Strategy

  • Aerodynamic validation: Compared against Lin (2005) 3D Euler equation results for a flapping rectangular NACA 0014 wing with 4° tip twist and 90° phase lag. Good agreement in both lift and thrust coefficients (Fig. 3).
  • FSI validation: Compared against DeLaurier (1993) computational and experimental results for a passively twisted flapping wing. Good agreement in average lift, average thrust, torsion amplitude, and flapping elastic amplitude (Fig. 4). Minor deviations attributed to differences in structural and aerodynamic models.

Results

Effect of Initial Geometric Twist Angle

  • Lift coefficient $C_l$ increases monotonically with increasing initial geometric twist angle $\theta_{geo}$ across all cases (Fig. 9a).
  • When $\beta_{mean} = 0°$, kinematic parameters have little impact on $C_l$; when $\beta_{mean} = 30°$, asymmetric wing configurations produce larger $C_l$ (Cases 2, 4 vs. Cases 1, 3).
  • Thrust coefficient $C_t$ first increases then decreases with $\theta_{geo}$, peaking near average angle of attack = 0° (Fig. 9b). $C_t$ clearly stratified into three levels: highest in Cases 7–8 ($C_t = 0.112, 0.104$), followed by Cases 3–4, 5–6, 9–10, lowest in Cases 1–2 ($C_t = 0.028, 0.027$).
  • Propulsion efficiency $\eta_P$ also first increases then decreases. Best: Case 7 ($\eta_P = 0.632$), Case 8 ($\eta_P = 0.622$).

Intra-Cycle Aerodynamic Variations

  • Lift coefficient during one period is approximately sinusoidal, generally positive but briefly negative during upstroke (Fig. 10a). Larger $\phi_\beta$ produces more lagging phase and smaller peak values.
  • When $\beta_{mean} = 30°$, the maximum lift coefficient is advanced over that when $\beta_{mean} = 0°$, consistent with reduced upstroke lift loss from the asymmetric folding trajectory.
  • Thrust coefficient curve has two peaks per cycle; downstroke peak > upstroke peak (Fig. 10b). Smaller $\phi_\beta$ yields larger thrust and more lagging phase.
  • Wing-tip passive twist angle is approximately linear along span (Fig. 11b). Maximum effective angle of attack $\theta_{eff}$ does not exceed 15° for all cases, validating the inviscid method applicability (Fig. 11c).

Surface Pressure Coefficient Analysis

  • Pressure distribution at 75% span analyzed at four phase points per cycle (Figs. 12–16). Suction peak indicates positive effective angle of attack at $t/T = 0.25 + \phi_{max}/2\pi$ and negative at $t/T = 0.75 + \phi_{min}/2\pi$.
  • $\beta_{mean} = 30°$ biases average angle of attack positive during the cycle (Case 2 vs. Case 1), making lift coefficient larger.
  • Cases 3–4 ($\phi_\beta = 90°$, $\beta_{in\_max} = 30°$) show excessively large effective angles of attack, risking airflow separation in practice.
  • Cases 7–8 ($\phi_\beta = 45°$, $\beta_{in\_max} = 30°$): maximum effective angle of attack is excessively large, indicating the need to adjust initial geometric twist angle. Not allowed during the flapping process (Ref. [18]).
  • Case 4 with $\theta_{geo} = -7°$ yields comparable airfoil pressure range to Case 6 with $\theta_{geo} = -4°$, but with higher thrust and propulsion efficiency, demonstrating that larger $\phi_\beta$ with adjusted twist angle is beneficial.

Key Quantitative Results (Table 2, $\theta_{geo} = -4°$)

  • $C_l$: ranges from 0.536 (Cases 3, 7) to 0.623 (Case 4). $\beta_{mean} = 30°$ consistently increases $C_l$ by ~3–15%.
  • $C_t$: ranges from 0.0274 (Case 2) to 0.112 (Case 7), a 4x variation. Dominated by $\phi_\beta$ and $\beta_{in\_max}$.
  • $\eta_P$: ranges from 0.431 (Case 1) to 0.632 (Case 7). Case 4 with $\theta_{geo} = -7°$: $\eta_P = 0.584$, $C_t = 0.069$, $C_l = 0.575$.

Results Interpretation

Physical Mechanisms

  • The flapping phase angle $\phi_\beta$ governs outer wing velocity amplitude: smaller $\phi_\beta$ produces higher wing-tip velocity, directly increasing thrust coefficient and unsteadiness. This is the dominant parameter for thrust and propulsion efficiency.
  • The mean folding angle $\beta_{mean}$ creates trajectory asymmetry between upstroke and downstroke. When positive, the outer wing folds downward during upstroke, reducing upward velocity and lift loss, yielding net higher cycle-averaged lift.
  • The inner wing amplitude $\beta_{in\_max}$ controls overall unsteadiness. Higher values increase thrust but risk excessive effective angle of attack on the outer wing, potentially causing separation and reducing propulsion efficiency.
  • Wing-tip velocity analysis (Fig. 8) confirms that the curve of velocity vs. time is sinusoidal; larger $\phi_\beta$ reduces peak velocity and introduces phase lag; $\beta_{mean}$ has only a tiny effect on velocity peak.

Relationship Between Pressure and Performance

  • Surface pressure coefficient analysis reveals the connection between kinematic parameters and effective angle of attack history: excessive angles of attack lead to suction peaks that would cause separation in viscous flow.
  • The inviscid panel method cannot simulate separation directly, but pressure distributions enable identification of parameter combinations that would be problematic in practice.
  • The initial geometric twist angle $\theta_{geo}$ serves as a design variable to keep the effective angle of attack within a reasonable range during both upstroke and downstroke.

Limitations

  • The method is inviscid (no viscous effects, no separation modeling), limiting applicability to conditions where effective angle of attack remains moderate (<15°).
  • Fixed-wake model used instead of free-wake, which may affect accuracy for large-amplitude motions.
  • Bending stiffness assumed rigid to isolate folding effects; real wings have coupled bending-torsion.
  • Only 2D airfoil characteristics used for torsion loading; 3D spanwise flow effects on separation are not captured.

Conclusion

Key Claims

  • The proposed UPM + linear beam element + Newmark-$\beta$ coupled method enables rapid and accurate aeroelastic analysis of folding flapping wings for early-stage design.
  • Kinematic parameters significantly impact performance: $\phi_\beta$ governs outer wing velocity and thrust; $\beta_{mean}$ governs trajectory asymmetry and lift; $\beta_{in\_max}$ governs overall unsteadiness.
  • A larger $\phi_\beta$ (e.g., 90°) reduces outer wing flapping velocity and thrust but creates beneficial asymmetric trajectories when combined with positive $\beta_{mean}$.
  • The mean folding angle $\beta_{mean}$ should be designed to align with the initial geometric twist angle $\theta_{geo}$ to prevent excessive angle of attack throughout the flapping cycle.
  • Best propulsion efficiency ($\eta_P = 0.632$) at Case 7; Case 4 with adjusted $\theta_{geo} = -7°$ achieves good balance of $C_l = 0.575$, $C_t = 0.069$, $\eta_P = 0.584$.

Design Guidelines for Folding Flapping Wings

  • For lift: use asymmetric configuration with positive $\beta_{mean}$ and large $\phi_\beta$ to reduce upstroke lift loss.
  • For thrust and propulsion efficiency: concentrate thrust generation in the downstroke by selecting positive $\beta_{mean}$; adjust $\beta_{in\_max}$ for overall unsteadiness; match $\theta_{geo}$ with $\beta_{mean}$ to maintain reasonable angle of attack range.
  • When $\phi_\beta = 0°$, although wing-tip flapping velocity is maximized, the outer wing experiences excessive angle of attack, reducing propulsion efficiency compared to $\phi_\beta = 45°$ or $90°$.

Future Directions

  • Extend to viscous methods (RANS or hybrid RANS-LES) for high-angle-of-attack regime with separation.
  • Incorporate free-wake models for improved wake interaction accuracy.
  • Include coupled bending-torsion structural response for more realistic wing deformation.
  • Optimization of full kinematic parameter space (including outer wing amplitude, wing planform geometry).
