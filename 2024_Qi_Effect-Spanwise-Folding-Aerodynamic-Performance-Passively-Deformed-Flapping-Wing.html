Overview

This paper presents a computationally efficient fluid-structure interaction (FSI) method for analyzing folding flapping wings with passive torsional deformation, targeting the early design stage of ornithopter-type vehicles. The approach couples an unsteady three-dimensional panel method for aerodynamic loads with a linear beam element model for structural torsion, solved via the Newmark-$\beta$ time integration scheme. The method is validated against Euler equation solutions by Lin and experimental data from DeLaurier. Using a Canadian goose-inspired wing geometry (half-span 0.8 m, aspect ratio 12, $Re \approx 367{,}500$), the authors conduct a parametric study across 10 kinematic configurations, varying the flapping phase angle $\phi_\beta$, inner wing flapping amplitude $\beta_{in\_max}$, and mean folding angle $\beta_{mean}$. Key findings show that $\phi_\beta$ and $\beta_{in\_max}$ dominate thrust and propulsion efficiency (peak $\eta_P = 0.632$), while $\beta_{mean}$ primarily controls lift via asymmetric flapping trajectories. This work is directly relevant to Dr. Yang's research on foldable flapping-wing mechanism optimization, as the paper cites Yang et al. (2024) and addresses the same design space of folding ornithopter wings.

Introduction

Motivation

  • Large birds exhibit multi-degree-of-freedom wing motions including active flapping, twisting, passive torsional and bending deformations, and spanwise folding — all contributing to exceptional flight capabilities.
  • Spanwise folding is a primary characteristic of large birds in flight, yet its interaction with passive torsional deformation on aerodynamic performance is not well understood.
  • Understanding the impact of folding kinematics on lift, thrust, and propulsion efficiency is pivotal for practical ornithopter design.

State of the Art

  • Existing folding flapping wing designs rely heavily on experiments and simple analytical methods, making it difficult to guide systematic design.
  • The vortex lattice method (VLM) cannot compute surface pressure coefficients — only lift and thrust changes — leaving the wing surface state unclear.
  • N-S equation solvers are accurate but computationally expensive, especially with FSI coupling, making them impractical for the initial design stage.
  • Previous studies seldom consider the combined effects of wing torsional deformation and geometric parameters, focusing solely on folding motion and often resulting in unrealistic widespread flow separation.

Gap & Contribution

  • No existing method provides fast, accurate FSI analysis of folding flapping wings with passive torsion that includes surface pressure distribution for the early design stage.
  • The impact of folding on propulsion efficiency has not been systematically studied.
  • This paper proposes a coupled unsteady 3D panel method + linear beam element approach that computes surface pressure coefficients and enables parametric design studies at low computational cost.

Related Works

Design and Experimental Studies

  • Kim et al. (2018) built a folding bird-like flapping wing, analyzed with simple methods, and conducted flight tests.
  • Ryu et al. (2020) experimentally studied a folding flapping wing with four-bar linkage, concluding that folding is conducive to lift generation.
  • Chen and Yeh (2021) used experiments to study the influence of wing-folding amplitudes on vertical aerodynamic force of a small flapping wing.
  • Qin et al. (2021) used PIV experiments to study tip vortex evolution of a folding flapping wing.

Computational Methods

  • Huang (2019) employed 2D airfoil method to solve folding flapping wing aerodynamics and optimized structural parameters in the linkage mechanism.
  • Han (2009) applied the panel method for unsteady aerodynamic force of a folding seagull wing, finding that folding produces greater lift and thrust than simple flapping, but without considering wing twist.
  • Yang et al. (2024) employed the unsteady vortex lattice method (UVLM) to calculate aerodynamic force of a folding ornithopter and used optimization algorithms to maximize average lift — directly related to Dr. Yang's research group.
  • Karimian and Jahanbin (2020) used a 2D lift line model with beam elements for aeroelastic modeling, showing that folding improves performance and that outer wing phase has great influence.
  • Verstraete et al. (2015) utilized UVLM with linear FEM to simulate seagull-like folding wings and studied the effect on flutter speed.
  • Chang et al. (2019) and Lang et al. (2022) applied N-S equation solvers to study folding flapping wing models, finding lift and thrust are mainly generated in the downstroke.
  • Bie et al. (2022) used N-S solver for a bat-like folding flapping wing, analyzing inner-to-outer wing ratio effects.

Materials and Methods

Problem Setup

  • Wing geometry inspired by the Canadian goose: half wingspan = 0.8 m, aspect ratio = 12, root chord = 0.3 m, root-to-tip ratio = 2.0.
  • Wing divided into inner section (45% span, torsionally rigid) and outer section (55% span, passively twisting).
  • Airfoils: NACA 8412 (root) to NACA 0012 (tip) with linear interpolation; root angle of incidence = 4 degrees.
  • Flight velocity $V_\infty = 15$ m/s; $Re \approx 367{,}500$ (root) to $183{,}750$ (tip); flapping period $T = 0.33$ s; Strouhal number $St = 0.167$; reduced frequency $k = 0.0952$.
  • Beam placed at 10% chord; lift center at ~25% chord to maximize aerodynamic torque on the beam.

Governing Equations

  • Aerodynamic model: Unsteady 3D panel method with source and dipole panels on the wing, dipole-only wake panels. Boundary condition: Neumann (zero normal velocity on wing surface) with unsteady Kutta condition at trailing edge: $$[V_t]_{up}^2 - [V_t]_{down}^2 = 2\frac{\partial(\Phi_{down} - \Phi_{up})}{\partial t}$$
  • Structural model: Linear beam element with stiffness matrix incorporating bending stiffness $EI$ and torsional stiffness $GJ$. Torsional stiffness varies with wingspan to the fourth power (linear cross-section diameter assumption).
  • Pressure coefficient via unsteady Bernoulli theorem: $C_p = \left(\frac{V_{move}}{V_\infty}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{V}{V_\infty}\right)^2 - \frac{2}{V_\infty^2}\frac{\partial \Phi}{\partial t}$
  • Performance metrics: Lift coefficient $C_l$, thrust coefficient $C_t$, power coefficient $C_P$, propulsion efficiency $\eta_P = C_t / C_P$.

Numerical Approach

  • Panel discretization: Wing divided into $M$ chordwise and $N$ spanwise panels; fixed-wake model (Reichert) used to save computation time.
  • FSI coupling: Dynamic equilibrium $[K]\vec{q}_{k+1} + [M]\ddot{\vec{q}}_{k+1} = \vec{F}_{k+1}$ solved via the Newmark-$\beta$ method (unconditionally stable).
  • Kinematic law: Inner wing flapping $\beta_{in}(t) = \beta_{in\_max}\cos(\omega t)$; outer wing $\beta_{out}(t) = \beta_{mean} - \beta_{out\_max}\cos(\omega t - \phi_\beta)$; fixed $\beta_{out\_max} = 30°$.
  • 10 parametric cases: $\phi_\beta \in \{0°, 45°, 90°\}$, $\beta_{in\_max} \in \{20°, 30°\}$, $\beta_{mean} \in \{0°, 30°\}$.

Validation Strategy

  • Aerodynamic validation: Compared against Lin's 3D Euler equation solution for a flapping rectangular NACA 0014 wing — lift and thrust coefficients closely matched (Fig. 3).
  • FSI validation: Compared against DeLaurier's (1993) computations and experiments for a passively twisted flapping wing — good agreement in average lift, thrust, torsion amplitude, and phase angles (Fig. 4).
  • Some deviations in outer wing twist angles attributed to differences in structural and aerodynamic model fidelity (non-stick model used).

Results

Kinematic Analysis

  • Mean folding angle $\beta_{mean}$ controls wing configuration symmetry: $\beta_{mean} = 0°$ yields symmetric upward/downward trajectories; $\beta_{mean} = 30°$ produces asymmetric trajectories resembling real large bird flight.
  • Smaller phase angle $\phi_\beta$ increases the range of movement and unsteadiness of the outer wing; wing-tip velocity peaks are highest at $\phi_\beta = 0°$ (simple superposition of inner/outer velocities).
  • The phase angle $\phi_\beta$ has the greatest impact on wing-tip velocity, followed by $\beta_{in\_max}$; $\beta_{mean}$ has minimal velocity effect.

Aerodynamic Performance vs. Geometric Twist

  • Lift coefficient increases monotonically with initial geometric twist angle $\theta_{geo}$ due to increased average angle of attack. When $\beta_{mean} = 0°$, only $\theta_{geo}$ significantly affects $C_l$; when $\beta_{mean} = 30°$, asymmetric configuration yields larger $C_l$ (Cases 2 and 4 vs. Cases 1 and 3).
  • Thrust coefficient first increases then decreases with $\theta_{geo}$ (peak near 0° average angle of attack). Three distinct levels: highest in Cases 7 and 8 ($C_t = 0.112$ and $0.104$); lowest in Cases 1 and 2 ($C_t = 0.0278$ and $0.0274$).
  • Propulsion efficiency follows similar trend to thrust: peak $\eta_P = 0.632$ (Case 7), lowest $\eta_P = 0.431$ (Case 1). Both $\beta_{in\_max}$ and $\phi_\beta$ are dominant parameters.

Within-Cycle Aerodynamic Analysis

  • Lift coefficient curves are approximately sinusoidal; larger $\phi_\beta$ causes more lagging phase and smaller peak values. When $\beta_{mean} = 30°$, the maximum $C_l$ is advanced and the phase of the minimum $C_l$ lags compared to $\beta_{mean} = 0°$.
  • Thrust coefficient exhibits two peaks per cycle (downward peak > upward peak). Asymmetric motion ($\beta_{mean} = 30°$) concentrates thrust generation in the downstroke while reducing upstroke thrust.
  • Wing-tip passive twist angles are nearly linear along the span. Maximum effective angle of attack does not exceed 15°, validating the inviscid method's applicability.

Pressure Coefficient Analysis

  • Airfoil surface pressure at 75% span was examined at key cycle moments (maximum/minimum lift instants). In Cases 3 and 4 ($\beta_{in\_max} = 30°$, $\phi_\beta = 90°$), the effective angle of attack becomes excessive, risking severe trailing-edge separation.
  • Cases 5 and 6 ($\phi_\beta = 45°$) show larger effective angle-of-attack range than Cases 1 and 2, with correspondingly higher thrust and propulsion efficiency.
  • Cases 9 and 10 ($\phi_\beta = 0°$) have the largest effective angle-of-attack range but lower thrust and efficiency than Cases 3 and 4 due to excessive separation risk — demonstrating that $\phi_\beta = 90°$ with moderate $\beta_{in\_max}$ is preferable.
  • Adjusting $\theta_{geo}$ from $-4°$ to $-7°$ in Case 4 matches the pressure coefficient range of Case 6, confirming that larger $\phi_\beta$ can be compensated by geometric twist to achieve similar aerodynamic loading.

Comprehensive Performance Comparison (Table 2)

  • At $\theta_{geo} = -4°$: $C_l$ ranges from $0.536$ (Cases 3, 7) to $0.623$ (Case 4); $C_t$ ranges from $0.0274$ (Case 2) to $0.112$ (Case 7); $\eta_P$ ranges from $0.431$ (Case 1) to $0.632$ (Case 7).
  • Case 4 with $\theta_{geo} = -7°$ achieves $C_l = 0.575$, $C_t = 0.0691$, $\eta_P = 0.584$ — outperforming Case 6 in all metrics.

Results Interpretation

Physical Mechanisms

  • The asymmetric folding trajectory ($\beta_{mean} > 0$) reduces the outer wing's upward speed during the upstroke, minimizing negative lift generation — this mimics the strategy observed in real large birds.
  • Thrust generation is fundamentally linked to wing-tip flapping velocity: smaller $\phi_\beta$ and larger $\beta_{in\_max}$ both increase outer wing velocity, directly boosting the thrust coefficient.
  • The $\beta_{mean}$ parameter primarily affects lift through trajectory asymmetry but has minor impact on thrust — it controls the effective angle of attack distribution throughout the cycle rather than velocity magnitude.

Design Trade-offs

  • There is a trade-off between high thrust (requiring large wing-tip velocity) and avoiding excessive effective angle of attack that leads to flow separation and reduced propulsion efficiency.
  • The initial geometric twist angle $\theta_{geo}$ serves as a tuning parameter to match $\beta_{mean}$, keeping the airfoil's effective angle of attack within reasonable bounds during both upward and downward flapping.
  • Cases 7 and 8 achieve highest thrust and efficiency, but their large $\beta_{in\_max} = 30°$ combined with small $\phi_\beta = 45°$ produces excessive angle-of-attack excursions that the inviscid method may not fully capture.

Limitations

  • The inviscid panel method cannot simulate flow separation; pressure coefficient analysis is used as a proxy for identifying separation risk, but actual separated flow effects are not captured.
  • The fixed-wake model (no free-wake evolution) may underestimate wake-induced effects at high unsteadiness.
  • The structural model assumes torsion-only deformation with rigid bending, excluding spanwise bending-torsion coupling effects.
  • Only one wing planform (Canadian goose-inspired) is studied; generalizability to other wing geometries is not demonstrated.

Conclusion
  • Kinematic parameters significantly impact performance: the flapping phase angle $\phi_\beta$ controls outer wing velocity and thrust magnitude; larger $\phi_\beta$ implies smaller flapping velocity and lower thrust.
  • The mean folding angle $\beta_{mean}$ significantly influences $C_l$ by creating asymmetric flapping trajectories; a positive $\beta_{mean}$ reduces lift loss during the upstroke, increasing overall lift coefficient.
  • The inner wing flapping amplitude $\beta_{in\_max}$ primarily affects overall wing unsteadiness; higher $\beta_{in\_max}$ elevates thrust but may produce excessive angle-of-attack excursions on the outer wing.
  • Design guidelines for folding flapping wings: (1) Use positive $\beta_{mean}$ and large $\phi_\beta$ for lift optimization; (2) concentrate thrust generation in the downstroke via asymmetric configuration; (3) match $\theta_{geo}$ with $\beta_{mean}$ to keep effective angle of attack within reasonable range.
  • The proposed panel-beam FSI method is computationally efficient for parametric design studies, providing both force coefficients and surface pressure distributions unlike VLM-based approaches.
  • Future work could incorporate viscous corrections or transition models to better predict separation-dominated regimes, and extend to free-wake models for improved accuracy at high reduced frequencies.
