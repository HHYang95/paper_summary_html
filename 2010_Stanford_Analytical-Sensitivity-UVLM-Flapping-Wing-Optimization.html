Overview

This paper derives a complete analytical sensitivity analysis for a three-dimensional unsteady vortex-lattice method (UVLM) applied to flapping-wing optimization in forward flight. The framework enables efficient gradient-based optimization of propulsive efficiency under lift and thrust constraints, using active shape morphing parameterized through modal decomposition and cubic splines. Wake propagation terms are shown to be essential for inter-timestep sensitivity coupling. Optimization with up to 28 design variables achieves propulsive efficiencies of 0.440 (low reduced frequency) and 0.781 (high reduced frequency), representing substantial improvements over baseline configurations.

Introduction

Computational Challenge

  • Flapping-wing design optimization faces a tension between computational fidelity (unsteady low-Re vortical flows with nonlinear elastic surfaces) and the many function evaluations required for optimization convergence
  • Quasi-steady blade element methods are inexpensive but may miss relevant flow physics; Navier-Stokes solvers capture viscous effects but are typically too costly for optimization beyond a few design variables
  • The UVLM provides a moderate-fidelity compromise: three-dimensional unsteady flow computation at relatively low cost, suitable for largely attached flows in forward flight

Literature Gap

  • Steady VLM sensitivities existed (Chittick & Martins; Stanford & Ifju), and semi-analytical methods for unsteady panel flows had been explored (Murthy & Kaza)
  • Frequency-domain sensitivity approaches were available (Hall; Thomas et al.), but a fully analytical sensitivity analysis for a time-domain UVLM with free deforming wake was absent from the literature
  • This work fills this gap by deriving complete time-domain analytical sensitivities and demonstrating their utility for flapping-wing optimization

Related Works

UVLM for Flapping Flight

  • Vest and Katz (1996) demonstrated adequate UVLM correspondence with wind-tunnel data for combined flapping and twisting in forward flight
  • Fritz and Long (2004) developed an object-oriented UVLM for flapping flight with wake decay algorithms
  • Smith (1996) and Zhu (2007) coupled UVLM with elastic structural models, revealing dominance of inertial forces over aerodynamic forces in flapping wing dynamics
  • Willis et al. (2007) developed a computational framework for fluid-structure interaction in biologically inspired flapping flight

Sensitivity Analysis and Optimization

  • Berman and Wang (2007) formulated energy-minimizing kinematics for hovering using blade element methods; Tuncer and Kaya (2005) optimized flapping airfoils with Navier-Stokes solvers limited to few design variables
  • Rumpfkeil and Zingg (2D) and Mavriplis (3D) addressed gradient-based aerodynamic design for unsteady flows, including reverse integration and storage requirements
  • Kolonay et al. computed analytical sensitivities of an unsteady doublet-lattice method in the frequency domain; Li and Livne presented similar work with a doublet-point method

Materials and Methods

UVLM Formulation

  • The method enforces the no-penetration boundary condition on a paneled wing at each time step: $C_1^i \{\Gamma_{\text{wing}}^i\} + C_2^i \{\Gamma_{\text{wake}}^i\} = \{L^i\}$, where influence matrices are built via the Biot-Savart law
  • Wake is modeled with interconnected vortex rings shed from the trailing edge, with positions updated through induced velocity computations from wing-wake and wake-wake interactions
  • Rectangular planform wing (aspect ratio 6, NACA 83XX) with 6 chordwise and 10 spanwise panels per semispan; flapping amplitude of 45 degrees with 5-degree root pitch
  • Two reduced frequencies studied: $k = 0.1$ (quasi-steady) and $k = 0.75$ (strongly unsteady), each with 40 time steps per flapping period and 3 periods for convergence

Analytical Sensitivity Derivation

  • The objective function $G = \sum_{i=I_o}^{I_f} \omega^i \cdot g^i$ is differentiated with respect to wing shape at each time step using the direct method of analytical differentiation
  • Piecewise expressions arise for circulation sensitivity because the source vector depends on geometry at only the current and previous steps, while wake circulation at step $i$ is independent of geometry at step $i$
  • The direct method is preferred over adjoint because intermediate terms $\partial\{\Gamma_{\text{wing}}^i\}/\partial\{x^j\}$ are needed for wake sensitivity propagation through the chain of equations (22)-(26)

Morphing Parameterization and Optimization

  • Wing shape decomposed into 4 modes: first/second twisting ($\theta_1, \theta_2$) and first/second bending ($\beta_1, \beta_2$), with temporal variation via sinusoids (8 DVs) or periodic cubic splines with 7 control points (28 DVs)
  • Kreisselmeier-Steinhauser (KS) constraint function limits out-of-plane deformation to 70% of root chord at each time step; side constraints bound modal amplitudes
  • Optimization via MATLAB's fmincon (SQP with quasi-Newton Hessian approximation), maximizing propulsive efficiency $\eta$ subject to baseline lift and thrust constraints

Results

Validation and Sensitivity Verification

  • UVLM validated against Euler computations (Neef and Hummel, rectangular wing AR=8) showing acceptable agreement in lift and thrust trends; thrust slightly overpredicted due to missing viscous and separation drag
  • Further validated against experimental data (Heathcote et al., plunging wing at Re=30,000) for both rigid and dynamically bending configurations
  • Analytical sensitivities verified against finite differences with excellent agreement at both $k = 0.1$ and $k = 0.75$

Optimization Results

  • At $k = 0.1$: sinusoidal first-mode alone unable to improve efficiency; combined first-mode twist+bend with splines achieves $\eta = 0.281$ (from baseline 0.191); all four modes with splines reaches $\eta = 0.440$
  • At $k = 0.75$: sinusoidal first-mode twist alone raises efficiency to 0.500; first-mode splines achieve $\eta = 0.627$; all four modes with splines reaches $\eta = 0.781$
  • Increasing design variables consistently improves efficiency with little increase in computational cost; lift constraint remains active in all cases

Results Interpretation

Reduced Frequency Effects on Optimal Strategy

  • At low $k$, optimal efficiency is thrust-driven: bending deformation plays the dominant role by counteracting flapping motion during mid-downstroke, redistributing forces to increase time-averaged thrust while power remains largely unaffected
  • At high $k$, optimal efficiency is power-driven: out-of-phase twisting (feathering) near 45-degree amplitude reduces effective angle of attack, dramatically lowering input power while thrust shows minor improvements
  • Second-mode shapes concentrate deformations inboard, providing finer control over the wing shape and enabling larger first-mode amplitudes within deformation constraints

Wake Influence on Sensitivity Structure

  • At $k = 0.1$, force sensitivity to wing shape decays rapidly backward in time, allowing truncation of the reverse summation with negligible accuracy loss
  • At $k = 0.75$, sensitivities are noisy in time with significant magnitudes at half-cycle lags, reflecting strong wake-wing interactions that demand the full time history for accurate gradient computation
  • These findings confirm that wake terms are the sole mechanism coupling forces across time steps and are indispensable for high-reduced-frequency optimization

Conclusion

Principal Achievements

  • First fully analytical sensitivity analysis for a time-domain UVLM with free deforming wake, enabling efficient gradient-based flapping-wing optimization
  • Demonstrated that increasing design freedom (8 to 28 variables) yields progressively superior morphing designs with minimal computational cost increase
  • Established distinct optimal morphing strategies: bending-dominant at low reduced frequencies (thrust improvement), twist-dominant at high reduced frequencies (power reduction)

Limitations and Future Directions

  • Inviscid model cannot capture flow separation, transition, or leading-edge vortices; limited to forward flight with largely attached flows
  • Morphing actuation power not included in efficiency calculation; no structural feasibility constraint for achieving the prescribed shapes
  • Future work: higher-fidelity validation, jig-shape aerostructural optimization (designing wing structure for passive deformation matching optimal active shapes), coupling with nonlinear structural models, and extension to hovering with discrete vortex particle wakes
